## Abstract 

We analyze the spectrum of collective modes in a superconductor, in which pairing is mediated by long-range nematic fluctuations. Previous experimental and theoretical studies have found that a superconducting gap in such a system is highly anisotropic and at any finite T < T c vanishes on four arcs of the Fermi surface even when the pairing symmetry is s−wave ( s+− between hole and electron pockets). We derive the expression for the pair susceptibility χ(q, Ω) at finite momentum q

and frequency Ω deep in the superconducting phase. We analyze the spectral function, Im χ(q, Ω), and its pole structure in transverse (phase) and longitudinal (amplitude) channels and compare the results with those of a conventional s-wave superconductor. We find that the analytic structure of the pair susceptibility in both channels is qualitatively distinct from that in a BCS superconductor. This gives rise to highly unconventional dispersion of phase and amplitude collective modes.

> I. Introduction

The interplay between superconductivity and electronic nematicity has emerged as one of the central themes in the study of a number of correlated quantum materials. Ne-matic order—characterized by spontaneous breaking of point-group rotational symme-try [1]—appears ubiquitously across several families of unconventional superconductors, in-cluding the iron-based superconductors [2–8], cuprates [9–14] and twisted bilayer graphene [15]. Among these, iron-based chalcogenide material FeSe and its isovalently doped coun-terparts, FeSe 1−xSx and FeSe 1−xTe x, have attracted significant interest as in distinction to other Fe-pnictide materials, they display nematicity and unconventional superconductivity without long-range magnetic order. A pure FeSe possesses long-range nematic order below 

Tp ∼ 85 K and superconductivity below Tc ∼ 8K. Upon doping by S or Te, Tp decreases and vanishes at some critical xc (0.17 for S doping and 0.53 for Te doping). Moreover, numerous experimental data [16–29] pointed out that superconductivity at x ≥ xc is qualitatively different from that in pure and weakly doped FeSe ( x < x c). The evidence includes, e.g., change of gap structure between x < x c and x ≥ xc [21–26], variation of the magnetic field dependence of Tc [20], temperature variation of the specific heat at T < T c [18, 27, 28], large residual density of states [26, 27] and small superfluid density [29] at x ≥ xc. These data fueled speculations that the pairing glue at x < x c and at x ≥ xc may be different. In pure/weakly doped FeSe, antiferromagnetic order does not exist at ambient pressure but emerges under a small increase of pressure. In this situation, the prevailing belief is that the pairing glue is still provided by spin fluctuations, like in other Fe-based superconductors. However, at x ≥ xc long -range magnetism does not develop even at strong pressure, and 3spin fluctuations are likely too weak to be the pairing glue. It has been pointed out, both microscopically and phenomenologically [30–35] that ex-perimental results for doped FeSe at x ≥ xc can be understood assuming that the pairing is mediated by nematic rather than spin fluctuations. In the vicinity of a generic nematic crit-ical point, nematic fluctuations are strong and peaked at momentum transfer kF − pF ≈ 0, where kF and pF are Fermi surface momenta. This gives rise to near-equal attraction in dif-ferent pairing channels: s−wave, p−wave, d−wave (Refs. [32, 36]. The peculiarity of doped FeSe is that in this system hole and electron Fermi surfaces are made of different d−wave Fe-orbitals. As the consequence, the pairing interaction mediated nematic fluctuations also contains strong dependence on ( kF + pF )/2 ≈ kF . The latter gives rise to strong angular variation of the gap function along the Fermi surface even if the pairing symmetry is s−wave (ref. [30]). The anisotropy is most prominent on the hole pocket, which is made from dxz 

and dyz orbitals. In a recent publication [37] the two us explored the consequences of strong angular variation of the superconducting gap on various thermodynamic, spectroscopic and transport observables, e.g., specific heat, Raman response, and optical conductivity. In the present work, we extend our analysis to another direction- the structure of collective excitations in a nematic-fluctuation-mediated superconductor (NFMS). Collective excitations in conventional superconductors are well understood; s−wave, 

p−wave and d−wave gap symmetries are the best-studied cases [38–66]. They describe fluc-tuations of a complex superconducting order parameter ∆( r, t ) = |∆|(r, t )eiϕ (r,t ). Transverse (phase) and longitudinal (amplitude) modes describe fluctuations of ϕ(r, t ) and |∆|(r, t ), re-spectively. For a charge neutral s-wave superconductor with a constant gap ∆, the phase mode is a gapless Goldstone mode [38, 41–43, 46] with dispersion relation Ω( q) = vF |q|/√2(in 2 d) where vF is the Fermi velocity. It is often called Anderson-Bogolubov (AB) mode [39, 40]. The longitudinal mode, often called Schmid-Higgs (SH) mode [44, 45, 67–71], is gapped. It exists at frequencies above 2∆ and is a resonance in the continuum rather than a sharp mode. For a d-wave superconductors with the nodal gap structure the phase mode is not much different from the s-wave case, but the SH mode is significantly modified due to scattering off gapless quasi-particles near the nodal points [56, 58, 62, 66]. In this communication, we analyze collective excitations in a 2D NFMS. We compute lon-gitudinal χT (q, Ω) and transverse χL(q, Ω) particle-particle susceptibilities and extract from them the dispersion of the longitudinal and transverse collective modes. For the convenience 4of a reader, we list here the key findings of our analysis. 

• In the transverse channel, we find, neglecting Coulomb interaction, that there are no sharp modes, but still, Im χT (q, Ω) has two maxima at Ω 1 = vF |q|| cos θq| and Ω2 = vF |q|| sin θq|, where θq specifies the direction of q with respect to ˆ x. The maxima merge at θ = π/ 4(1 + 2 n), but even in this case the transverse mode remains damped. We expect that in the presence of the Coulomb interaction, the dispersion of the two maxima will change from Ω ∝ q ro Ω ∝ √q, but the transverse modes remain damped.

The pairing interaction mediated by nematic fluctuations is attractive and generally has the form V (θk, θ p) = −f 2(θk, θ p)χnem (θk − θp), where χmem is the nematic susceptibility, and f (θk, θ p) is the nematic form factor. The form of f (θk, θ p) depends on the microscopic model and the origin of the nematic order. In many Fe-based superconductors, nematic transition occurs only a few Kelvin above a magnetic transition into a stripe-magnetic phase with momenta ( π, 0) or (0 , π ) , and the two transition lines follow each other as functions of doping. In these systems nematicity is believed to be a vestigial state in which the symmetry between ( π, 0) and (0 , π ) is broken, but a magnetic long-range order is not yet set. For this case, f (θk, θ p) does not have strong angle-dependence and can be reasonably well approximated by a constant. As we said above, in doped FeSe magnetic and nematic transitions are well separated. We follow earlier works [30–33, 36, 72] and assume that nematicity in FeSe is a spontaneous Pomeranchuk order. In the microscopic consideration, this order emerges because of multiple d−wave orbitals, most notably dxz and dyz , and the primary nematic order parameter is the difference between the occupation of these two orbitals. In this situation, the form factor is the combination of the coherence factors 6from the transformation from orbital to band basis. Microscopic calculation in [30, 31] found f (θk, θ p) = A cos( θk + θp). Near the onset of a nematic order in FeSe 1−xSx(Te x)(coming from larger x), χnem (θk − θp) is strongly peaked at θk = θp and for the purpose of superconductivity, can be approximated by δ(θk − θp). Then f (θk, θ p) = A cos 2 θk and the pairing interaction can be expressed as 

V (k, p) ≡ V (θk, θ p) = −V0 cos 2 2θkδ(θk − θp) (3) where V0 > 0. A similar form of the pairing interaction has been proposed for one-band models on phenomenological grounds [32, 33, 36, 72]. This interaction is local ( θk = θp)and for this reason is the same in all pairing channels that form irreducible representations of the D4h group for the square lattice (one- dimensional A1g,B1g,B2g and two-dimensional

V. Conclusions 

In this work, we investigate the collective mode dynamics in a superconductor in which the pairing is driven by soft charge-nematic fluctuations. Such a pairing scenario is espe-cially relevant to FeSe 1−xS(Te) x systems, which host a nematic quantum critical point near 

xc ∼ 0.17 (for S-doping) and xc ∼ 0.5 (for Te-doping). Near xx, strong nematic fluctuations, 22 peaked at zero momentum, generate a highly anisotropic attractive pairing interaction be-tween electrons in the form V (k, −k; p, −p) ∝ − cos 2 2θk δ(θk − θp), where θk and θp are the angular position of the incoming/outgoing fermions on the Fermi surface. The factor cos 2 2θk is the effect of coherent factors of the transformation from orbital to band basis (quantum geometry, in modern language). This attraction gives rise to superconductivity with a highly anisotropic gap function ∆( θk), even in the s−wave channel, which we con-sider here: ∆( θk) = ∆ 0 exp( − tan 2 2θk/g ). The superconducting gap is maximized at the hot spots, θk = nπ/ 2, n = 0 , 1, 2, 3,and is significantly suppressed in the cold regions around 

θk = (2 n + 1) π/ 4. We analyze transverse and longitudinal collective excitations of such a superconductor. For this, we compute the pair-pair susceptibility χ(q, Ω) and decompose it into transverse ( χT ) and longitudinal ( χL) components. We find that the structure of the pair-pair susceptibility in our case is qualitatively different from that in conventional 

s−wave BCS superconductor, reminiscent of the difference between series and parallel resis-tor networks. In the transverse channel, we found, neglecting the long-range Coulomb interaction,that Im χT (q, Ω) is non-zero in a finite range 0 < Ω < v F |q|. Within this range, it has two maxima at Ω = vF |q| cos θq and Ω = vF |q| sin θq, where θq defines the direction of the momentum q

relative to ˆ x towards a hot spot. These peaks correspond to anisotropic sound-like damped collective modes, whose velocities scale linearly with θq. We expect that in the presence of Coulomb interaction, two damped modes remain, but with dispersion Ω ∝ √q.In the longitudinal channel, we found qualitatively different behavior at q = 0 and at a finite q. At zero momentum, Im χL(0 , Ω) is nonzero for all Ω. It vanishes at Ω = 0, but very rapidly increases at a non-zero Ω, as 1 /(log(Ω /∆0)1/2. We related this behavior to the unique nodal structure of our ∆( θk). Near Ω = 2∆ 0, we found that Im χL(0 , Ω) diverges as log( |Ω − 2∆ 0|). This gives rise to a discontinuity in the real part of χL at the same frequency. At a finite q, we found that at small Ω ≪ ∆0, Im χL(q, Ω) has one or two discontinuities, depending on the value of θq. The real part of χL(q, Ω) diverges logarithmically at these frequencies.
