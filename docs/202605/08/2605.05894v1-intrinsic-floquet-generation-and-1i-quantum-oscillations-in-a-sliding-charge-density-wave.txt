## Intrinsic Floquet Generation and 1/I Quantum Oscillations in a Sliding Charge-Density Wave 

Yi Zhou 1, ∗

> 1

Institute of Physics, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing 100190, China 

(Dated: May 8, 2026) The realization of intrinsic, tunable high-frequency quantum states without external radiation is a major goal in condensed matter physics and quantum device engineering. Here, we demonstrate that a uniformly sliding charge-density wave (CDW) acts as an intrinsic dc-to-ac converter, transforming spatial periodicity into tempo-ral periodicity to realize a unique periodically driven quantum state. We show that the isolated sliding-CDW problem is exactly solvable in Floquet form, yielding split gap edges and a ladder of Floquet sidebands. Using this exact solution, we reveal that weak-probe tunneling spectroscopy naturally yields an inverse-current (1 /I)oscillation as a fixed-bias cut of the sideband ladder. Matching the observed oscillation period to theory indi-cates that the macroscopic current must percolate through a highly localized coherent filament, with an e ff ective channel number orders of magnitude smaller than the geometric chain count. Furthermore, using a segmented multiterminal model, we demonstrate that inelastic phase-slip dephasing near the contacts explains the strong suppression of oscillation visibility on outer voltage probes. Ultimately, our results provide a rigorous transport interpretation of the striking 1 /I quantum oscillations recently observed in quasi-one-dimensional CDW insu-lators. More broadly, they highlight a universal spatial-to-temporal conversion mechanism where the insulating gap protects Floquet coherence, o ff ering a novel paradigm for intrinsically driven quantum devices. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Macroscopic quantum states, such as superconductivity and charge density waves (CDWs), o ff er unique platforms for ex-ploring emergent quantum phenomena and developing next-generation quantum materials [1–4]. A CDW is a sponta-neously formed, spatially periodic modulation of the elec-tronic charge density and lattice lattice coordinates that nat-urally arises in quasi-one-dimensional (quasi-1D) conduc-tors [1, 2], with related considerations applying to spin density waves [5–7]. Above a critical depinning threshold, the CDW condensate can slide collectively, leading to highly nonlinear charge transport [4, 8–13]. Once such an ordered state slides uniformly, its spatial phase becomes explicitly time-dependent. Consequently, the condensate itself generates a periodic drive. This universal spatial-to-temporal conversion implies that a sliding density wave acts as an intrinsic dc-to-ac converter: the temporal pe-riodicity is not imposed by external radiation, but is instead created internally by the collective motion of the ordered state. This viewpoint represents a fundamental departure from the more familiar Floquet settings, which typically require com-plex architectures to apply external microwave fields or ultra-fast laser pulses [14–19]. This perspective is especially timely given that quantum oscillations are traditionally associated with Landau quan-tization and exhibit periodicity in inverse magnetic field (1 /B) [20–22]. Therefore, the recent experimental observa-tion of robust quantum oscillations periodic in inverse ap-plied current (1 /I) in a quasi-1D CDW insulator is highly striking [23]. Crucially, this coherent quantum phenomenon relies strictly on the CDW being a fully gapped insulator. In many conventional two-dimensional CDWs, a residual Fermi surface persists well below the transition temperature.

Strikingly, based on typical device dimensions [23], the full geometric chain count is roughly Ngeom ∼ 3 × 10 4. The fact that Neff ≪ Ngeom is a profound physical signature: it dic-tates that the sliding state does not depin as a monolithic bulk. Instead, as sketched in Fig. 5, the macroscopic terminal-to-terminal current I must heavily percolate through a narrow, highly coherent active filament. Crucially, this filamentary confinement is precisely what makes the observation of these quantum oscillations experimentally feasible. Achieving the required high sliding frequency Ω uniformly across the entire bulk Ngeom would require a total applied current nearly two or-ders of magnitude larger, and the resulting macroscopic Joule heating would thermally destroy the delicate Floquet phase coherence. At fixed ϵ = eV − Ev, successive sideband edges cross the probe chemical potential whenever ϵ ≃ (m + 12 )ℏΩ(Im), so 1/Im is linear in m. This mechanism provides a striking tem-poral analog to the Shubnikov–de Haas e ff ect. In conventional magnetic quantum oscillations, a periodicity in 1 /B arises be-cause Landau levels with energy spacing ℏωc ∝ B succes-sively cross a fixed Fermi energy. Here, the periodicity in 1 /I

arises because Floquet sidebands with energy spacing ℏΩ ∝ I

successively cross a fixed contact mismatch energy ϵ. Figure 6 shows the resulting fixed-bias inverse-current oscillation. The same near-edge construction also yields the fixed-current cuts of G(V, I) near the singular edge structure shown in Fig. 7. The single-contact spectra and the inverse-current oscillation are therefore complementary cuts of the same Flo-quet sideband ladder. If one inserted the full geometric chain count of the nanowire, ℏΩ would fall far below the thermal and Dynes widths, so the observed spectroscopy must be con-trolled by a much smaller contact-local e ff ective channel num-ber. > V. DISCUSSION AND OUTLOOK

The exact sliding-CDW solution generates one Floquet sideband ladder, but di ff erent measurements project that lad-der in di ff erent ways. A weak single contact reads it as local spectroscopy; once the depinned state is entered and the local off set varies only weakly with current, the same ladder yields a fixed-bias 1 /I sequence through repeated sideband cross-ings. A persistent-current-driven multiterminal device reads it through terminal-dependent voltage drops, whereas a homo-geneous voltage-biased span reads it as threshold shifts in the (V, Ω) plane. The experiment of Ref. [23] belongs to the for-mer category, so spatial inhomogeneity and contact dephasing are essential for understanding why the inner-terminal signal exhibits a much stronger 1 /I oscillation than the raw outer-terminal voltage. A central physical deduction of our framework is the true nature of the sliding state itself. The parameter Neff repre-sents the e ff ective coherent channel number of a percolating filament, rather than a literal geometric chain count of the whole nanowire. For typical device dimensions [23], the ge-ometric chain count Ngeom ∼ 3 × 10 4 is nearly two orders of magnitude larger than the Neff ∼ 480 required to reproduce the experimental oscillation period at a reasonable bias o ff set. Physically, this strong reduction Neff ≪ Ngeom dictates that the macroscopic quantum coherence probed by the contacts is highly localized. This likely arises from surface-dominated tunneling (where the metallic probe primarily couples to the outermost CDW chains), filamentary depinning of the CDW in transverse domains, or a finite transverse phase-correlation length that limits the number of chains participating coher-ently in the local Floquet spectroscopy. Furthermore, this ef-fective spatial confinement acts as a vital protective mecha-nism. By reaching high sliding frequencies with a relatively small total applied current, the active region avoids the severe macroscopic Joule heating that would otherwise induce rapid thermal dephasing. Naturally, several important ingredients remain outside the present treatment. Our segmented current-driven model is in-tended as a minimal transport interpretation of the visibility hierarchy along such a filament, not as a quantitative fit to the oscillation amplitude or to the full sawtooth waveform. A future material-specific microscopic theory would have to de-termine self-consistently how the imposed persistent current sets the local voltage drops, how phase-slip conversion near the contacts [30] feeds back on the sliding frequency, and how additional non-CDW channels modify the measured terminal voltages. Within these limitations, the present sequence of exact Flo-quet solution, weak-probe spectroscopy, segmented current-driven modeling, and homogeneous transport reference pro-vides a robust transport framework for the observed inverse-current oscillations. More broadly, the universal spatial-to-temporal conversion demonstrated here establishes a critical material prerequisite for observing macroscopic sliding co-herence. We emphasize that these 1 /I quantum oscillations are expected to emerge only in fully gapped CDW insulators.
