The Emery model is the quintessential model for cuprate superconductors. In his eponymous paper, Emery only considered the next-nearest-neighbor oxygen-copper hopping. Later, also the relevance of nearest- and next-nearest oxygen-oxygen hoppings has been pointed out. Using dy-namical vertex approximation, we find a superconducting dome consistent with cuprates. However, long-range hoppings beyond the three conventional hopping parameters are necessary for the quan-titatively correct phase diagram and for a proper d-wave order parameter. 

Introduction. Cuprates are charge-transfer insula-tors according to the scheme of Zaanen, Sawatzky and Allen [1]. That is, doped holes primarily go into the oxygen orbitals, as experimentally confirmed [2–4]. This makes the Emery model [5] with the copper dx2−y2 and two in-plane oxygen orbitals (see inset of Fig. 1) the quintessential model for superconducting cuprates [6]. Despite this multi-orbital nature, oxygen and copper orbitals lead to a single Fermi surface [7] of mixed copper-oxygen character. This motivates the use of the one-band Hubbard model instead of the more involved three-band Emery model. Indeed, most theoretical studies of cuprate superconductivity are based on this Hubbard model [8– 10]. On a qualitative level, such a simplified calculation with a Hubbard model is reasonable; however, quantita-tively reliable calculations with a single set of parame-ters across dopings require the more fundamental Emery model [11]. The Emery model has been studied with various meth-ods, including – most closely to our study – dynami-cal mean-field theory [11–16], its cluster [17–24] and di-agrammatic extensions [25], as well as the two-particle self-consistent (TPSC) approach [26]. Cluster extensions of DMFT [17–24, 27] and direct lattice quantum Monte Carlo simulations [28–30] are, in principle, numerically exact, when extrapolated to infinite cluster size. However, the sign problem ren-ders this extrapolation infeasible. Typically, only 2 ×2clusters have been used for low temperature simula-tions of the Emery model around Tc [19, 20], and are known to strongly overestimate superconducting tenden-cies. Larger clusters such as 6 ×6 [20], 8 ×8 [30] and 16 ×4[28, 29] have been studied – but only at elevated tem-peratures. Even these clusters are definitely too small to properly resolve Fermi surfaces, which is crucial for an accurate description of superconductivity that gaps them. From these considerations, we can conclude that rel-evant hopping elements are neglected when we restrict ourselves to the conv. Emery model. While this con-ventional approach is appealing, and thus has been used extensively hitherto in the literature, it is not sufficient. In days of modern computing, it is also no problem to in-clude all hopping elements of the full d-p-model, which we make accessible in our data repository [link to be added upon publication]. 

Superconducting instabilities in cuprate d-p models. 

We now come to the second main finding of our paper: the superconducting dome and its dependence on these longer-range hopping parameters. Specifically, we compare the full d-p model (2D) to the conv. Emery model (both for CaCuO 2), as well as to the so-called covalent Emery model. The latter is also a conv. Emery model. It was derived in [17] for Bi 2Sr 2CaCu 2O8

(Bi-2212) with the hopping parameters in Table I, and is often employed in the relevant literature [19]. For all of these models, we perform DΓA calculations in the range of relevant hole dopings δ = 5 − n (n: total filling) for cuprate superconductivity. Figure 3 shows the superconducting eigenvalue 4

λSC (T ), with λSC (T ) = 1 indicating the superconduct-ing instability; additional dopings are shown in the Sup-plemental Material [52]. The conv. Emery model (empty circles) shows no sign yet of an increase of λSC (T ) down to the lowest accessible temperatures and for a doping of 15% and 20%, i.e., the typical range of optimal doping in cuprates. This changes dramatically if we include the hopping elements beyond the conv. Emery model (two uppermost solid lines), where we obtain superconductiv-ity at δ = 0 .15. At δ = 0 .2, we also extrapolate to a small Tc, using the fit formula λSC (T ) = a + b log T as in previous calculations [35, 56–58]. Although for this latter doping, also Tc = 0 is possible within the extrap-olation error. In any case, we clearly see how important the longer range hopping beyond the conv. Emery model is. Also shown in Fig. 3 is λSC (T ) for the covalent Emery model for Bi-2212. Here, δ = 0 .2 behaves similar as for the conv. Emery model (for CaCuO 2). For both, d-wave superconductivity is the leading eigenvector, but there is no indication at all of a superconducting transition. For the covalent Emery model at δ = 0 .15 we see a rather sudden and sharp increase of the d-wave eigen-value at low temperatures. The associated d-wave eigen-function, shown in the inset of Fig. 3, shows a somewhat unusual modulation with a suppression at the antinodes. This can be explained as follows: without longer range hoppings, the van Hove singularity is too close to the Fermi energy ( t′ correspondingly small in Table I) so that a pseudogap already starts to develop as discussed previously in [25]. This pseudogap suppresses the spec-tral weight at the antinode which, thus, contributes little to superconductivity. In the inset of Fig. 3 we see that the superconducting pairing adjusts to this suppressed weight at the antinode by developing a modulated d-wave order parameter with a suppressed superconducting eigenvector at the antinode where the spectral function is suppressed [59]. In Fig. 4 we present the superconducting dome of the full d-p model (2D) for CaCuO 2. We observe the typical dome of cuprate superconductors with superconductivity in the range of about 7-22% hole doping. Let us note, however, that experimentally bulk CaCuO 2 has hith-erto not been found to be superconducting [60]. But, superconducting heterostructures could be synthesized [61, 62]. In films, CaCuO 2 has a maximal Tc of about 40 K [61, 63]. Let us emphasize that our calculations are for bulk CaCuO 2, and differences between films and bulk which are hitherto poorly understood might be of importance. In contrast, the conv. Emery model (blue curve in Fig. 4) has a very narrow superconducting dome between 9% and 14% hole doping only. Fig. 4 also shows the superconducting dome for the co-valent Emery model, which is the conv. Emery model for Bi2212. The main difference to the conventional Emery model for CaCuO 2 is the larger charge transfer energy 

εd − εp, see Table I. In Fig. 3, we have seen that an atyp-ical, i.e., modulated, d-wave order parameter is found for this model (but not in experiment [64]) because the van Hove singularity is too close to the Fermi level (which leads to a large pseudogap, eventually suppressing plain 

d-wave superconductivity). The same modulation is also found for the conventional Emery model (blue line in Fig. 4). That is, without longer range hoppings, the van Hove singularity is too close to the Fermi level. This again leads to a modulated d-wave order parameter and a rather narrow superconducting dome shifted to smaller hole-dopings. In particular, there is no superconductiv-ity at larger dopings above 17% for the covalent Emery model for Bi2212, whereas in experiment superconduc-tivity for Bi2212 extends up to 30% hole doping [65]. 

Conclusions. Superconductivity in the conventional Emery model has hitherto only been studied for small clusters that are known to largely overestimate Tc—at least for the Hubbard model. We here calculate super-conductivity of the full d-p model using DΓA, and find a phase diagram in good agreement with cuprates. For an accurate quantitative description, hopping matrix el-ements beyond the tpd , tpp and t′ 

> pp

of the conventional Emery model are absolutely necessary. Otherwise, the superconducting dome is found at too small hole doping and there is also a strong modulation of the d-wave order parameter. Our work opens the path to a more material realistic description of cuprate superconductivity which certainly necessitates the inclusion of oxygen orbitals.

However, the strong hybridizations between d and p states, as well as the large spread of the orbital in the 1 band model, suggest that an elaborated and material realistic model for cuprates needs more than a single localized Cu dx2−y2 orbital. In the main text we perform superconducting calculations using models based on DFT for effectively two-dimensional systems, with momentum grids of the form nkx × nky × 1. When transforming the real-space Wannier Hamiltonian to momentum space, matrix elements with an out-of-plane component may contribute. We therefore distinguish between two DFT-based models for CaCuO 2: the full d-p model (3D) and the full d-p model (2D). The former is obtained by directly transforming the full Wannier Hamiltonian, whereas for the latter all matrix elements with an out-of-plane component are set to zero prior to the transformation. Except for Table II of the main text, we use the full d-p model (2D) and thus omit this distinction. For the conventional Emery model, no such distinction arises by construction, since it contains no out-of-plane hoppings.    

> II. SUPERCONDUCTING CALCULATIONS FOR d-pMODELS

The details of the ladder DΓA extension to treat d-p models are described in the Supplemental Information of [1] up to the point where Σ ladder DΓA ,dd (k, iν ) is calculated. To determine the superconducting Tc, we have to go beyond this step and analyze the pairing instability. The pairing susceptibility has an orbital structure that originates only from the external non-interacting legs. The reason is that the irreducible particle-particle vertex Γ pp ,ijkm , where i, j, k, m 

denote orbital indices, has only one non-vanishing contribution, Γ pp ≡ Γpp ,dddd . The reason for this is that all Feynman diagrams for Γ must start at an interaction U , which is restricted to the only interacting Cu 3 dx2−y2 orbital. Since the superconducting instability cannot originate from the external Green function ”legs”, we can analyze the linearized Eliashberg equation in a one-orbital form
