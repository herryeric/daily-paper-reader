Abstract 

Clarifying the magnetic ground state is essential for analysing unconventional superconductivity, because microscopic magnetic order provides one of the basic starting assumptions for spin-fluctuation pairing theories. FeSe exhibits pronounced stripe- and Néel-type spin fluctuations yet lacks static long-range order, posing a long-standing puzzle. By combining PBE and 𝑟 2SCAN mixed exchange-correlation calculations with spectrally weighted simulations of the static spin structure factor 

𝑆 (𝑞 ), we show that FeSe is not governed by a single magnetic configuration but by a nearly degenerate manifold of phase-slip defects embedded in a stripe background. We term this state a residual-order spin state (ROSS): a spin state that retains local stripe-like antiferromagnetic correlations but loses long-range phase coherence because of phase slips. Multiple slip configurations are compressed into an exceptionally narrow energy window. Non-local magneto-elastic coupling redistributes domain-wall formation energy through the lattice, whereas competing magnetic interactions truncate the real-space coherence length to an optimal scale of about ten moments. Weighted superpositions of these slip states quantitatively reproduce the momentum-space lineshapes of both stripe- and Néel-type spin fluctuations observed by inelastic neutron scattering, providing a microscopic basis for superconductivity models built on spin fluctuations. 

Keywords: FeSe; magnetic disorder; inelastic neutron scattering; Néel fluctuation; superconductivity 

# Main 

The microscopic mechanism of unconventional supercon-ductivity has long remained a central puzzle in condensed-matter physics, and the key to resolving it lies in identifying the physical origin of the electron-pairing interaction kernel [1, 2]. In the widely accepted picture of spin-fluctuation-mediated pairing, the magnetic ground state of a system and the fine structure of its excitation spectrum in reciprocal space directly construct the effective pairing potential [3– 5], thereby governing the symmetry and nodal structure of the superconducting gap [6, 7]. This implies that an accu-rate reconstruction of the microscopic magnetic background of a material is not only essential for the completeness of any theoretical description, but is also crucial for deter-mining whether calculations of the pairing mechanism are physically reliable [1, 8]. However, most unconventional superconductors are complicated by chemical doping and disorder effects, such that their intrinsic magnetic structures are often obscured by phase separation or doping inhomo-geneity [9, 10]. In contrast, FeSe, as a chemically simple superconducting system that does not require heavy dop-ing, avoids the complications of chemical disorder and thus provides a particularly valuable opportunity to disentangle magnetism and superconductivity in the clean limit. Among iron-based superconductors, the magnetic ground state of FeSe remains highly controversial because of its strongly unconventional character [8, 11–19]. On the one hand, 𝜇 SR and NMR experiments consistently show the ab-sence of static long-range order, pointing to a magnetically disordered ground state [14, 20–22]. On the other hand, inelastic neutron scattering (INS) has detected strong spin excitations at the stripe wave vector [12, 13], while recent high-resolution measurements further revealed substantial spectral weight in the Néel channel [8, 11–13]. The coex-istence of these multiple magnetic correlations challenges existing theoretical frameworks and has been regarded as a key issue in understanding the unconventional pairing mech-anism in FeSe. 1

At present, major theoretical difficulties remain. For example, a large body of density functional theory calcu-lations tends to predict a static magnetic ground state for FeSe with an overwhelming energetic advantage [15, 23– 27], in fundamental conflict with the strongly frustrated and nearly degenerate magnetic background indicated by experiments [11, 14, 15]. Furthermore, pairing scenarios based solely on stripe fluctuations are often insufficient to account for the fine anisotropy of the experimentally ob-served superconducting gap [28–30], and scattering degrees of freedom from the Néel channel must be introduced to complete the angular harmonic components of the pairing kernel [11, 31, 32]. However, the microscopic real-space origin of such Néel excitations—whether they arise from an independent Néel order or from some form of hidden magnetic order—remains unresolved. This unresolved di-chotomy suggests that the magnetic ground state of FeSe is neither a conventional long-range ordered phase nor a fully random disordered state, but rather a state that retains resid-ual local magnetic correlations while lacking long-range phase coherence. In this work, we refer to such a state as a residual-order spin state (ROSS). In this work, by using the XC combination functional in-terface in VASP to specify the exchange-correlation compo-nents [33–41], we successfully construct a strongly compet-ing and frustrated magnetic manifold involving stripe order, double-checkerboard order, and triple-checkerboard order. Based on this frustrated magnetic manifold, we propose a residual-order spin state (ROSS), in which 𝜋 phase slips are embedded in a stripe-antiferromagnetic background, as schematically summarized in Fig. 1. Such slip configu-rations are not sparse high-energy excitations; rather, they form low-energy domain walls associated with magnetic phase breaking in the vicinity of SAFM order. The low-energy magnetic order of the system should therefore be viewed as arising from strong competition and quantum dynamical mixing among multiple nearly degenerate slip configurations, while the relevant excitations should be re-garded as collective excited states [12, 42]. Within the ROSS picture, the system retains residual short-range stripe-like order, while long-range magnetic coherence is destroyed by the proliferation and dynamical mixing of 𝜋 phase-slip de-fects. This naturally provides a microscopic route to the strongly competing quasi-degenerate magnetic-state man-ifold and spin correlations in FeSe. This picture is also consistent with the overall experimental phenomenology of FeSe, namely the coexistence of strong spin responses and the absence of static long-range magnetic order [14, 20– 22]. # First-principles calculations 

In VASP calculations, a wide variety of exchange-correlation treatments are available. In DFT calcula-tions, the commonly used PBE functional belongs to the GGA class [38]. Because it cannot fully cancel the self-interaction error of electrons, it tends to over-delocalize the electronic density in real space, leading to delocal-ization errors. In the more refined meta-GGA functional 

𝑟 2SCAN, the kinetic-energy density 𝜏 is introduced to iden-tify single-orbital regions, a mechanism that effectively sup-presses self-interaction errors and thus significantly im-proves the description of electron localization; however, the SCAN/ 𝑟 2SCAN family of functionals is also prone to over-stabilizing local magnetic moments [37, 39]. There-fore, for a system such as FeSe, which simultaneously ex-hibits itinerant and localized character, and considering that the computational cost of applying hybrid function-als to large supercells makes structural optimization im-practical, we adopt a compromise strategy: by using the mixed exchange-correlation parameter Xc_c in VASP, we adjust the mixing ratio between PBE and 𝑟 2SCAN so as to balance the deficiencies of the two schemes. Although this mixed-functional approach substantially increases the computational cost, the overall computational time remains manageable for structural optimization. According to ex-perimental constraints, the mixing ratio of Xc_c should place the system in a strongly magnetically frustrated regime [11, 14, 15], with stripe order retaining a slight energetic advantage [26, 27]. Moreover, the magnetic configurations obtained in this strongly frustrated regime should be able to reproduce the excitation phenomenology of FeSe observed in INS experiments as displayed in Fig. 1. Here, 𝑎 \𝑏 denote the lengths of the ferromagnetic-direction stripe segments at the left and right sides of a slip wall, and 𝑁 is defined as the sum of the stripe lengths at the two ends. For the sources of the extracted data and the complete raw dataset, see the Supplementary Materials [Table S1,S2]. In actual calculations, with the representative mag-netic configurations shown in Fig. 1, under the PBE+D3 exchange-correlation treatment, the TRI state (3\3) and PCB (2\2) are close in energy and behave as nearly degener-ate ground states, whereas under 𝑟 2SCAN+rvv10, SAFM emerges as the absolute ground state. Therefore, these three states can in principle be tuned into a strongly frustrated regime while keeping SAFM as the ground state. In the scan of the Xc_c mixing ratio, we chose 45% PBE and 55% 

𝑟 2SCAN, for which the resulting energetics satisfy the re-quired condition. At the same time, the energy differences among TRI, PCB, and SAFM exhibit a clear nonlinearity Fig. 2(a), indicating that the Xc_c mixing is not a simple en-ergetic interpolation, but instead reconstructs the electronic structure of the system. For comparison, we further per-formed DFT+ 𝑈 calculations and found that the energetic ranking of the competing magnetic states can also be al-tered, further supporting that FeSe is located near a highly frustrated and nearly degenerate magnetic regime[fig.S1]. Among these three states, TRI is particularly special, because its structure contains two kinds of Fe sites with dis-tinct spin environments. This suggests that the system hosts complex local interactions and that uniform states do not possess an absolute energetic advantage. It also implies that the energetic cost of a 𝜋 phase slip is acceptable, while the system does not favor the formation of denser 𝜋 phase slips such as PCB and Néel Fig. 2(b). To quantify the response of the system to 𝜋 phase slips, we carried out systematic first-principles calculations for Fe supercells with 𝑁 = 6, 8, and 10. Relative to the fully developed SAFM magnetic order, a large number of magnetic configurations with different slip thicknesses are compressed into an extremely narrow energy window of within about 2 meV/Fe Fig. 2(b). This energy scale is already close to the low-temperature spin gap of FeSe, 2.5 meV [12, 13]. Therefore, in the quantum limit at absolute zero, the magnetic ground state should be un-derstood not as a single static configuration, but as a ROSS renormalized by low-energy slip states. Because the cost of independently flipping spins is extremely high, collective ex-citations of the slip walls dominate. As shown in Fig. 2(b), several slip configurations are compressed into an energy window comparable to the spin gap. These low-energy con-figurations can therefore participate in the ROSS ensemble and dynamically mix with the SAFM-like stripe background 3under weak perturbations, as schematically summarized in Fig. 1 and reconstructed in Fig. 4. The extremely low exci-tation strength does not imply that it can be directly excited by neutron scattering at the same energy scale. As noted above, the excitation in FeSe is not single-particle-like but collective, so the minimum energy to which the system can respond also depends on the size of the smallest excitable unit. Owing to the extremely weak interlayer coupling in FeSe [11, 43], the active excitation region appears in the motion of domain walls and can, to a good approximation, be regarded overall as a quasi-one-dimensional excitation within a single layer [44, 45]. To understand why the slip states carry such a small en-ergetic cost, we performed force calculations for a represen-tative (3\7) slip state on the fully relaxed SAFM lattice back-ground. The lattice response induced by the magnetic slip wall is distinctly non-local: the forces triggered by the wall propagate outward through the elastic lattice network and remain appreciable even up to the sixth-nearest-neighbor Fe atoms Fig. 3(a). This result highlights the crucial role of magneto-elastic coupling in FeSe, which distributes the domain-wall formation energy over a larger spatial scale and thereby significantly reduces its effective energetic cost. At the same time, atoms at the nearest-neighbor sites across the domain-wall interface experience a pronounced repulsive force, whereas those at the next-nearest-neighbor sites feel an attractive force. This sign reversal of the local force field around the domain wall provides direct real-space evidence that FeSe lies in a crossover regime between short-range correlations and intermediate-range delocalized response. However, the long-ranged character of the lattice distor-tion does not guarantee long-range locking of the magnetic phase: as a 𝜋 phase defect, the slip wall introduces a non-negligible phase truncation into spin coherence, such that the magnetic correlation length is jointly determined by the defect density and the degree of electronic localization. As a result, even when the elastic response extends over a rela-tively long distance, the magnetic coherence can still remain finite. From the energetics of the slip-state manifold, it can be seen that the formation energy of the system varies with slip-wall thickness (Fig. 3(b)): in (2 ×8) and (2 ×10), slip walls with thickness 3 are energetically more favorable, whereas in the very short-period TRI state they introduce a significant energy penalty. This indicates that the magnetic interac-tion associated with the domain wall is highly sensitive to its local environment. Based on the energy differences of configurations such as (3\3), (3\5), (3\7), and (3\9) relative to their corresponding SAFM states of the same size, we define an effective formation energy for a single slip wall, and find a trend toward a minimum between (3\5) and (3\9), corresponding to an intrinsic optimal magnetic correlation scale of about 10 moments for a thickness-3 domain wall. This also shows that the lowering of the total energy does not rely on long stripe antiferromagnetic segments to reduce the average energy, but instead genuinely reflects the unusual magnetic correlations of the system. # Conclusions 

Taken together, our first-principles energy evaluation and spectrally weighted 𝑆 (𝑞 ) reconstruction show that the mag-netic ground state of FeSe is best described as a Residual-Order Spin State (ROSS), rather than as either a conven-tional long-range ordered phase or a fully random disor-dered state. In this regime, local stripe-like antiferromag-netic correlations persist, whereas long-range phase coher-ence is destroyed by the competition and dynamical mixing of low-energy 𝜋 phase-slip defects. This picture naturally reconciles the coexistence of strong stripe- and Néel-type spin excitations with the absence of static magnetic order, and provides a microscopic basis for spin-fluctuation de-scriptions of superconductivity in FeSe.

# Methods. 

First-principles calculations were performed using the Vi-enna ab initio Simulation Package (VASP 6.5.0) within the collinear spin-polarized framework. A plane-wave cutoff energy of 425 eV was adopted. Brillouin-zone integrations were carried out using Monkhorst–Pack k-point meshes sat-isfying 𝑁 𝑖 𝑎 𝑖 > 40 along each lattice direction. For sev-eral representative configurations, denser k-meshes with 

𝑁 𝑖 𝑎 𝑖 > 60 were further tested, and both the relative en-ergy hierarchy and the main magnetic conclusions remained unchanged, demonstrating good k-point convergence.The ionic relaxation was continued until the residual forces were smaller than 1 × 10 −3 eV/Å, and the total-energy conver-gence criterion was set to 1 × 10 −7 eV. The in-plane lattice constant was fixed at 3.77 Å, and a 0.2% orthorhombic dis-tortion was introduced along the ferromagnetic direction.
