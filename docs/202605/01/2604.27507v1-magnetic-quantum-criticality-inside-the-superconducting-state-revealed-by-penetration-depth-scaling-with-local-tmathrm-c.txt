# Magnetic Quantum Criticality inside the Superconducting State Revealed by Penetration Depth Scaling with Local Tc

(Dated: May 1, 2026) We demonstrate a magnetic quantum critical point embedded within the superconducting state of Zn-doped CeCoIn 5, revealed by a pronounced peak in the magnetic penetration depth at zero temperature λ(0). Using scanning SQUID microscopy, we determine the local superconducting transition temperature Tc and λ(0). By parameterizing λ(0) in terms of the local Tc rather than nominal Zn substitution, we circumvent the ambiguity caused by doping inhomogeneity and enable a more precise extraction of the critical exponent. The extracted exponent exceeds the clean spin-density-wave value, indicating a disorder-modified quantum critical regime. The enhancement of 

λ(0) reflects the suppression of the superfluid stiffness and is consistent with critical scaling. Our approach provides a route to uncover intrinsic quantum critical behavior hidden by inhomogeneity in unconventional superconductors. 

Quantum critical points (QCPs), continuous phase transitions at zero temperature, are a central organiz-ing principle in correlated electron systems [1, 2]. In heavy-fermion compounds, antiferromagnetic quantum critical points (AF-QCPs) are particularly well studied due to the competition between Kondo screening and magnetic order [3]. When superconductivity develops in a dome-like region centered around an AF-QCP, uncon-ventional pairing mediated by critical spin fluctuations can emerge [4]. Understanding this interplay remains a central open question. A key theoretical prediction is that magnetic penetra-tion depth at zero temperature λ(0) is enhanced near an AF-QCP by quantum critical fluctuations associated with magnetic order [5, 6]. While theory predicts a non-analytic increase of λ(0) on the superconducting side of the QCP, whether this leads to an actual peak remains debated [6]. Experimentally, a sharp peak in λ(0) has been reported in BaFe 2(As 1−xPx)2 [7], and a similar en-hancement has been inferred in CeCo(In 1−xZn x)5 from muon spin relaxation ( μSR) [8]. CeCoIn 5 is a prototypical strong-coupling d-wave su-perconductor [9, 10]. Zn substitution has been proposed to locally suppresses Kondo screening, thereby inducing AF order and leading to a low-field AF-QCP at extremely low doping levels ( ∼ 1%) [8]. By contrast, the pristine compound exhibits a distinct field-tuned QCP [11] de-scribed within an itinerant spin fluctuation (SCR) frame-work [12, 13], suggesting a different microscopic origin for the Zn-induced QCP. The microscopic origin of the Zn-induced AF-QCP remains under discussion: previ-ous work has considered both a Kondo-breakdown sce-nario and a more itinerant spin-density-wave (SDW)-like

We also note that scanning tunneling microscopy studies on CeCo(In 1−xZn x)5 films found no clear evidence for impurity-induced AF droplets around individual Zn sites, pointing to a more subtle local effect of Zn substitu-tion [14]. However, the extremely low doping levels required to access the Zn-induced AF-QCP regime make spa-tial inhomogeneity unavoidable. Bulk probes such as 

μSR necessarily average over macroscopic volumes, mak-ing it difficult to disentangle intrinsic critical behavior from disorder-induced broadening. This is evident in CeCo(In 1−xZn x)5 , where μSR measurements on a clus-ter of crystals [8] and neutron scattering on a single crys-tal [15] yield substantially different relationships between nominal doping x, the superconducting transition tem-perature Tc, and the N´ eel temperature TN [Figs. 1(a) and 1(b)]. Here we overcome this limitation using scanning super-conducting quantum interference device (SQUID) mag-netometry and susceptometry to probe superconducting and magnetic properties locally in CeCo(In 1−xZn x)5 sin-gle crystals. This allows simultaneous determination of the local λ(0) and local Tc, and we use the latter as an ef-fective tuning parameter [Fig. 1]. In contrast to nominal doping, this local Tc parametrization yields a clearer and more direct mapping of λ(0) across the quantum critical regime, avoiding ambiguities associated with doping. In Fig. 1(c), by correlating λ(0) with the locally de-termined Tc, we construct a high-resolution map of the penetration depth across the phase diagram. We observe a pronounced enhancement of λ(0) near the AF-QCP, at the local Tc where TN vanishes [Fig. 1(b)]. In a scal-ing analysis with respect to the peak in λ(0), the data

Single crystals of CeCo(In 1−xZn x)5 (nominal x ≤

0.06) were grown by the indium-flux method [16, 17]. The actual Zn concentration is approximately 0 .35 x based on energy-dispersive x-ray spectroscopy, but we use the nominal concentration x throughout. The ab -plane sur-faces were hand polished to ∼50 nm final grit. The data reported here were obtained from five samples with nom-inal Zn concentrations x = 0 , 0.03 , 0.045 a, 0.045 b, and 0.06. Local magnetic measurements were performed using a scanning SQUID microscope in a dilution refrigerator down to ∼ 60 mK. The sensor consists of a gradiometric pickup loop/field coil with inner radii of ∼ 3.0 μm and 

∼ 1.5 μm, respectively [18]. Magnetometry images the local dc flux Φ from isolated vortices in units of the flux quantum Φ 0 = h/ 2e. Susceptometry applies an ac cur-rent Iac ∼ 1 mA at ∼ 1 kHz to the field coil and detects the induced ac flux Φ ac . At contact, the corresponding maximum excitation field at the sample surface is of order 0.1 mT. We define the local susceptibility as the mutual inductance (MI) M = Φ ac /I ac in units of Φ 0/A. To track the penetration depth across the AF-QCP in CeCo(In 1−xZn x)5 , we first imaged isolated vortices at T ∼ 60 mK in samples with nominal x = 0, 3%, 4.5% a, and 6% [Fig. 2(a)]. The vortex profiles show a clear doping dependence, with a strongly suppressed peak height at x = 0 .045. We fit the data using the monopole approximation for the vortex stray field [19],

As shown in Fig. 2(b), the fits describe the vortex cross sections well, yielding λ = 170, 280, 590, and 160 ± 50 nm for nomi-nal x = 0, 0.03, 0 .045 a, and 0.06, respectively. The ex-tracted λ shows a pronounced maximum near x = 0 .045 [Fig. 1(c)], close to the AF-QCP identified by neutron scattering [15]. Since these data were acquired at 60 mK (∼ 3% of Tc), they provide an excellent approximation to λ(0) within our experimental resolution. In the pristine compound, we obtain n = 1 .05 − − 1.24 depending on location, close to the linear-T behavior ex-pected for a clean d-wave state. Previous reports for CeCoIn 5 span n = 1 .25 [27, 28] to n = 1 .5 [29–31], the latter having been discussed in terms of quantum-critical renormalization of nodal quasiparticles [31]. The broad spread of reported values, however, indicates that the low-temperature exponent is not universal even in the pristine CeCoIn 5. By contrast, our data show a system-atic increase of n with Zn substitution [Fig. 1(d) and Fig. 3(b)], which is robust and broadly consistent with increasing impurity scattering in a dirty d-wave state. The extracted λ(0) provides an independent estimate of the penetration depth, complementing the vortex-based values in Fig. 2 [Fig. 1(c)]. For pristine CeCoIn 5,we obtain λ(0) = 200 ±100 nm, consistent with previous microwave and two-coil measurements (196–281 nm) [27, 30, 32], though smaller than μSR estimates of 550– 611 nm [8, 33]. Within this experimen-tally motivated peak-centered parametrization, the data disfavor the clean Gaussian value ν = 0 .5 expected for an itinerant SDW QCP within the Hertz–Millis frame-work [3], and instead favor larger effective exponents. This is naturally compatible with a disorder-modified critical regime, since quenched disorder is expected to be a relevant perturbation to the clean Hertz fixed point in d = 2 and 3. Any resulting conventional dirty critical point must then satisfy the Harris criterion, ν ≥ 2/d [3]. Real-space inhomogeneity is intrinsic to doping-tuned QCPs and cannot be eliminated even with a local probe. However, the use of locally determined Tc substantially reduces the artificial broadening associated with bulk av-eraging over nominal doping and allows the critical en-hancement of λ(0) 2 to be resolved more faithfully. The remaining width of the peak reflects intrinsic real-space inhomogeneity of the sample, implying that the measured 

λ(0) 2 still provides a spatially averaged response and thus a lower bound on the intrinsic divergence. Within this framework, the data are most naturally interpreted in terms of a disorder-broadened critical regime with en-hanced local magnetic correlations beyond the simplest Hertz–Millis description. The phenomenological susceptibility exponent α shows a different evolution. Above Tc, 1/χ ∝ T α yields 

α < 1, and α increases strongly with Zn content while the average magnitude of χ remains nearly unchanged [Fig. 1(e)]. This behavior is not expected for a sim-ple clean quantum-critical scenario with universal ex-ponents, nor is it consistent with a trivial increase of a Curie term from a growing number of fully lo-calized moments. Rather, the systematic growth of 

α is more naturally interpreted as a disorder-sensitive magnetic response amplified near the AF instability, for example through impurity-enhanced AF correlations 5and/or Griffiths-like magnetic fluctuations [35]. Similar disorder-sensitive, nonuniversal magnetic exponents have been discussed in disordered Kondo-lattice systems such as CePd 1−xRh x [36] and in disordered quantum criti-cal systems such as Ni 1−xVx [37]. In this sense, the Zn evolution of α is consistent with the disorder-broadened critical regime inferred independently from the local λ(0) analysis, although the present data do not by themselves distinguish among these scenarios or establish a Griffiths phase. Thus, α behaves as a more disorder-sensitive ef-fective exponent than λ(0), which provides a more direct measure of the critical suppression of the superfluid stiff-ness. The larger value of α reported for CeCoIn 5 in bulk measurements under 0.1 T may indicate some field sen-sitivity of the anomalous susceptibility, although the ab-solute magnitude of χ around 3 K is compatible with our results [38]. However, this field scale is much smaller than that relevant to the field-tuned quantum critical regime (∼5 T) in pristine CeCoIn 5 [11], and therefore does not by itself imply proximity to a field-driven QCP. We have used scanning SQUID microscopy to probe the superconducting properties of CeCo(In 1−xZn x)5 at the microscale and determined both the local penetra-tion depth λ(0) and the local superconducting transition temperature Tc. By using the local Tc rather than nom-inal doping as the tuning parameter, we reduce ambigu-ity from spatial inhomogeneity and thereby resolve the enhancement of λ(0) near the AF-QCP more quantita-tively. By correlating local measurements of Tc, λ(0), 

λ(T ) ∝ T n, and 1 /χ (T ) ∝ T α, we show that the sharp peak in λ(0) 2 cannot be explained by impurity scattering alone, but is instead consistent with quantum-critical en-hancement. Within an experimentally motivated peak-centered scaling analysis, the data disfavor the clean Gaussian value ν = 0 .5 expected from conventional itin-erant SDW theory and instead favor larger effective ex-ponents, indicating that disorder and local magnetic cor-relations are important ingredients of the critical regime. Rather than eliminating intrinsic inhomogeneity, the role of the local probe is to reduce additional broadening from macroscopic averaging and thereby expose the disorder-modified quantum critical regime realized in the actual doped material. Our results establish the combination of local penetration-depth measurements and local Tc map-ping as a powerful approach for probing quantum-critical behavior in intrinsically inhomogeneous superconductors.

−50 Φ 0/A [Fig. S1(b)]. The onset of the diamagnetic response is sharp in the parent compound, whereas it becomes more gradual in the doped samples. This broadened evolution of the susceptometry signal suggests the presence of superconducting inhomogeneity on a length scale smaller than the micron-scale spatial resolution of scanning SQUID susceptometry. In addition, we observe a systematic increase in paramagnetic susceptibility above Tc with increasing Zn concentra-tion. In the x=0.06 sample, where TN > T c, M (z0) exhibits neither a clear peak at TN nor a pronounced decrease below it [Fig. S1(c)]. Since neutron scattering has reported antiferromagnetic order with the c axis as the easy axis [15], a more distinct anomaly would be expected in our out-of-plane susceptometry geometry for a homogeneous long-range ordered state. Its absence suggests that the magnetic state is instead short-ranged or strongly inhomogeneous, more in line with the NMR results [25]. In the x = 0 .045 sample, where TN < T c is expected, no anomaly is detected either, implying that any coupling between antiferromagnetic fluctuations and Cooper pairs is too weak to produce a resolvable susceptometry feature.  

> 2. Fitting Analysis of the penetration depth enhancement at AF-QCP

To examine how the extracted critical exponent depends on the fitting procedure, we analyze the λ(0) 2 data using the combined form introduced in the main text, 

λ(0 , Γ) 2 = q(Γ , Γc, A, C, ν ) h(Γ) , (S1) where 

q(Γ , Γc, A, C, ν ) = 1

C + A|Γ − Γc|3ν−1 (S2) describes the quantum-critical enhancement [6], and 

h(Γ) = 1 + (0 .79) 2Γπ/ 3 + 0 .79 pπΓ/3 (S3) describes the strong-coupling dirty d-wave contribution in the unitary limit [23]. In this parametrization, A controls the overall strength of the critical enhancement, while C sets the scale that limits the apparent divergence at the QCP and hence controls the peak amplitude. For a given ν, C varies monotonically together with A, so the fit landscape is most transparently visualized in the ( ν, A ) plane. Therefore, Fig. S5(a) shows the residual sum of squares (RSS) in the ( ν, A ) plane with C fixed to its best-fit value. The global minimum is obtained at ν = 0 .922, A = 639, and C = 0 .15. Figure S5(a) plots the RSS normalized by its minimum value, together with contours corresponding to 5%, 10%, 20%, and 50% increases above the minimum. Figure S5(b) compares the experimental data with the global best fit and with fits obtained by fixing ν = 1 .0, 0.8, 0.7, 0.6, and 0.5 and optimizing the remaining parameters. In performing this analysis, we exclude one point just above the peak center (nominal x = 0 .04), where the sample shows the strongest microscopic inhomogeneity and the local estimate of λ is least reliable. We also fix Γ c to nominal 

x = 0 .04, corresponding to the observed center of the λ(0) 2 peak. This choice is phenomenological but not arbitrary: shifting Γ c away from this value leads to visibly poorer fits. Moreover, nearly symmetric peak-like enhancements of λ

have been reported in other quantum-critical superconductors [7], even though their microscopic origin remains under debate. In this sense, the present peak-centered parametrization is experimentally motivated, while the resulting preference for ν > 0.5 should still be understood within this fitting framework. 8A further complication is that, in the coexistence regime of superconductivity and AF order, the Abrikosov-Gor’kov description of Tc suppression is no longer adequate, because AF order competes with superconductivity. Therefore, to display the fitted curves in Fig. S5(b) on the nominal-x axis, we convert the measured local Tc to nominal x

using a polynomial interpolation of the experimental x-Tc relation rather than the Abrikosov-Gor’kov fit [Fig. 1(a)]. This introduces a slight asymmetry in the displayed curves, which reflects the experimental mapping between Tc and nominal x, rather than an intrinsic asymmetry of the underlying scaling form. The main conclusion of this analysis is that the clean Gaussian value ν = 0 .5 cannot reproduce the sharp rise of λ(0 , Γ) 2 near the AF-QCP within the present peak-centered fitting procedure, whereas larger effective exponents, already from ν ∼ 0.6, provide substantially better agreement. The global best fit occurs near ν ∼ 0.9, but the precise optimum should not be overinterpreted, because the fit quality depends in part on how the residuals are distributed between the low-x side and the peak region. In particular, the rounded peak structure observed experimentally can arise both from intrinsic scaling with larger effective ν and from residual real-space inhomogeneity. Therefore, this analysis should be taken as evidence that the data disfavor ν = 0 .5 within a simple clean SDW-like description, rather than as a precise or model-independent determination of ν.
