(Dated: March 19, 2026) Nature contains massless particles with linear dispersions, and massive particles whose energies depend quadratically on their momenta with finite mass gaps. Both have equivalents in condensed matter physics in the form of collective modes and quasiparticles , measurable excitations with well-defined energy-momentum relations. A hypothesised third particle type – the super-luminal tachyon – would have an undefined energy at low momentum. A similar collective mode – long hypothesised within the hydrodynamic theory of matter – would have a purely imaginary energy at low momentum, corresponding to a finite lifetime. This third possibility has never been directly observed in a quantum system. Through a careful comparison of hydrodynamics with microscopic models of metals, we establish that this previously unseen third dispersion occurs in correlated quantum matter whenever the electronic fluid undergoes momentum relaxation due to explicit breaking of translation by impurities. As a specific example of these momentum-relaxed modes we consider the recent discovery of an acoustic plasmon – dubbed Pines’ demon – in Sr 2RuO 4. The observed dispersion of this neutral mode differed significantly from the massless linear behaviour predicted by the random phase approximation. We demonstrate that the observed dispersion corresponds, in fact, to a momentum-gapped quasiparticle. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

A dispersion relation links the energy of a particle to its momentum [1]. In high-energy physics, familiar examples include massless particles with linear, gapless spectra ω ∼

q, such as photons, and massive particles with finite rest energies and energy-gapped dispersions ω2 ∼ q2 + ∆ 2 [2, 3] (Fig. 1). Their condensed-matter counterparts appear as collective modes and quasiparticles with well-defined energy–momentum relations. Well known examples include massless Weyl fermions [4–6] and massive optical plasmons [7, 8]. A third possibility has been theorised but not observed: a dispersion in which the mode is overdamped below a finite momentum qc and the dispersion becomes purely imaginary. Hereafter we refer to this as a momentum gap. In particle language this resembles an “imaginary mass” and is reminiscent of the hypothetical tachyon with a dispersion of the form ω2 ∼ q2 − ∆2 [9, 10]. In hydrodynamics, which need not admit a single-particle interpretation, momentum-gapped modes have long been predicted [11–20], yet have only been observed in shear waves in classical settings [21, 22]. In such cases, there is no notion of the wave vector k corresponding to the momentum of a particle or quasiparticle: it is the wave number of a sound wave. These ideas have become timely following the recent report of an acoustic electronic collective mode in Sr 2RuO 4 [23], dubbed Pines’ demon. This is a 3D acoustic plasmon arising as a collective mode of electrons between different bands [24–27]. The conditions for its existence have been studied for decades [24, 26, 27], but the experimental evidence of its existence was only obtained in 2024 [23]. The data show certain inconsistencies with the original predictions [24–27]: while the dispersion of an acoustic mode intercepts the origin — having zero energy at zero momentum —the measured dispersion of Pines’ demon in Sr 2RuO 4

does not, and the low energy behaviour remains experimentally challenging to resolve. Crucially, real materials are never perfectly clean: at the energy and momentum scales of the momentum-resolved electron energy loss spectroscopy (m-EELS) measurements used to observe Pines’ demon [23], even small amounts of disorder can introduce notable momentum relaxation and thereby qualitatively reshape collective dynamics. Microscopically, standard theoretical approaches based on the Random Phase Approximation (RPA) do not capture the observed behaviour [7, 28–31]. Moreover, commonly used ad hoc vertex corrections to treat disorder, such as Hubbard [7] or Mermin [32] schemes, can become unphysical once momentum relaxation is introduced. A controlled treatment of disorder-induced vertex corrections is therefore required to obtain aconsistent dielectric response and to interpret the demon’s dispersion. Furthermore, Pines’ demon could mediate an exotic new form of superconductivity [33, 34]. It is therefore crucial to understand its behaviour in real materials with finite disorder. Here we show that the presence of any amount of momentum relaxation – always present in real materials – causes Pines’ demon to develop a momentum gap [35]. The demon’s observation in Sr 2RuO 4

therefore constitutes the first direct measurement of a momentum-gapped quasiparticle. In the general setting, we derive the dispersion in both momentum-relaxed hydrodynamics and amicroscopic quantum theory incorporating disorder

Specialising to Sr 2RuO 4, we quantitatively predict the dispersion of Pines’ demon and compare it with the experimental results in Ref. [23], obtaining aremarkable agreement. > II. RESULTS

The central object by which to study Pines’ demon’s dispersion is the dielectric function ϵ(q, ω ) as a function of electronic momentum transfer q and angular frequency 

ω [24, 26, 27], which encodes how a material screens charges. Collective charge modes are obtained by solving 

ϵ(q, ω ) = 0, corresponding to poles in the physical response function ϵ−1. For optical plasmons at small 

q, the plasmon is an eigenmode of the Hamiltonian and satisfies Im ϵ(q, ω ) = 0 [7, 8], so the dispersion can be read off from Re ϵ(q, ω ) = 0. Acoustic plasmons in 3D are qualitatively different. They generally lie inside the particle-hole continuum, meaning they acquire intrinsic damping with Im ϵ(q, ω )̸ = 0. Their dispersions must be extracted from the complex-frequency solutions of 

ϵ(q, ω ) = 0 with ω ∈ C. We proceed to present this calculation using both hydrodynamic and microscopic theories.

> III. CONCLUSIONS

Hydrodynamics and its extension to slowly-relaxing momentum has become a vital tool in the study of electronic systems in which entanglement and strong correlations cause the breakdown of the quasiparticle picture and the failure of standard microscopic theories. Notable examples include the ‘strange metals’ from which many cuprate high-Tc superconductors form, ‘bad metals’ which include Sr 2RuO 4, and graphene [43– 45]. Degree-of-freedom counting often suggests aconnection between macroscopic hydrodynamic modes and microscopic quasiparticle dispersions, but the direct demonstration of an adiabatic connection between the two has so far proven elusive even in simple models. This makes the exact analytic agreement we report here – between hydrodynamics on the one hand, and quasiparticle dispersions of disordered multi-band metals on the other – all the more remarkable. It seems reasonable to hope that this connection may provide clues in the search for microscopic descriptions of systems in which hydrodynamics provides the best current understanding. Our results also reveal a general feature of quasiparticle dispersions. Any real material has momentum relaxation – be it from defects, disorder, or finite crystal size – and so any putative gapless bosonic quasiparticle will feature a momentum gap energy scale. In this sense, the detailed dispersion of Pines’ demon we report in Fig. 2 is generic. There are two main reasons momentum-gapped dispersions had not previously been measured. First, emergent bosons’ masslessness is often symmetry–protected, such as when they arise from Goldstone’s theorem (as in the case of acoustic phonons in crystals). Second, a mass gap will also typically be present; if the mass gap is larger than the momentum gap, the familiar massive-particle dispersion will result. Importantly, a mass gap will always open in the presence of Coulomb interactions: the standard optical plasmon is an example. The special feature of Pines’ demon that allowed its momentum gap to be revealed is that it is charge neutral. This made its experimental observation all the more remarkable [23]. Crucially, acoustic plasmons could play a role in the superconducting pairing of exotic and high-Tc superconductors, such as metal hydrides [33, 34], by lowering the Coulomb repulsion between electrons [28, 46–49]. In this regard, it is relevant to ask if the momentum-gapped Pines’ demon plays a role in the exotic but not yet fully understood multiband superconductivity of Sr 2RuO 4 [37, 50–54], and the high-T c superconductivity of multiband metals.
