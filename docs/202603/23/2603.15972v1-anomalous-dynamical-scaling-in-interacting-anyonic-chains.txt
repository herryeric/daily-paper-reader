(Dated: March 18, 2026) Particle statistics impose fundamental constraints on nonequilibrium quantum dynamics, yet it re-mains an open question whether fractional statistics can lead to emergent universal dynamical scaling beyond the conventional Bose-Fermi paradigm. Here we investigate the far-from-equilibrium many-body relaxation of anyons in a one-dimensional lattice and uncover an unconventional yet universal scaling behavior governed by fractional statistics. Based on large-scale numerical simulations and scaling analysis, we identify a distinct separation between particle transport and information spread-ing: density correlations spread superdiffusively, whereas entanglement entropy grows ballistically. The anomalous particle dynamics can be interpreted intuitively from the statistical-phase-induced quantum interference, which suppresses coherent holon-doublon propagation. In contrast, the en-tanglement growth turns out to be dominated by its configurational component, which propagates ballistically. Our results establish anyonic statistics as a distinct source of universal nonequilib-rium dynamics beyond bosons and fermions, with direct relevance to current quantum simulation experiments. 

Introduction— Fractional statistics provides a funda-mental extension of quantum many-body physics beyond the conventional Bose-Fermi framework [1–5]. Its phys-ical relevance has been established through the observa-tion of exotic quantum phenomena, including the frac-tional quantum Hall effect [6–9] and topological spin liquids [10–12], and has stimulated extensive interest in topological quantum computation [13–19]. While anyonic statistics are intrinsically associated with two-dimensional systems, anyonic behavior can also emerge in one dimension (1D). For instance, fractional exclu-sion statistics were first revealed through the generalized Pauli principle in the Haldane-Shastry model [20, 21]. This has triggered extensive studies of 1D anyonic mod-els both in the continuum [22–25] and on discrete lat-tices [26–28], leading to hallmark ground-state phenom-ena such as asymmetric momentum distributions [26, 29– 31], Friedel oscillations [32], and statistics-induced phase transitions [27, 33, 34]. Experimentally, recent advances in ultracold atoms have brought the engineering of any-onic properties within reach [2, 35, 36, 38–40], making it both timely and essential to investigate the role of any-onic statistics in the nonequilibrium dynamics [41–46] of quantum many-body systems. Far-from-equilibrium dynamics in bosonic and fermionic settings has attracted considerable attention in recent years [47–52]. These quantum many-body systems exhibit rich emergent dynamical scaling behav-ior, providing valuable insights into collective quantum phenomena [53–58]. Ballistic and diffusive transport have been observed experimentally in a broad range of quantum simulators [59–62]. Beyond these conven-tional regimes, qualitatively new universality classes have also been identified, such as Kardar-Parisi-Zhang scaling in 1D quantum magnets [63, 64]. This behavior arises from the interplay of integrability and internal non-Abelian symmetries [65–67] and has been observed in quantum gas microscopy [68] and programmable quantum circuits [69]. These developments raise afundamental question: can particle statistics itself give rise to universal scaling in far-from-equilibrium quantum dynamics beyond the Bose-Fermi paradigm? To address this question, we investigate the many-body relaxation dynamics of a 1D anyon-Hubbard model (AHM) [Fig. 1(a)], starting from far-from-equilibrium initial states. Upon varying the statistical angle θ, we uncover emergent universal scaling behavior in the long-time dynamics that is distinct from conventional bosonic and fermionic limits. This feature manifests as a pro-nounced separation between the dynamics of density cor-relations and entanglement [Fig. 1(b)], reminiscent of particle-information decoupling discussed in many-body-localized systems [70–73]. Specifically, the dynamical scaling of density correlations exhibits a crossover from ballistic ( z = 1) to superdiffusive ( z = 3 /2) behavior as

Anomalous scaling of density correlation— We first investigate the far-from-equilibrium scaling dynamics of density correlations in the anyonic chain for the noninter-acting limit. We find that tuning the statistical angle θ

drives a crossover from ballistic to superdiffusive spread-ing, in contrast to the ballistic dynamics of integrable bosonic and fermionic chains. This anomalous behav-ior is revealed by the CTD following a quench from the unit-filled product state |ψ0⟩ = | · · · 111 · · · ⟩ . As shown in Fig. 2(a), following a short θ-independent period, the characteristic length l(t) exhibits robust power-law scal-ing, l(t) ∼ tβ . The growth exponent β decreases mono-tonically with increasing θ, from β = 1 in the bosonic limit ( θ = 0) to β = 2 /3 at θ = π [inset of Fig. 2(a)]. In a 1D system, the corresponding dynamical exponent satisfies z = 1 /β [90], establishing superdiffusive dynam-ics (1 < z < 2) induced by anyonic statistics, distinct from the bosonic and fermionic limits ( z = 1) [82, 90]. In finite systems, boundary effects can affect the scaling behavior. Here we find that a lattice size of L = 100 al-ready suffices to capture the anomalous scaling [90]. This scaling is independently confirmed by half-chain density fluctuations ∆ N (t), which collapse onto a universal curve when time is rescaled as t/L z , yielding z = 3 /2 at θ = π

[Fig. 2(b)]. Although larger systems cannot be evolved long enough for ∆ N (t) to reach the L-dependent satura-tion plateau, a small system of L = 14 readily saturates, allowing accurate extraction of z (inset of Fig. 2(b), [84]). We next examine the interplay between anyonic statis-tics and interactions by analyzing the CTD dynamics across a broad range of interaction strengths U . In the weakly interacting regime, the scaling exponent β re-mains robust. As summarized in Fig. 2(d), the dynam-ics at θ = π is superdiffusive with β ≈ 2/3 ( U ≤ J), while the bosonic limit ( θ = 0) remains ballistic. By contrast, in the strong-interaction regime, the growth of 

l(t) is nearly ballistic for all θ, with β ≈ 1, as shown in Fig. 2(c). This reflects the suppression of particle-exchange processes by strong interactions, rendering the effect of fractional statistics negligible and recovering conventional ballistic dynamics. The universal anomalous scaling is found to be robust against disorder and initial-state variations. We study 

l(t) in the presence of finite onsite disorder and for dif-ferent initial states, such as | · · · 110211 · · · ⟩ and the den-sity wave | · · · 0202 · · · ⟩ [90]. In all cases, we find that the growth exponent β remains largely unchanged, demon-strating that the anomalous scaling is intrinsically deter-mined by anyonic statistics.

Unlike many-body localized systems, where such separation is induced by disorder [70–73], here it emerges intrinsically from anyonic statistics. To understand this behavior, we decompose S(t) into number and configurational contributions. This decom-position was introduced as a symmetry-resolved entan-glement diagnostic to distinguish many-body localiza-tion from thermalization in disordered systems [95–98]. We find that while eSN (t) exhibits an anomalous dynamical exponent z = 1 .5 [Fig. 3(c)], eSC (t)grows ballistically with z = 1 .05 [Fig. 3(d)]. Throughout the evolution, SC (t) ≫ SN (t), and thus the total entropy inherits ballistic scaling from the configurational degrees of freedom. This accounts for the faster spread of entan-glement compared to density fluctuations [Fig. 2(b)(d)]. 

Quantum interference in anyonic lattice— We can un-derstand the anomalous dynamical scaling by appealing to the propagation of a holon-doublon pair on a unit-filled background, which reflects the spreading of den-sity correlations [93]. We find that propagation of the pair is suppressed due to quantum interference between hopping paths, revealing that the anomalous correlation scaling arises from anyonic statistics. Fig. 4(a) schemati-cally illustrates holon-doublon propagation, where differ-ent hopping paths acquire θ-dependent phases. Starting from a holon-doublon pair contributing density correla-tions at distance d = 1, we consider its propagation to distance d = 3, where d denotes the holon-doublon sep-aration. A representative contribution involves two in-terfering paths: (i) two adjacent particles tunnel sequen-tially to the right (orange arrows) without exchanging, yielding an amplitude J2 in the noninteracting limit; (ii) the left particle tunnels twice (green arrows), exchang-ing with its neighbor, acquiring an anyonic phase fac-tor 2 J2e−iθ . The resulting amplitude arises from the coherent sum of such processes. In the bosonic limit (θ = 0), the two paths interfere constructively, enhancing the propagation of the holon-doublon pair. In contrast, at θ = π, the interference becomes destructive, strongly suppressing the propagation. Additional exchange pro-cesses give rise to more complex interference patterns, which can suppress correlation spreading over a broad range of θ > 0 [90]. To quantify the effect of anyonic statistics on holon-doublon propagation, we compute the time evolution of the density deviation δn j (t) = nj (t) − ¯n in the weakly interacting regime, starting from an initial state 

| · · · 110211 · · · ⟩ containing a central holon-doublon pair. Here, ¯n denotes the average density. As shown in Fig. 4(b), the pair spreads ballistically in the bosonic limit ( θ = 0). By contrast, the propagation is strongly suppressed for intermediate (θ = π/ 2) and pseud-ofermionic ( θ = π) statistics. 

Experimental accessibility— The identified many-body features are within reach of current ultracold atom exper-iments, leveraging the Floquet protocol recently demon-strated in Ref. [2, 40]. While this protocol was originally demonstrated in a two-particle setting, the underlying in-terference mechanism remains robust in the many-body regime. As detailed in Fig. 4(c), a three-tone modulation of the lattice depth resonantly restores tunneling other-wise suppressed by the static tilt. Crucially, the phase offset θ of the V3 component (dot-dashed line) imple-ments the density-dependent Peierls phase. Conclusion— We have studied the far-from-equilibrium quench dynamics of interacting anyons in a 1D lattice, revealing unique dynamical scaling governed by anyonic statistics. In the weakly interacting regime, particle-number fluctuations cross over from ballistic to superdif-fusive, a behavior beyond both bosonic and fermionic limits. Remarkably, while number fluctuations spread superdiffusively, the half-chain entanglement entropy re-mains ballistic, demonstrating a clear separation between particle and information propagation. This anomalous scaling is connected to quantum interference effects in-duced by the anyonic statistical phase, which suppress the propagation of coherent holon-doublon pairs. Our results provide new insights into nonequilibrium physics governed by anyonic statistics and advance the con-trol of transport and entanglement in synthetic quan-tum matter, realizable with current experimental tech-niques [2]. Future work may clarify the universality classes underlying the predicted scaling using high-order cumulants in the long-time dynamics of larger quantum systems [68, 69]. It is an interesting question to ex-plore richer nonequilibrium dynamics and entanglement growth beyond one dimension [5, 15]. We acknowledge useful discussions with Yinghai Wu and Felix Helluin. > II. NUMERICAL CONVERGENCE

In this section, we present convergence tests for the numerical results on the dynamics of the anyonic Hubbard chain, obtained using the time-dependent variational principle algorithm. Since matrix-product-state simulations are inherently variational, it is essential to verify convergence with respect to the numerical control parameters. We perform systematic convergence checks by varying the bond dimension, system size L, time step dt , and local occupation cutoff Nm, as shown in Fig. S1. The results demonstrate that, for the model parameters considered in this work, the dependence on these parameters is weak and does not significantly affect the system dynamics.  

> III. EXTRACTION OF GROWTH EXPONENT β

In this section, we describe the numerical procedure used to extract the dynamical exponent β from the time evolution of the CTD l(t). As discussed in the main text, l(t) exhibits a steady growth regime at intermediate times, characterized by a power-law scaling l(t) ∼ tβ . To determine β reliably, we perform power-law fits to l(t) within an intermediate-time window [ tl, t r ], where neither short-time transients nor finite-size saturation effects dominate the dynamics. Specifically, we vary the left boundary tl of the fitting window while keeping the right boundary tr fixed, and monitor the convergence of the extracted exponent. The early-time regime is excluded from the fitting window because the dynamics in this regime is dominated by free processes, which do not reflect the emergent many-body scaling behavior. At late times, l(t) gradually approaches a saturation value due to the finite system size, and data points in this regime are also excluded from the fit. We find that all contributions exhibit ballistic scaling, with the configuration entropy SC (t) remaining larger than the number entropy 

SN (t) throughout the evolution. These features are consistent with the behavior observed in the anyonic case ( θ = π/ 2) discussed in the main text.

> VI. ADDITIONAL PROPAGATION PROCESSES OF HOLON-DOUBLON PAIRS

In the main text, we presented a representative example of the propagation of correlations from d = 1 to d = 3, corresponding to the leftward propagation of the holon. Here we provide the complementary process associated with the rightward propagation of the doublon, in Fig. S6(a). This contribution also involves two interfering paths: (i) two adjacent particles tunnel sequentially to the right (orange arrows), exchanging once and yielding an amplitude 2J2e−iθ ; (ii) the left particle tunnels twice (green arrows), exchanging twice with its neighbor and acquiring an anyonic phase 4 J2e−i2θ . The total amplitude arises from the coherent sum of these processes. In the bosonic limit ( θ = 0), the two paths interfere constructively, enhancing the propagation of the holon-doublon pair, whereas in the fermionic limit ( θ = π), the interference becomes destructive, strongly suppressing the propagation. The dynamics of the density deviation δn j (t) shown in the main text indicate that the site with δn j < 0 remains on the left while the site with δn j > 0 remains on the right. This implies that for θ > 0, the holon and doublon effectively propagate toward opposite sides of the chain. To understand this behavior, we analyze the motion of the holon-doublon pair. We consider cases where the pair propagates coherently to the right [Fig. S6(b)] or to the left [Fig. S6(c)]. For the rightward propagation [case (b)], the contribution again involves two interfering paths: (i) one particle of the doublon hops to the left, filling the holon site, followed by the other hopping to the right, yielding an amplitude 2 J2; (ii) one particle of the doublon hops to the right first, then the other hops to the left, acquiring an anyonic phase 2 J2e−iθ . The hopping order determines the accumulated phase, and the total amplitude is given by the coherent superposition of these processes. Such coherent pair propagation is suppressed for θ > 0. Consequently, during the time evolution the holon-doublon pair tends either to remain localized or to separate toward opposite sides of the chain. As a result, the growth of particle-number fluctuations in the half-chain is suppressed for θ > 0, and the corresponding number entropy exhibits a strongly reduced growth rate, consistent with the main text. To elucidate the role of the anyonic statistical angle in the coherent propagation of holon-doublon pairs at larger distances, we illustrate the representative processes contributing to a separation d = 4, as shown in Fig. S6(d). Three typical paths contribute to this process, each acquiring a distinct phase determined by the anyonic angle θ, which controls the resulting interference pattern. (i) Three adjacent particles hop sequentially to the right (blue arrows), involving one exchange and yielding an amplitude 2 J3e−iθ ; (ii) two particles separated by one site each hop once to the right, followed by a hop of one particle from the left doublon (orange arrows), resulting in an amplitude 4 J3e−i2θ ; (iii) the leftmost particle hops three times sequentially from left to right (green arrows), acquiring an amplitude 8 J3e−i3θ .Additional hopping sequences are omitted for clarity. The total amplitude for propagation to d = 4 arises from the coherent superposition of all contributing paths, each carrying a distinct exchange phase. As the propagation distance increases, the superposition of these multiple contributions generates increasingly intricate interference structures in

> VII. EXPERIMENTAL REALIZATION OF THE ANYON HUBBARD MODEL

Following the experimental framework demonstrated in Ref. [2], we discuss the realization of the anyon Hubbard model in an optical lattice. As shown in Fig. S7(a), the experimental implementation utilizes a tilted 1D Bose-Hubbard chain with interaction U0, where a magnetic field gradient creates a static energy offset E between adjacent sites. This potential tilt effectively suppresses the bare tunneling J. To restore particle tunneling with amplitude J′, the lattice depth is modulated with resonant frequencies that compensate for the energy differences of specific hopping processes, a technique known as Floquet engineering. This Floquet approach provides an effective route to realize density-dependent hopping phases, as illustrated in Fig. S7(b). Defining the effective on-site interaction as U , the primary processes are: (i) |1, 0⟩ → | 0, 1⟩, driven at frequency ω1 = E; (ii) |1, 1⟩ → | 0, 2⟩, driven at ω2 = E − (U0 − U ); (iii) |2, 0⟩ → | 1, 1⟩, driven at ω3 = E + ( U0 + U ). To map these processes onto the anyon Hubbard model, an additional phase offset θ is imprinted on the drive for process (iii), while processes (i) and (ii) remain reference transitions [gray block in Fig. S7(b)]. This configuration is sufficient for low fillings; however, at unit filling, an additional process (iv) |2, 1⟩ → | 1, 2⟩ emerges as an important additional contribution. Due to the energy offset E, this transition is unavoidably induced by the same Floquet channel as process (i) and lacks the required phase (orange block). We demonstrate that this imperfect higher-order term does not destroy the observation of holon-doublon propaga-tion. As shown in Fig. S7(c), the expansion of a pair from distance d = 1 to d = 3 involves an interference between two major paths: (left) sequential particle hops that accumulate no phase, and (right) one particle hops twice, ac-quiring an anyonic phase e−iθ . The resulting θ-dependent interference patterns remain robust, as process (iv) does not dominate the dynamics in this regime. Similarly, in Fig. S7(d), two processes contribute to pair expansion: (left) the sequential hopping process accumulates a phase θ, whereas (right) twice hopping carries no phase in the present Floquet scheme. Although this differs from the ideal anyonic model, where the latter would acquire a phase e2iθ , the interference between the two paths remains sensitive to θ.Our analysis confirms that the proposed Floquet scheme remains a powerful tool for observing the hallmarks of anyonic statistics, specifically the suppression of coherent transport and asymmetric expansion dynamics. The parameters used in the numerical simulation of Floquet engineering in the main text are E/J = 30, U0/J = 4, 

U/J = 0 .2, K/J = 40, with an effective tunneling rate J′/J ∼ 0.5.
