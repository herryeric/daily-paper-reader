Abstract  Cuprate  HTSCs exhibit a dome -shaped dependence of the superconduct-

ing transition temperature on the charge carrier concentration,  Tc(p), with a maxi-

mum at  p = 0.16. Near the composition  p = 1/8, a dip in  Tc is observed  (the тАЬ1/8

investigating the hydration process of  La 2-xBa xCuO 4 and  YBa 2Cu 3O6+ яБд conducted at 

room temperature (RT)  and  under the influence of a high -frequency magnetic field, 

we have discovered unusual weight changes in HTSC samples during the initial stage 

of hydration. For both studied compounds, the dependence of weight changes on the 

concentration  p was found to almost exactly replicate the patterns of the correspond-

ing  Tc(p) dependencies, including the  тАЬ1/8 anomaly тАЭ. Such a manifestation of charac-

teristic low -temperature features of HTSC systems at RT is intriguing. The results of 

this experimental work will be useful for the further development of HTSC theories.

# Introduction 

One of the most interesting features of cuprate  high -temperature superconductors is 

the non -monotonic dependence of the critical transition temperature  Tc on the hole 

carrier concentration  p. While the overall topology of this dependence is represent-

ed by a dome with a peak at  p = 0.16  [1, 2] , in several systems, such as  La 2-

> x

Ba xCuO 4 (LBCO), a sharp anomalous suppression ( тАЬdip тАЭ) of  Tc is observed in a 

narrow range near  p яВ╗ 1/8  [3 тАУ5] . In other systems, such as  YBa 2Cu 3O6+ яБд (YBCO), a 

similar suppression of  Tc occurs, although it is less pronounced and  тАЬsmeared тАЭ over 

a wide interval of  p values centered at  p яВ╗ 1/8  [6 тАУ8] . This phenomenon, known as 

the  тАЬ1/8 anomaly тАЭ, indicates a fundamental competition between superconductivity 

and alternative types of electronic/hole ordering.  In cuprate HTSCs, its appearance 

is due to the existence of fluctuating charge density waves (CDW) in 

the  CuO 2 structural planes (as in the case of YBCO  [9 тАУ11] ) or alternating charge 

and magnetic  тАЬstripes тАЭ (as in the case of LBCO  [12] ). At  p яВ╗ 1/8, their wave vectors 

begin to resonate with the periodicity of the crystal structure, becoming static and, 

in some cases, forming a three -dimensional structure. Once they  тАЬgain strength тАЭ, 

they disrupt the phase coherence of the superconducting order parameter (SC), re-

ducing  Tc, sometimes down to zero  [2, 13] .

On the phase diagram of cuprate  HTSCs, a so -called quantum critical point 

(QCP) is located at  p* = 0.19,  T = 0  [14]. At this point, quantum magnetic fluctua-

tions reach their maximum; these fluctuations are believed to work in tandem with 

phonons to bind holes in the  CuO 2 planes into Cooper pairs. This leads to maximum 

values of the critical temperature  Tc near  p* (at  тАЬoptimal тАЭ doping  p яВ╗ 0.16 ). As the 

temperature increases, the range (of  p) of quantum fluctuations expands, forming 

the  тАЬstrange metal тАЭ phase region, where spin fluctuations persist up to  T >> RT  [15, 

16] . At  T = RT, this represents a large, phase -homogeneous region centered at  p яВ╗

0.19. It encompasses both the optimal doping region , p яВ╗ 0.16 , and the  p яВ╗

1/8  region without any specific features at these points  [2, 15 ]. Over the last decade, 

numerous studies have begun to identify dynamic  charge density fluctuations  within 

the strange metal phase (see, e.g.,  [16 ] and references therein), although specific 

long -range ordered electronic and magnetic phases were not observed there  [2, 15, 

16 ].

In the work presented below, the specific intervals of values near  p =

1/8  and  p = 0.16, similar to those observed in the  Tc(x/яБд) dependence, manifest at a 

sufficiently high temperature. What is their nature? In our experiments on cuprate 

HTSCs  La 2-xBa xCuO 4 and  YBa 2Cu 3O6+ яБд, they appear as a kind of exotic weight loss 

in samples during their hydration at RT. On the phase diagram, this temperature is 

significantly higher than both  Tc and the onset temperature of  CDW  formation  (100 

K < TCDW  < 200  K).  The aim of this work is to record this feature of the  p =

1/8  and  p = 0.16  points  тАУ their temperature independence; this allows them to be 

viewed as a manifestation of something more stable than CDW and superconductiv-

ity. 

This paper presents experimental data on the sharp weight loss of gas -tight 

reactors containing powdered HTSC and a crystal hydrate that stabilizes a humid 

atmosphere. It is shown that the magnitude of weight loss, depending on the HTSC 

composition, replicates the shape of the well -known dome -

shaped  Tc(x/яБд) dependencies with a deep  тАЬdip тАЭ near  p = 1/8. This study is a continu-

ation of the author's previous works  [17, 18 ] on this topic. 

# Experimental Details 

The experimental procedure for the YBCO compound is described in detail in our 

previous works  [17, 18 ]. Here, we have supplemented the results obtained in the lat-

ter study with weight loss data for two new oxygen compositions ( яБд = 0.27 

and  0.9 0). The cuprate  La 2-xBa xCuO 4 (compositions  x = 0.05, 0.10, 0.1125, 0.125, 

0.1375, 0.15, 0.20 , and  0.25 ) was synthesized from  La 2O3, CuO  oxides and barium 

carbonate using ceramic technology. The initial calcination of stoichiometric mix-

tures was performed at  900  ┬░C for 20 hours. After regrinding, the compositions 

were fired at  1100  ┬░C for 20 hours, then at  1080  ┬░C for 45 hours with intermediate grinding.  The synthesized material was oxidized in an oxygen atmosphere at 510  ┬░C 

for 3 h. All subsequent procedures, including sample preparation, placement in a 

gas -tight reactor, treatment in an alternating magnetic field, and weighing, were per-

formed in strict accordance with the protocols previously used for YBCO samples; 

these are described in detail in our previous work [18].

# Discussion 

The dip in Fig. 4 corresponding to the  яБДW( яБд) dependence is significantly wider than 

that observed for  Tc(яБд). If we assume they are indirectly linked through some com-

mon physical phenomenon, then the broadening of the dip with increasing tempera-

ture is quite natural. At the same time, the width of the dip in Fig. 3, as well as the 

regions of maximum values, conversely, narrows. Thus, the competition between 

the nominal phases  тАУ тАЬCDW тАЭ and  тАЬSC тАЭ (superconductivity)  тАУ at RT leads to the 

victory of the  тАЬCDW тАЭ phase: its region extends right up to the  тАЬSC тАЭ.

Naturally, neither CDW nor SC phases exist at RT. However, it was recently 

discovered that an extensive region of short -range charge modulations exists above 

the low -temperature CDW phase, persisting to temperatures well above the CDW 

onset temperature [16, 19]. These modulations interact with the crystal lattice (their 

intensity peaks at  p яВ╗ 1/8 ); thus, the doping region near  p яВ╗ 1/8  should be distinct. 

What if the wide dip in the  яБДW( яБд) dependence of the YBCO cuprate centered at  p яВ╗

1/8  reflects the transformation of the low -temperature CDW phase into high -

temperature  charge density fluctuations? In turn, the high -temperature tetragonal 

modification existing in the LBCO cuprate at RT could, by analogy with the low -

temperature tetra -phase, effectively  тАЬpin тАЭ these fluctuations in a narrow  p range  (to-

gether with impurity centers having the corresponding concentration) .

The weight loss of a cuprate HTSC was first observed by E. Podkletnov at 

temperatures  T < Tc [20, 21].  The magnitude of the weight loss was significant. When the temperature rose above  Tc, the effect vanished  тАУ apparently, it simply de-

creased greatly and was not recorded under rather difficult weighing conditions. 

Thus, the drop -effect is clearly associated with the superconducting state in HTSC 

cuprates. Our experiments showed that the maximum weight loss values at  T =

RT  are only  ~0.03%  of the sample mass. Therefore, the existence region of the su-

perconducting phase also appears to have an  тАЬecho тАЭ at high temperatures. In-

deed,  quantum magnetic fluctuations, believed to be the  тАЬglue тАЭ for pairing charges, 

exist in the strange metal region up to the crystal destruction temperatures  [15, 16] .

So , it has been hypothesized that fluctuations of the superconducting order parame-

ter may persist within the strange metal phase even at room temperature (RT) [22 тАУ

24]. The identification of high -temperature charge modulations has required state -

of -the -art experimental techniques and substantial effort. It is anticipated that fur-

ther investigations will lead to the explicit detection of charge -pairing fluctuations. 

The drop -effect dependencies on  x/яБд clearly trace the struggle between two 

antagonistic phenomena, identical to the low -temperature competition between the 

CDW and SC phases. It can be assumed that competition for charge carriers occurs 

here as well, but between  тАЬhigh -temperature modifications тАЭ of CDW and SC.  The 

regions of тАЬmodified CDW and SCтАЭ not only compete but also gravitate toward 

each other, as evidenced by their convergence. This may explain why the positions 

of the maximum  Tc and  яБДW values remain unchanged 

# Conclusion 

Experimental data were obtained on the sharp weight loss (drop -effect) of  La 2-

> x

Ba xCuO 4 samples during hydration in gas -tight reactors under a high -frequency 

magnetic field (50 MHz). The magnitude of the drop -effect shows a dependence on 

the polyvalent substitution degree  x, which almost exactly replicates the well -

known dome -shaped  Tc(x) curve. These results may be of interest for emerging 

HTSC theories.
