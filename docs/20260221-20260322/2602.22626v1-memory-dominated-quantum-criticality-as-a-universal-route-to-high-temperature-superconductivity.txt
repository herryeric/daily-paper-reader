# Memory-Dominated Quantum Criticality as a Universal Route to High-Temperature Superconductivity 

Byung Gyu Chae 1

> 1

Understanding the dynamical origin of high–temperature superconductivity remains a central challenge of strongly correlated quantum matter. Conventional approaches to quantum criticality assume overdamped Markovian dissipation governed by Ohmic Landau damping. Here we show that infrared collective dynamics is instead generically controlled by the time–scale density of states (TDOS) of relaxation modes. Within the Martin–Siggia–Rose–Janssen–De Dominicis formalism, we derive an exact spectral representation of the collective susceptibility in terms of the TDOS. A finite infrared TDOS defines a new universality class of memory–dominated critical dynamics characterized by long–time kernels K(t) ∼ 1/t and nonanalytic dynamical response. This spectral reorganization produces a strong infrared amplification of the particle–particle channel, converting the marginal instabilities of BCS and Eliashberg theories into algebraic superconducting transitions. As a result, the transition temperature scales linearly with the infrared spectral weight of slow collective modes, naturally yielding superconducting domes and Uemura scaling without invoking bosonic glue or fine tuning. The same slow–mode reservoir governs anomalous normal–state dynamics, including long– time correlations and strange–metal behavior, providing a unified description of thermodynamic and dynamical phenomena in correlated superconductors. Our results establish dynamical spectral organization as a fundamental principle of quantum critical matter and identify memory–dominated criticality as a generic route to high–temperature superconductivity. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

High–temperature superconductivity remains one of the central unsolved problems of strongly correlated quantum matter [1, 2]. Despite decades of intensive theoretical and experimental effort, no consensus has emerged on the microscopic mechanism responsible for robust pairing far above conventional phonon scales. A central difficulty lies in the intimate proximity of super-conductivity to quantum critical regimes, where strong collective fluctuations dominate low–energy dynamics. Within the prevailing theoretical paradigm, quantum critical fluctuations are assumed to be overdamped by fast electronic continua [3, 4]. This framework, formal-ized in Hertz–Millis theory, predicts a universal Ohmic Landau damping kernel of the form |ω| governing collec-tive dynamics. While remarkably successful in weakly correlated metals, this assumption has increasingly come into tension with experiments in strongly correlated sys-tems, including cuprate superconductors and strange metals, where anomalous temporal correlations, non-Markovian response, and 1 /f -like noise are ubiquitously observed [5–9]. These observations raise a fundamental question: Is overdamped Markovian critical dynamics truly universal, or does it rely on implicit assumptions about the struc-ture of dynamical spectra that break down in strongly correlated regimes? A key limitation of conventional approaches is their formulation in terms of a small number of collective order-parameter fields [10, 11]. Such low-dimensional re-ductions necessarily generate a finite set of soft modes and therefore preclude the emergence of a dense con-tinuum of relaxation time scales. A genuine slow-mode spectrum can only arise when the full high-dimensional collective dynamical state space is retained. In this work we show that infrared collective dynam-ics is generically controlled not by a single overdamped mode but by the time–scale density of states (TDOS) of correlated internal relaxation modes. By formulating a dynamical coarse–graining theory within the Martin– Siggia–Rose–Janssen–De Dominicis (MSRJD) framework [12–14], we derive an exact spectral representation of the collective susceptibility in terms of the TDOS. We demonstrate that a TDOS scaling ρ(λ) ∼ λα di-rectly classifies universality classes of quantum critical dynamics. While the conventional Hertz–Millis kernel emerges only for α = 1, a finite TDOS at the origin (α = 0) produces a marginal nonanalytic retarded kernel proportional to sgn( ω), corresponding in the time do-main to a universal long–memory response K(t) ∼ 1/t .This memory–dominated infrared dynamics represents a breakdown of Markovian overdamped criticality and de-fines a new universality class governed by self–organized slow modes rather than fast external baths. Crucially, we show that such flat relaxation mode spec-tra arise naturally in strongly correlated collective sys-tems, including self–organized dynamical manifolds ex-plored in our previous work [15–17]. The resulting long– memory kernel dynamically amplifies intrinsic electronic pairing tendencies, providing a natural route to strong superconductivity without invoking finely tuned bosonic mediators.

> IV. PAIRING FROM MEMORY–DOMINATED CRITICAL DYNAMICS

Rather than producing conventional overdamped fluctuations described by Ohmic Eliashberg dynamics, the accumu-lation of near–marginal relaxation rates generates long– time memory kernels and nonanalytic infrared response. Superconductivity in this setting emerges as a dynam-ical instability of the memory–dominated collective spec-trum. The Cooper channel is reorganized by the slow– mode reservoir into a relevant infrared interaction, funda-mentally distinct from marginal pairing mediated by fast bosonic modes. Quantum criticality thus acts not merely as a source of fluctuations but as a self–organized dynamical amplifier that simultaneously governs thermo-dynamic and dynamical phenomena in strongly corre-lated superconductors. This unified picture yields a concrete strategy for ex-perimental tests: systems that exhibit enhanced low– frequency TDOS weight (or equivalently, strong long– time correlations) should simultaneously display in-creased pairing susceptibility, elevated Tc, and reduced 9phase stiffness consistent with Uemura scaling. The re-sulting real-frequency spectral function generated by the memory-dominated self-energy, including its universal scaling form and infrared structure, is derived in Ap-pendix E. 

> VI. DISCUSSION

The central result of this work is that infrared col-lective dynamics in strongly correlated quantum matter is governed not by local Markovian dissipation but by the time–scale density of states of collective relaxation modes. Criticality corresponds to a reorganization of the entire dynamical spectrum, and universality classes are naturally classified by the low–energy scaling of the TDOS. In particular, a finite TDOS at vanishing relax-ation rate defines a memory–dominated infrared fixed point characterized by long–time kernels K(t) ∼ 1/t .This perspective clarifies the limited domain of validity of conventional coarse–grained field theories. Away from criticality, relaxation spectra are gapped in the infrared, leading to finite memory times and justifying reduction to local-in-time damping and a small number of collective order parameters. Near correlated critical regimes, how-ever, relaxation rates condense into a continuous spec-trum extending to zero. Internal modes cannot be inte-grated out: the full relaxation spectrum remains dynam-ically active at all time scales. The breakdown of Marko-vian coarse–graining is therefore a universal consequence of dynamical criticality rather than a model-dependent artifact. This observation explains why Hertz–Millis–type ap-proaches, which assume overdamped local dynamics of a small set of collective fields, cannot capture the infrared structure of strongly correlated critical matter. Criti-cality does not correspond to the softening of a single bosonic mode but to a spectral condensation of near– marginal collective degrees of freedom. The TDOS thus emerges as the natural organizing variable of infrared dy-namics, replacing phenomenological damping parameters and providing a microscopic foundation for anomalous relaxation and transport. A central physical consequence of this spectral reor-ganization is the qualitative enhancement of supercon-ducting instabilities. Rather than requiring finely tuned bosonic mediators, superconductivity emerges generi-cally from dynamical amplification of intrinsic electronic pairing tendencies by the slow–mode reservoir. The exponential suppression characteristic of weak-coupling BCS theory is replaced by algebraic scaling controlled by infrared spectral weight. A single infrared parameter, ρ0, governs thermody-namic and dynamical phenomena alike. The supercon-ducting dome reflects truncation of long–time memory away from criticality, Uemura scaling follows from the shared spectral origin of pairing and phase coherence, and anomalous normal-state dynamics arises from the same non-Markovian kernel. Quantum criticality there-fore acts as a self-organized dynamical amplifier that si-multaneously controls fluctuations, transport, and super-conducting order. Although electrons propagate as Bloch quasiparti-cles on a crystalline lattice, collective particle–hole and particle–particle excitations form extended many-body coordinates not constrained to lattice energy eigenstates. In strongly correlated regimes these collective modes are generically overdamped by the electronic continuum, ren-dering a relaxation-rate description more fundamental than an energy-eigenmode picture. While conventional lattice theories organize collective behavior in terms of Hamiltonian eigenmodes, the present framework shows that near criticality the relevant organizing structure is instead the dynamically self-organized spectrum of relax-ation rates. This relaxation–rate organization is not a phenomeno-logical assumption but a direct consequence of the fun-damental structure of nonequilibrium quantum and clas-sical dynamics. As shown in the Appendix F, both Liou-villian evolution in open quantum systems and general-ized memory–kernel dynamics naturally decompose into continua of decay modes characterized by complex re-laxation eigenvalues rather than by Hamiltonian energy eigenstates. Even in the absence of spatial degrees of freedom, the Laplace–domain representation of non-Markovian dy-namics generically yields continuous spectra of relaxation rates whenever long–time memory kernels are present. The emergence of a dense TDOS therefore reflects a uni-versal mathematical property of dynamical systems with extended temporal correlations, not a model-dependent artifact of coarse–graining. From this perspective, energy eigenmodes describe conservative reversible dynamics, while relaxation spec-tra encode the physically relevant irreversible orga-nization governing collective behavior near criticality. Strongly correlated quantum matter near dynamical crit-ical points is therefore more naturally classified by its relaxation-rate spectrum than by its Hamiltonian excita-tion spectrum. The TDOS framework introduced here thus elevates relaxation modes to the status of fundamental collec-tive coordinates, providing a principled dynamical basis for infrared universality, memory effects, and emergent macroscopic coherence. Away from criticality the dynamical spectrum col-lapses onto a trivial fast mode. Near memory-dominated criticality an extensive continuum of slow collective modes emerges and governs macroscopic coherence. Su-perconducting pairing in this regime corresponds to con-densation within a slow-mode reservoir rather than bind-ing mediated by a single sharp bosonic excitation. 10 This unified picture resolves long-standing puzzles of unconventional superconductivity, including the coexis-tence of strange-metal behavior with robust pairing and the apparent universality of dome-shaped phase diagrams across disparate materials. Rather than invoking com-peting orders or material-specific bosonic glue, these phe-nomena arise naturally from universal infrared spectral structure. More broadly, the TDOS framework establishes dy-namical spectral organization as a fundamental princi-ple of correlated quantum matter. Memory-dominated criticality represents a new universality class beyond tra-ditional dissipative field theories, with implications ex-tending to non-Fermi liquids, glassy dynamics, and self-organized critical systems. Future directions include quantitative determination of TDOS spectra in microscopic models and exper-iments, extension to nonequilibrium driven systems, and exploration of engineered platforms in which slow-mode reservoirs can be dynamically controlled. In this light, high-temperature superconductivity appears not as an anomaly requiring special microscopic ingredients, but as a generic consequence of infrared spectral self-organization in strongly correlated matter. 

Memory–dominated dynamics as a guiding principle for room-temperature superconductivity. Recent exper-imental efforts toward room-temperature superconduc-tivity have largely focused on engineering stronger mi-croscopic pairing interactions through pressure, lattice design, or exotic chemical compositions. Within the present framework, however, enhancing the bare pairing scale alone is insufficient. Our results show that the de-cisive control parameter for high transition temperatures is not the magnitude of an attractive interaction but the infrared dynamical organization of collective modes en-coded in the relaxation-rate density of states. A dense reservoir of near-marginal slow modes fun-damentally reorganizes the Cooper channel, converting moderate intrinsic electronic pairing into an algebraically amplified instability with Tc ∼ ρ0Epair . From this per-spective, many existing high-pressure hydride systems and correlated materials may fail to reach extreme tran-sition temperatures not because pairing is weak, but be-cause their collective spectra do not self-organize into a flat TDOS regime. The present theory therefore suggests a new design principle for superconductivity beyond material-specific glue engineering: the deliberate stabilization of dy-namical phases supporting extensive slow-mode con-tinua. Controlling electronic structure, frustration, di-mensionality, and feedback mechanisms to promote near-marginal collective dynamics may be as crucial as increas-ing microscopic attraction itself. Room-temperature su-perconductivity, in this view, corresponds to a dynamical phase characterized by maximal infrared memory rather than by extreme coupling strength. 

> VII. CONCLUSION

We have identified dynamical spectral organization as a fundamental principle governing infrared behavior in strongly correlated quantum matter. Formulating col-lective dynamics in terms of the time–scale density of states reveals that criticality is generically controlled not by local Markovian dissipation but by the accumulation of slow collective relaxation modes. A finite infrared TDOS defines a memory–dominated universality class characterized by long–time kernels 

K(t) ∼ 1/t and nonanalytic dynamical response. Within this regime, intrinsic electronic pairing tendencies are dy-namically amplified, converting the marginal instabilities of conventional theories into algebraic superconducting transitions governed by infrared spectral weight. This mechanism provides a unified explanation of key experimental phenomena in unconventional supercon-ductors, including superconducting domes, Uemura scal-ing of transition temperatures with superfluid stiffness, and anomalous long–time dynamical correlations in the normal state. These signatures arise generically from in-frared spectral reorganization, without invoking specific bosonic glue, competing orders, or fine tuning. More broadly, our results recast quantum criticality as a constructive dynamical process that enhances collective quantum phenomena through self–organized memory ef-fects. Memory–dominated criticality thus represents a new paradigm beyond traditional dissipative field theo-ries, with implications extending to non-Fermi liquids, glassy dynamics, and engineered quantum platforms. In this perspective, high–temperature superconductiv-ity emerges not as an exception requiring special mi-croscopic ingredients, but as a natural consequence of infrared spectral self–organization in strongly correlated matter. Future exploration of TDOS-controlled dynam-ics may enable systematic design of materials and de-vices that exploit memory–dominated criticality to sta-bilize and enhance collective quantum states.

> Appendix F: Relaxation-rate spectra as the fundamental dynamical basis of collective dynamics

In conventional quantum many-body theory, collective behavior is typically organized in terms of energy eigen-modes of an underlying Hamiltonian, 

H|n⟩ = En|n⟩, (S129) with dynamical response expressed through Fourier modes oscillating at frequencies set by energy differences. This structure naturally emphasizes stationary states and conservative dynamics, with dissipation treated as a secondary perturbative effect. However, this description represents a special limit cor-responding to closed, Hamiltonian systems. In general interacting and open systems — particularly near criti-cality — collective dynamics is governed not by energy eigenmodes but by the stability spectrum of the full dy-namical flow.
