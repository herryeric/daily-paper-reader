(Dated: February 24, 2026) The resistivity maximum at a temperature T = Tmax is a recurring feature of bandwidth-tuned Mott systems, yet its meaning remains controversial: is it a coherence-incoherence crossover of an electronically homogeneous metal, or does it mark the onset of transport through a mixed landscape of metallic and insulating regions? Even more debated is whether a true phase-coexistence regime survives in the relevant parameter range, or whether apparent inhomogeneity is merely extrinsic. Here we address these questions by moving beyond temperature sweeps and probe charge transport in the time domain. Near T = Tmax , we find that the resistance of a model system, a quasi-two-dimensional Mott spin liquid material, exhibits clear random-telegraph switching between discrete levels over long timescales. The statistics of the switching – sharp two-level behavior with thermally activated dwell times – point to a mesoscopic “current-controlling” region that dynamically toggles between metallic and insulating states, intermittently opening and closing the dominant conduction channel. This characteristic fluctuating dynamics provides direct evidence for intrinsic metal–insulator coexistence and establishes T ∼ Tmax as the regime of Mott intermittency, where transport is governed by stochastic domain switching rather than quasiparticle decoherence. 

The interaction-driven metal-insulator transition (MIT) [1] marks the boundary of the conventional metallic regime, where the fundamental nature of elementary excitations changes dramatically. While being recognized as one of the key open questions in the physics of electronic systems, the physical nature of the transition region has long remained the subject of controversy and debate [2]. New insight has started to emerge in recent years, with the rise of several exper-imental platforms that permit systematic and precise control of the approach to the transition point, as well as significant advances in the relevant characterization methods. This included studies of various high-mobility two-dimensional electron gases (2DEGs) in conventional semiconductors [3], organic spin-liquid materials [4], and more recently several transition-metal dichalcogenide moir´ e bilayers [5]. Remarkably, the qualitative transport signatures found in all these systems proved similar, despite the fact that their characteristic (Fermi) energies differ by orders of magnitude, hinting at a surprising degree of universality across all these platforms, with a robust underlying mechanism for the transition. In contrast, the spectrum of proposed theoretical sce-narios [2] remains surprisingly broad, which calls for more precise validation against experiments. In this regard, one prominent feature of transport stands out, which is the significant drop of the resistivity ( ρ) on the metal-lic side, below the resistivity maxima at temperature 

T = Tmax [3, 6, 7]. Remarkably, Tmax is found to uni-versally decrease towards the transition in all materials, although its origin is attributed to very different physi-cal processes within the theoretically proposed scenarios. For example, in the context of 2DEGs, it was attributed to a percolative mechanism [8] in analogy to colossal-magnetoresistance manganites [9], to competing “quan-tum corrections” within the weakly disordered Fermi liq-uid picture [10], or alternatively to the onset of quasipar-ticle coherence within the Wigner-Mott scenario [6, 11], in analogy to heavy fermion behavior [12]. Of course, these physical pictures dramatically differ in the assumed role of disorder and spatial inhomogeneity, an issue that is difficult to directly access using bulk probes. In this regard, quasi-2D organic spin-liquid materials [4] seem to provide new clues. In these systems, one finds both a uniform correlated phase further on the metallic side and, as demonstrated by dielectric measurements [13], a more spatially inhomogeneous phase coexistence region around the transition point. Remarkably, very similar resistivity maxima are found in both regimes, with Tmax decreasing towards the transition. This opens the possibility to conclusively pinpoint the relevant trans-port mechanism in each case. Time-resolved transport, i.e., resistance noise measurements provide a uniquely sensitive probe for this task. First, slow domain dy-namics are expected in the phase coexistence region of a first-order transition. Coexisting phases have nearly equal chemical potentials, allowing a domain to ther-mally fluctuate between the two phases, provided that 

T is sufficiently high to overcome the energy barrier. If the two phases possess distinct transport properties, as in the Mott MIT, domain fluctuations should manifest themselves as two-level switching (TLS) of resistance. Second, such a TLS event is sensitive to the percolation threshold. Away from the threshold, a global transport

is essentially determined by the percolation threshold [2]. Measurements were performed along the c-axis (the most conductive direction within the 2D plane [23]) of millimeter-sized plate-like single crystals of κ-STF x. The behavior of the 1 /f noise is discussed in more de-tail in the Supplemental Material [19]. Here we focus on RTN, which was observed in the T ranges highlighted in Fig. 1(c). For example, for x = 0.11 [Fig. 3(a)], some discrete resistance jumps appear at a few kelvins above Tmax , followed by the emergence of distinct TLS, at 21 .6 K [Fig. 3(b)] and below. Upon further cooling, another, “faster” TLS event appears between 17 .5 K and 12 .3 K, resulting in four discrete resistance states of the sample [Fig. 3(c)]. The TLS events occur less frequently at lower T [Fig. 3(d)], and almost disappear below 12 .3 K. The emergence of discrete states and their evolution with 

T can also be seen from the peaks in the histograms of resistance fluctuations [Fig. 3(e)]. Similar RTN has been observed from the x = 0 .12 sample [Figs. 3(f)–(h)]: well-defined TLS appears at 15 .6 K < T max ≈ 16 K, and its occurrence rate decreases on cooling. To test the effect of thermal cycling on RTN, we per-formed the following protocols. i) We heated the sam-ples only up to 10 K above their Tmax , and found that the RTN with the same statistical features, discussed below, reappeared at the same T . ii) In contrast, by warming up to 50 K above Tmax , we found that the RTN could assume quantitatively different statistics or even disappear. To illustrate, ten consecutive noise mea-surements were performed on the x = 0 .11 sample at 21 .5 K . Tmax ≈ 22 .3 K following the identical thermal-cycling protocol between 21 .5 K and 70 K. Well-defined RTN was observed in three of the ten runs (See Sup-plemental Material, Fig. S3 [19]). This stochastic reap-pearance of RTN after large thermal cycles is reminis-cent of the similar phenomenon in manganites [16]. In contrast, well-defined RTN was never observed from the 

x = 0 .16 sample near its Tmax [Fig. 3(i)], despite many repeated measurements following small or large thermal cycles (See Supplemental Material, Fig. S4 [19]). To understand its physical meaning, we perform sta-tistical analysis on the RTN near Tmax . In particular, we focus on noise in x = 0 .11 at T = 17 .5 K, which shows four states [marked as states #1–#4 in Fig 3(c)] arising from the superposition of two TLS systems: one switch-ing between states #1 and #2 (or states #3 and #4), and the other switching between states #1 and #3 (or states #2 and #4). The former has many more switching events to provide accurate statistics, and hence we refer to it as the “faster TLS”. We analyze its statistics by consider-ing states #1 and #3 as the higher resistance state (de-noted by ↑), and states #2 and #4 as the lower resistance state (denoted by ↓). (Analyzing the other TLS system in x = 0 .11 or that in x = 0 .12 yields qualitatively consis-tent statistics; see Supplemental Material, Fig. S5 [19].) The histograms of the dwell times τ↑ and τ↓ reveal an exponential distribution P (τi) = (1 /〈τi〉) exp ( −τi/〈τi〉), where P denotes the probability density, i =↑, ↓, and 

〈τi〉 is the mean dwell time [Fig. 4(a)]. From a statis-tical perspective, this exponential distribution immedi-ately suggests that the RTN arises from a memoryless process [24]. In addition, due to the presence of dis-crete states, the resistance noise exhibits a non-Gaussian amplitude distribution [Figs. 3(e) and (h)]. While non-Gaussian amplitude distributions can sometimes indicate a nonstationary process, we have analyzed higher-order statistics [25] of the noise and found no signature of non-stationarity (see Supplemental Material, Fig. S6 [19]). The TLS occurs less frequently at lower T , which can be quantitatively characterized by the T -dependence of the mean dwell time 〈τi〉. Taking the “faster TLS” of 

x = 0 .11 at T = 14 .4–17 .5 K as an example, Fig. 4(b) shows that, although limited in a T range, 〈τi〉 de-creases with T in a thermally activated manner, 〈τi〉 =

τ0,i exp ( Ei/k BT ), where τ0,i and Ei are the attempt times and energy barriers, respectively. The observed behavior is consistent with an asymmetrical TLS system [16, 26] where an energy barrier separates the two states with height Ei from each side [Fig. 1(a)], as expected for a domain fluctuating between two coexisting phases. The RTN observed near Tmax is, therefore, attributed to domains that switch back and forth between metal-lic and insulating states, driven by thermal fluctuations. Our results indicate that such domains are located within the dominant conduction channel [Fig. 1(a)], which forms the metallic percolating cluster once T decreases below 

Tmax . In other words, we find that the dynamics near the 4

In deuterated κ-(BEDT-TTF) 2-Cu[N(CN) 2]Br [27, 28], resistance noise suddenly slows down in the vicinity of the critical point of the Mott MIT, where the transition becomes continuous. The slowing down was naturally attributed to the criticality of the Mott transition and was believed to be univer-sal near a Mott critical point. However, this interpre-tation has been challenged by a very recent work [29], which finds such slow dynamics absent in pressurized κ-(BEDT-TTF) 2Cu[N(CN) 2 ]Cl. It is worth noting that in those two Mott organic systems, a glass-like freezing of the BEDT-TTF molecules’ terminal ethylene groups also contributes to the slow resistance noise [22], which can complicate the interpretation of the noise results. In the meantime, glassy electron kinetics probed near the first-order Mott MIT of Ruddlesden-Popper ruthenate Ca 3(Ru 0.9Ti 0.1)2O7 were ascribed to accompanying lat-tice distortion and magnetic defects [30]. By comparison, 

κ-STF x possesses genuine Mott transition without inter-ference from additional competing degrees of freedom. Our observation of stationary TLS thus points to an in-trinsic feature of the Mott MIT, in line with the theoret-ical prediction that Mott localization tends to suppress the glassy behavior of electrons [31]. By combining transport and noise spectroscopy across the Mott transition in κ-STF x, we identify time-domain signatures of dynamical phase coexistence. Our results demonstrate that strong electronic correlations, rather than static disorder, control the arrest of charge motion at the genuine Mott transition. We establish that the resistivity maximum at T = Tmax in bandwidth-tuned Mott systems marks a dynamical boundary reflecting real-time competition between metallic and insulating tendencies, rather than a mere crossover scale. Time-resolved transport reveals that conduction in this regime is governed by intermittency, rather than by a smooth, homogeneous loss of coherence. More broadly, our work elevates time-resolved transport to a powerful probe for identifying Mott phase competition and provides a uni-fied framework for interpreting the resistivity maxima in correlated materials.

x = 0 .16 (0 .04 × 1.0 × 1.4 mm 3) prepared by electrochem-ical oxidation [S1]. Here, BEDT-TTF and BEDT-STF stand for bis(ethylenedithio)tetrathiafulvalene and bis-(ethylenedithio)diseleniumdithiafulvalene, respectively. From BEDT-TTF to BEDT-STF, two S atoms in the inner ring are replaced by Se that have more extended orbitals. Such a substitution thus increases the band-width and tunes the system from a Mott insulator to-wards a Fermi liquid. Along the c-axis (the most con-ductive axis [S2]) of these samples, electrical contacts were made by the DOTITE XC-12 carbon paste con-nected to 25 μm-thick gold wires, with a typical distance of ∼ 0.4 mm between the voltage contacts. The experiments were conducted in 4He cryostats over the temperature ( T ) range 3–280 K for the x = 0 .11 and 0 .12 samples, and 5–200 K for the x = 0 .16 sam-ple. Transport and noise were measured with a Lakeshore 372 AC resistance bridge or Signal Recovery 7265 lock-in amplifier and LI-75A voltage preamplifier using the standard four-probe ac technique (typically ∼ 17 Hz), except that the noise data on x = 0 .11 were acquired using a five-probe setup [S3] to minimize the effects of T

fluctuations, contact noise, and excitation source fluctu-ations. Both the resistance bridge and the lock-in ampli-fier measured the in-phase and out-of-phase components of the signal simultaneously. The power spectrum of the out-of-phase component, which contains only the back-ground noise including instrumental and environmental contributions, was consistently several orders of magni-tude lower than the power spectrum of the in-phase com-ponent. When RTN was observed in the in-phase com-ponent, the out-of-phase component did not show TLS. Since the in-phase component of the signal contains both sample resistance noise and background, the background contribution was nevertheless subtracted from the power spectrum of the in-phase component to obtain the re-sistance noise power spectrum. To avoid aliasing, time constants of the instruments were set to 0 .5 s or 1 s, while the data were collected at a time interval of 0 .2 s or 0 .5 s. At each measured T , excitation currents ranging from 50 nA to 100 μA were employed for the noise mea-surements and yielded quantitatively consistent results. This consistency ruled out heating effects and contact-noise contributions, as both are expected to depend on the excitation current. In parallel with transport and noise measurements, sample temperatures were monitored with a Lakeshore 336 temperature controller and a Cernox thermometer placed a few mm below the sample. Typically, the stan-dard deviation of T fluctuations was about 1 mK for 

T ≤ 100 K, and a few mK for T > 100 K. Tempera-ture never exhibited any two-level switching, and thus it did not account for the TLS observed in the resistance of the samples. To quantify the effect of T fluctuations on the measured resistance fluctuations, we calculated the power spectral density ST of T fluctuations and the resulting resistance fluctuations ST →R = ST (d〈R〉/dT )2.By comparing ST →R and the measured power spectral density of the resistance fluctuations SR, we found ST →R

to be at least one order of magnitude lower than SR for most of the data. We also confirmed that the correla-tions between the measured T and R fluctuations were negligible. Any measurements in which T fluctuations gave a comparable or larger contribution than the intrin-sic 1 /f sample noise, such as for x = 0 .12 near its Tmax 

where dR/dT is high, have been excluded from the anal-ysis (see, e.g., Fig. 2). We note, however, that TLS from the x = 0 .12 sample [Fig. 3(f)] cannot be attributed to 

T fluctuations, as discussed above.  

> 1/f NOISE

Some representative 1/f noise data are shown in Fig. S1. Here we discuss the evolution of the noise magnitude, SR/〈R〉2 at a fixed f , with T (Fig. 2). At 

T /T max > 1, the samples are either in or approach-ing an insulating state (the Mott insulator or the inco-herent semiconductor state, which are separated by the quantum Widom line [S4]). In this regime, the simplest possible scenario for the origin of the observed Gaus-sian 1 /f noise is that each individual charge carrier acts as an independent fluctuator. As the density of free charge carriers decreases exponentially on lowering T , an enhanced SR/〈R〉2 is expected from Hooge’s law [S5]. At T /T max < 1, where all samples are in the phase-coexistence regime, the noise magnitude for x = 0 .11 demonstrates an exponential growth upon cooling. Asimilar but weaker trend is seen for x = 0 .12, while for 

x = 0 .16, the noise magnitude no longer depends on T .Although developing a precise theoretical model to de-scribe the origin of 1 /f noise is beyond the scope of this work, our results clearly show (Fig. 2) that the noise is strongly enhanced in the regime of phase coexistence (T /T max < 1) compared to that at higher T .8
