# Memory-Dominated Quantum Criticality as a Universal Route to High-Temperature Superconductivity 

Byung Gyu Chae 1

> 1

Understanding the dynamical origin of high–temperature superconductivity remains a central challenge in strongly correlated quantum matter. Conventional treatments of quantum criticality assume overdamped Markovian dissipation governed by Ohmic Landau damping of a small num-ber of collective modes. Here we show that infrared dynamics is instead controlled more generally by the relaxation-rate spectrum of the underlying dissipative many-body evolution. Introducing the time–scale density of states (TDOS) of collective decay modes, we derive within the Martin– Siggia–Rose–Janssen–De Dominicis framework an exact spectral representation of the collective susceptibility in terms of this relaxation spectrum. We demonstrate that the low– λ scaling of the relaxation–rate spectrum directly classifies dynamical universality classes. In particular, a finite TDOS at vanishing relaxation rate defines a memory–dominated critical regime characterized by long–time kernels K(t) ∼ 1/t and nonanalytic dynamical response. In this regime, intrinsic elec-tronic pairing tendencies are dynamically amplified: the marginal logarithmic growth of BCS and Eliashberg theories is replaced by algebraic enhancement governed by the infrared spectral weight of slow modes. As a consequence, the superconducting transition temperature scales proportionally to the low–energy TDOS weight, naturally generating superconducting domes and Uemura scal-ing without invoking material-specific bosonic glue or fine tuning. The same relaxation spectrum controls anomalous normal-state dynamics, including long–time correlations and strange-metal be-havior, providing a unified spectral framework for both thermodynamic and dynamical phenomena in correlated superconductors. These results identify dynamical spectral organization of relaxation modes as a fundamental organizing principle of quantum critical matter and establish memory– dominated criticality as a generic mechanism for enhanced superconductivity. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

High–temperature superconductivity remains one of the central unsolved problems of strongly correlated quantum matter [1, 2]. Despite decades of intensive theoretical and experimental effort, no consensus has emerged on the microscopic mechanism responsible for robust pairing far above conventional phonon scales. A central difficulty lies in the intimate proximity of super-conductivity to quantum critical regimes, where strong collective fluctuations dominate low–energy dynamics. Within the prevailing theoretical paradigm, quantum critical fluctuations are assumed to be overdamped by fast electronic continua [3, 4]. This framework, formal-ized in Hertz–Millis theory, predicts a universal Ohmic Landau damping kernel of the form |ω| governing collec-tive dynamics. While remarkably successful in weakly correlated metals, this assumption has increasingly come into tension with experiments in strongly correlated sys-tems, including cuprate superconductors and strange metals, where anomalous temporal correlations, non-Markovian response, and 1 /f -like noise are ubiquitously observed [5–9]. These observations raise a fundamental question: Is overdamped Markovian critical dynamics truly universal, or does it rely on implicit assumptions about the struc-ture of dynamical spectra that break down in strongly correlated regimes? A key limitation of conventional approaches is their formulation in terms of a small number of collective order-parameter fields [10, 11]. Such descriptions implic-itly assume that the infrared behavior can be captured by a finite set of soft bosonic modes with local-in-time damping. However, strongly correlated quantum mat-ter is fundamentally governed by Liouvillian evolution of the density matrix, where the full spectrum of relaxation eigenvalues determines long-time dynamics [12–14]. In more general, criticality need not correspond to soften-ing of a single excitation mode but may instead involve reorganization of the entire relaxation-rate spectrum. Rather than reducing the dynamics to a few collec-tive coordinates, one must consider the possibility that the Liouvillian spectrum develops an extended contin-uum of near-marginal decay modes. Such spectral ac-cumulation naturally generates a broad distribution of relaxation time scales and invalidates the assumption of Markovian coarse-graining near criticality. The relevant organizing structure of the infrared theory is then not a small set of overdamped bosons, but the time–scale den-sity of states (TDOS) of collective relaxation modes. In this work we show that infrared collective dynamics is generically controlled by the TDOS of correlated inter-nal decay channels. By formulating a dynamical coarse– graining theory within the Martin–Siggia–Rose–Janssen– De Dominicis (MSRJD) framework [15–17], we derive an exact spectral representation of the collective suscepti-

> VI. DISCUSSION

The central result of this work is that infrared col-lective dynamics in strongly correlated quantum matter is organized by the structure of its relaxation-rate spec-trum rather than solely by softening of a single order parameter. The time–scale density of states provides a quantitative measure of this organization. When the TDOS remains finite as λ → 0, the system enters a memory–dominated regime characterized by long–time kernels K(t) ∼ 1/t and nonanalytic infrared response. This viewpoint clarifies the limits of conventional coarse-grained field theories. Away from criticality, the relaxation spectrum is gapped in the infrared, lead-ing to finite memory times and justifying reduction to local-in-time damping and a small number of collective fields. Near correlated critical regimes, however, relax-ation rates condense into a continuous spectrum extend-ing toward zero. In this regime, internal modes cannot be integrated out: the full decay spectrum remains dynami-cally active across time scales. The breakdown of Marko-vian coarse-graining is therefore not a model-dependent artifact but a universal consequence of dynamical criti-cality. This observation highlights the structural difference from Hertz–Millis–type approaches. Conventional quan-tum critical theories assume overdamped local dynamics of a small set of bosonic modes, with dissipation con-trolled by Landau damping. In contrast, the present framework allows for spectral condensation of near-marginal relaxation channels. Criticality is reinterpreted not as single-mode softening but as reorganization of the 10 entire decay spectrum. The TDOS thus replaces phe-nomenological damping coefficients with a microscopic spectral quantity governing infrared universality. A direct physical consequence of this spectral reorga-nization is qualitative enhancement of superconducting instabilities. Superconductivity need not rely on finely tuned bosonic mediators. Instead, intrinsic short-range electronic pairing tendencies are dynamically amplified by the slow-mode reservoir. The exponential suppression characteristic of weak-coupling BCS theory is replaced by algebraic scaling controlled by infrared spectral weight. In this sense, superconducting order corresponds to con-densation within an extended slow-mode manifold rather than binding via a sharp bosonic excitation. A single infrared parameter, ρ0, governs both thermo-dynamic and dynamical phenomena. The superconduct-ing dome reflects truncation of long-time memory away from the optimal spectral reorganization point; Uemura scaling follows from the shared spectral origin of pairing and phase coherence; anomalous normal-state dynamics arises from the same non-Markovian kernel. Quantum criticality therefore acts as a dynamical amplifier linking transport, coherence, and pairing within a unified spec-tral framework. The relaxation-rate organization emphasized here is not merely a phenomenological assumption. Both Liou-villian evolution in open quantum systems and general-ized memory-kernel dynamics naturally decompose into continua of decay modes characterized by complex re-laxation eigenvalues. Whenever long-time correlations are present, Laplace-domain representations generically produce continuous relaxation spectra. A dense TDOS therefore reflects a universal mathematical property of dynamical systems with extended temporal correlations, rather than a model-specific construction. From this perspective, Hamiltonian energy eigenmodes describe conservative reversible dynamics, whereas relax-ation spectra encode the irreversible organization gov-erning collective behavior near criticality. Strongly cor-related quantum matter near dynamical critical points may thus be more naturally classified by its relaxation-rate spectrum than by its excitation spectrum alone. More broadly, the TDOS framework elevates dynam-ical spectral organization to a fundamental organiz-ing principle of correlated quantum matter. Memory-dominated criticality represents a universality class dis-tinct from traditional dissipative field theories, with po-tential implications for non-Fermi liquids, glassy systems, and self-organized critical dynamics. 

Memory–dominated dynamics as a guiding principle for room-temperature superconductivity. Most contempo-rary efforts toward room-temperature superconductivity focus on strengthening microscopic pairing interactions through pressure, lattice engineering, or exotic chemical composition. Within the present framework, however, enhancing the bare pairing scale alone is insufficient. The decisive control parameter for high transition tempera-tures is the infrared dynamical organization of collective modes encoded in the relaxation-rate density of states. A dense reservoir of near-marginal slow modes reorga-nizes the Cooper channel, converting moderate intrinsic electronic pairing into an algebraically amplified insta-bility with Tc ∼ ρ0Epair . From this viewpoint, certain high-pressure hydrides or correlated materials may fail to reach extreme transition temperatures not because pair-ing is weak, but because their collective spectra do not self-organize into a flat TDOS regime. The present theory therefore suggests a comple-mentary design principle for superconductivity beyond material-specific glue engineering: stabilization of dy-namical phases that support extensive slow-mode con-tinua [27]. Control of electronic frustration, dimension-ality, feedback mechanisms, or proximity to dynamical criticality may be as crucial as increasing microscopic attraction itself. Room-temperature superconductivity, in this perspective, corresponds to a dynamical phase characterized by maximal infrared memory rather than simply by extreme coupling strength. 

> VII. CONCLUSION

We have identified dynamical spectral organization as a fundamental principle governing infrared behavior in strongly correlated quantum matter. By formulating col-lective dynamics in terms of the time–scale density of states, we show that criticality is generically controlled not by local Markovian dissipation but by the accumula-tion of slow collective relaxation modes. A finite infrared TDOS defines a memory–dominated universality class characterized by long–time kernels 

K(t) ∼ 1/t and nonanalytic dynamical response. Within this regime, intrinsic electronic pairing tendencies are dy-namically amplified, converting marginal logarithmic in-stabilities into algebraic superconducting transitions gov-erned by infrared spectral weight. This mechanism provides a unified account of key ex-perimental phenomena in unconventional superconduc-tors, including superconducting domes, Uemura scaling of transition temperatures with superfluid stiffness, and anomalous long–time dynamical correlations in the nor-mal state. These signatures arise naturally from in-frared spectral reorganization, without requiring specific bosonic mediators or material-dependent fine tuning. More broadly, the results recast quantum criticality as a dynamical process that enhances collective quan-tum phenomena through extended temporal correlations. Memory–dominated criticality therefore represents a uni-versality class distinct from traditional dissipative field theories, with implications for non-Fermi liquids, glassy dynamics, and engineered quantum platforms. 11 In this perspective, high–temperature superconductiv-ity appears as a generic consequence of infrared spectral organization in strongly correlated matter. Systematic exploration of TDOS-controlled dynamics may provide a pathway toward stabilizing and enhancing collective quantum states in future materials and devices.
