## Pseudogapped Fermi liquids from emergent quasiparticles 

Andreas Gleis 1, ∗ and Gabriel Kotliar 1, 2, †

> 1

Department of Physics and Astronomy, Rutgers University, Piscataway, NJ 08854, USA 

> 2

Condensed Matter Physics and Materials Science Department, Brookhaven National Laboratory, Upton, NY 11973, USA 

(Dated: March 17, 2026) We propose an interacting model that is exactly solvable in any spatial dimension and gives rise to a Fermi liquid (FL) featuring a pseudogapped (PG) single-particle spectral function and a vanishing quasiparticle (QP) weight at half-filling, without invoking Mott physics. The PG originates from a purely fermionic mechanism through emergent QPs arising from a correlated hopping interaction. By employing an appropriate coherent-state basis, we derive a Gaussian path-integral representation of the partition function, which enables systematic treatments of deviations from the Gaussian limit using standard many-body techniques, such as diagrammatic perturbation theory or mean-field theory. We explicitly demonstrate and discuss several properties of the exactly solvable limit on the square lattice, including the mechanism for temperature-dependent PG opening, the singular behavior of the self-energy, the violation of the Luttinger sum rule, and the role of Luttinger and Fermi surfaces. Finally, we explore quantum phase transitions between PG-FLs and Landau FLs. 

Pseudogapped (PG) metals represent one of the most longstanding unresolved mysteries in strongly-correlated electron physics. While most famously observed in cuprates [ 1– 8], they also appear, for instance, in nick-elates [ 9], iridates [ 10 , 11 ], and magic-angle twisted bi-layer graphene [ 12 ]. In contrast to conventional Landau Fermi liquids (FL) — where electron spectroscopy re-veals a large electronic density of states near the Fermi level — PG metals exhibit a depression of the electronic density of states at the Fermi level, which becomes in-creasingly prominent at lower temperatures. Furthermore, while quantum oscillations [ 13 ] in PG metals detect closed Fermi surfaces (FS), electronic spectroscopy typically only observes disconnected Fermi arcs [ 14 – 17 ]. This dichotomy suggests that quasiparticles (QP) are “hidden” along seg-ments of the FS. Apart from the previously mentioned bulk systems, local PG metals with hidden QP physics also emerge in certain impurity systems, which are ex-perimentally realizable [ 18 – 21 ] and amenable to highly controlled theoretical analysis [22–31]. Several mechanisms for the depletion of low-energy spectral weight have been proposed. Those invoke either fluctuating order [ 32 – 39 ] or Mott physics [ 40 – 51 ]. In the latter, the FS is reconstructed through a self-energy pole that has a hidden fermion interpretation [ 52 –56 ]. Recently, it has also been argued that Luttinger surfaces (LS) [ 57 ], which are inherently present in PG metals, can host hid-den QPs [ 58 – 60 ]. Overall, the precise origin of PG metals remains unsettled and may differ depending on the specific physical context. In this letter, we propose an interacting model that realizes hidden QPs and depletion of low-energy spec-tral weight through an intrinsically fermionic mechanism, without invoking Mott physics. In our model, which is ex-actly solvable in arbitrary spatial dimensions, a correlated hopping (CH) interaction gives rise to new emergent QPs, and phase space constraints subsequently produce a PG in the electronic spectral function. A Gaussian path-integral construction demonstrates the stability of our PG-FL fixed point and provides a framework for studies beyond the exactly solvable limit [ 61 , 62 ]. Further, our model generically exhibits LSs, whose physical role we examine. Finally, we discuss quantum phase transitions separating PG-FLs from Landau FLs across different dimensions.

Outlook.— Interesting future avenues include exploring 

q-particle interactions and symmetry breaking out of PG-FLs, particularly superconductivity. It also remains to be seen under what circumstances q-particles emerge in microscopic settings lacking explicit CH, and whether they are present in known PG metals. As a first step, the “End Matter” confirms a q-particle resonance within the PG phase of the two-impurity Anderson model [52]. Finally, we conjecture that q-particles may play a role in Mott PG metals, which phenomenologically arise via the hybridization of electrons with an emergent fermion [ 50 –56 ]. Through equations of motion, this emergent fermion can be related to the q-particle in Hubbard models [ 52 ], suggesting a significant q-particle character along hidden FS segments. If present, such deconfined q-particles could explain some of the spectral features observed in Mott PG metals. B. Further results on the impurity model 

In this section, we show that the impurity model in the main text exhibits two-channel Kondo behavior [ 113 – 118 ]at the quantum critical point, where Vc = Vq . For that, we computed both the spin and charge susceptibility at the impurity χspin (ω) and χcharge (ω), respectively. Their spec-tra at T = 10 −8 are shown in Fig. S3(a). They both fit the phenomenological ∝ tanh ω 

> 2T

form, typical for two-channel Kondo behavior. Note that χspin (ω) = 14 χcharge (ω), be-cause there is no local Hubbard interaction and the sys-tem is both particle-hole and spin symmetric. As a result, charge and spin can be interchanged. Figure S3(b) shows that −Im Σ( ω) = −Im Σ(0) + bp|ω|, which is also typi-cal two-channel Kondo behavior. Finally, Fig. S4 shows that the impurity contribution to the entropy approaches log √2 at T → 0, another hallmark signature of the two-channel Kondo model. limit, while keeping Vx/U 2 constant, this impurity model becomes a two-channel Kondo model.
