Abstract 

The new family of superconducting nickelates centered around La 3Ni 2O7 possesses attractive features, such as the high transition temperature and the presence of an antiferromagnetic ground state at ambient pressure, suggesting an unconventional pairing mechanism. In the nonsupercon-ducting state, the possibility of different density-wave orders with opposite pressure dependencies is discussed, whose relationships and microscopic origins are largely unknown. However, sample-quality issues, such as impurity-phase formation or oxygen vacancies, impede the progress in the field. Here, we employ high-pressure synthesis and hydrostatic high-pressure transport techniques to investigate bilayer nickelates with controlled band width and filling, and perform a system-atic study on their impact on the superconductivity and other characteristic properties. While increasing the tilting of the NiO 6 octahedra shifts the superconducting phase to higher pressure, simultaneous hole doping reverts this trend. We also observe up to three distinct anomalies in the nonsuperconducting state which are possibly related to density-wave formation. 

Keywords: nickelates; unconventional superconductivity; high-pressure synthesis; onset-pressure control

> arXiv:2604.13875v1 [cond-mat.supr-con] 15 Apr 2026 I. INTRODUCTION

The recent discovery of bulk superconductivity in the bilayer nickelate La 3Ni 2O7 has established a new research field and stimulated tremendous research activity since its su-perconducting onset temperature under pressure exceeds the boiling point of liquid nitro-gen [1–6]. La 3Ni 2O7 belongs to a class of materials called Ruddlesden-Popper (RP) se-ries, Rn+1 MnO3n+1 , and is composed of double layers ( n = 2) of NiO 6 octahedra. Various transition-metal oxides M with the RP structure have been intensively studied as strongly correlated electron systems, particularly in connection with cuprate superconductors, since the 1980s. RP series are well known to offer an ideal platform to control the band width and the band filling independently without disturbing the M – O network by partially changing the R site with an ion with different ionic radius and different valence state, respectively [7]. Extensive investigations after the discovery of superconductivity in La 3Ni 2O7 have re-vealed that it undergoes structural transitions, as a function of pressure, from the slightly distorted orthorhombic Amam structure at ambient pressure [Fig. 1(a)] to also orthorhom-bic F mmm and eventually to tetragonal I4/mmm structure. The main difference between 

Amam and F mmm is that in the former the NiO 6 octahedra are tilted from the c axis and are aligned in the latter. This alignment is considered to be a key prerequesite for the emergence of superconductivity [1, 8–10]. In the low-pressure region of the phase diagram, La 3Ni 2O7 exhibits also intriguing features: Charge- and spin-density-wave (CDW and SDW) ordered states with different temperature and pressure dependences are discussed [11–20], although their interplay and microscopic origins remain mostly unresolved. There are also various theoretical proposals to explain the superconductivity in La 3Ni 2O7

with the anticipated antiferromagnetic ground state [20, 21], pointing toward an unconven-tional pairing mechanism. Currently, many studies suggest favorable s±-wave pairing, e.g., [10, 22–26], while others also consider d-wave pairing, e.g., [27–30]. Relevant to the present work is the prediction that partial substitution of La with Nd may enhance Tc [31]. Such attempts to partially replace La with divalent Sr or isovalent Pr, Sm, and recently Nd, have been reported. In pristine La 3Ni 2O7, the nominal Ni valence state is 2 .5+, and hence, the electron configuration is 3 d7.5. Figure 1(b) depicts orbital levels for a single La 3Ni 2O7 formula unit with large bonding-antibonding splitting of the d3z2−r2 orbitals and small splitting of the dx2−y2 orbitals while both exhibit a large dispersion when hybridization 2with the surroundings is further taken into account. Three electrons are accommodated into these four orbitals, and Sr substitution for La dopes a hole into the Ni site. On the other hand, partial substitution of La with smaller and isovalent rare-earth ions increases the lattice distortion, and hence, decreases the bonding-antibonding splitting as well as the hybridization with the surroundings. While only semiconducting behavior is found in La 2.8Sr 0.2Ni 2O7 [32], the rare-earth-substituted nickelates exhibit superconductivity [33–35]. Despite these efforts, problems with the reproducibility of experimental results persist, leading to uncertainties and apparent inconsistencies regarding the intrinsic nature of the superconductivity and the nonsuperconducting state from which it emerges. This is reflected, e.g., in wide ranges of the superconducting onset temperature T onset c , the zero-resistance temperature Tc, and the critical pressure pc required to induce a zero-resistance state [1, 2, 4, 5, 33, 34, 36, 37], which is mainly due to the formation of different RP impurity nickelate phase intergrowths and oxygen deficiency, e.g., [21, 38]. Here, we systematically investigate the different effects on the superconducting and nonsuperconducting phases when changing the band width and/or the band filling in La 2−xRSr xNi 2O7 with R = La ( x = 0, 0.1) and R = Nd ( x = 0, 0.1, 0.2) grown by a high-pressure synthesis method. The phase diagram of La 3Ni 2O7 is reproduced, and su-perconductivity is not observed in hole-doped La 2.9Sr 0.1Ni 2O7. As for La 2NdNi 2O7 with an enhanced tilting of the NiO 6 octahedra, or a reduced band width, pc is shifted to higher pressure p than in La 3Ni 2O7. Hole doping into La 2NdNi 2O7 by Sr ( x = 0 .1) decreases pc

again. Slightly larger x = 0 .2 does not yield bulk superconductivity any more. In the low-pressure region of the phase diagrams, we observe up to three distinct anomalies evolving with p that are possibly related to density-wave instabilities as discussed in the literature.  

> II. RESULTS

Figures 1(c) – (g) display scanning transmission electron microscopy results on La 2NdNi 2O7,revealing a perfect atomic arrangement of the n = 2 RP structure without indications of stacking faults or intergrowths of other RP series compositions. Moreover, the intensities of the La and Nd spots in panels (e) and (f) suggest that Nd is preferentially replacing La at the R(2) crystallographic site [shown in Fig. 1(a)] in the rock-salt blocks between the NiO 2

bilayers: The atomic ratios La : Nd are (78 .8 ± 0.8) : (21 .2 ± 0.8) and (61 .0 ± 0.6) : (39 .0 ± 0.6) 3for the R(1) and R(2) sites, respectively [see Section S4 in the accompanying Supporting Information (SI) [39] for the details and additional images]. This result is qualitatively consistent with the case of R = Sm [34]. Figure 2 summarizes temperature-dependent resistivity ρ(T ) data from ambient p to 20 GPa of (a) La 3Ni 2O7, (b) La 2NdNi 2O7, and (c) La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7. Enlarged views of the raw data are summarized in Section S5 in [39], including respective data for La 2.9Sr 0.1Ni 2O7

and La 1.8NdSr 0.2Ni 2O7. As shown in Fig. 2(a), at ambient p, ρ(T ) of La 3Ni 2O7 exhibits a metallic dependence except toward very low T . As a function of p, ρ is suppressed and remains metallic up to 8 GPa. At 10 GPa, ρ(T ) exhibits a semiconducting upturn for 

T < 100 K and intersects with the 8-GPa data, highlighting a major change in the transport behavior at low T (cf. also Section S7 in [39]). There is also a discernible drop in ρ(T ) below 

T onset c ≲ 14 K, indicating the emergence of superconductivity. As p increases, ρ(T ) is further suppressed, T onset c is enhanced, and zero resistance is achieved at 16 GPa, with Tc reaching 52 K at 20 GPa. In the nonsuperconducting state, three types of anomalies are observed in ρ(T ) data (see also Supplementary Fig. S6 in [39]): At ambient p, there is a wavy feature around 110 K. At 3 and 5 GPa, this feature is difficult to recognize in ρ(T ), but still discernible in the temperature derivatives dρ/dT (not shown). It shifts to lower T with p and is no longer detectable for p ≥ 8 GPa. A distinct anomaly develops for p ≥ 3 GPa at temperatures 

T > 150 K. Initially, it appears as a shoulder or slope change in ρ(T ), develops into a maximum for 12 GPa ≤ p ≤ 16 GPa, and fades out above. Consequently, the metallic T

dependence for T > T onset c changes into semiconducting behavior above the maxima. Due to the nonmonotonic evolution of ρ(T ) with p, the 10-GPa data exhibits a minimum as third type of anomaly around ∼ 75 K. For La 2NdNi 2O7, the pressure dependence of ρ(T ) shown in Fig. 2(b) differs from that of La 3Ni 2O7. At ambient p, there is already a broad maximum around ∼ 230 K with a metallic 

T dependence below, but the absolute value is large ( > 10 −1 Ωcm). At 3 GPa, ρ(T ) exhibits a semiconducting T dependence and exceeds the ambient-p data below ∼ 260 K. Upon further increasing p, ρ(T ) is strongly suppressed but retains a semiconducting T dependence. Metallic conduction and an onset of superconductivity are observed at 20 GPa, although a few μΩcm remain at 4.2 K in spite of the comparatively sharp drop of the resistivity. La 2NdNi 2O7 exhibits only two notable features in the nonsuperconducting state (see also 4Fig. S8 in [39]): At ambient p, a wavy anomaly appears at ∼ 130 K. At 3 GPa, this weakens but is still discernible in dρ/dT (not shown). Another anomaly is observed at T > 150 K in the intermediate p range, albeit less pronounced than in La 3Ni 2O7.Figure 2(c) summarizes respective data on La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7. As in La 3Ni 2O7, the resis-tivity is suppressed as p increases. However, closer inspection of the data reveals noteworthy differences: At ambient p, La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7 exhibits a semiconducting T dependence in the entire T range. This changes drastically at finite p. For 3 GPa ≤ p ≤ 12 GPa, ρ(T ) exhibits a semiconductor-like T dependence at low- and high-T , and metallic behavior in between, forming pronounced minima and maxima. At p = 14 GPa, a sharp suppression of the resis-tivity is observed below T onset c ≈ 20 K, indicating the emergence of superconductivity. Zero resistance is already achieved at slightly higher p = 15 GPa. Upon further increasing p,

T onset c and Tc enhance, with Tc reaching 43 K at 20 GPa. The transition width T onset c − Tc is almost constant as a function of p, suggesting a rather homogenous sample quality as well as a highly hydrostatic nature of the pressure used in the resistivity measurements. Moreover, the T dependence of the resistivity for p ≥ 14 GPa is metallic above T onset c , highlighting a p-induced semiconductor-to-metal transition when traversing from 12 GPa to 14 GPa at low T . This transition occurs at around ρ ∼ 1 mΩcm, close to the Ioffe-Regel limit for the resistivity in this system. As La 3Ni 2O7, La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7 exhibits three distinct types of anomalies in the non-superconducting state (see also Fig. S9 in [39]): However, no wavy feature is discernible and an anomaly is barely observed only in dρ/dT at ∼ 93 K in the ambient-p data, but not at finite p. The pronounced maxima and minima in the data for 3 GPa ≤ p ≤ 12 GPa appear at T > 150 K and T < 110 K, respectively. While the maxima shift to higher T with p, the minima exhibit an opposite p dependence. Figure 3 presents T − p phase diagrams for (a) La 3Ni 2O7, (b) La 2NdNi 2O7, and (c) La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7. The background color in each panel reflects the absolute value of the respective resistivity ρ(T, p ). The latter allows an easy assessment of the differences in the evolution leading to the emergence of superconductivity: In La 3Ni 2O7, the resistivity drops to a few mΩcm (orange-red shading in Fig. 3) only for p > p c in a small T range close to Tc.In La 2NdNi 2O7, the conductivity becomes worse, and the introduction of Nd requires higher pressure to suppress ρ(T ) and induce superconductivity. By contrast, La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7

exhibits a sub-mΩcm resistivity for p ≥ 14 GPa in the entire T range. 5The p dependences of the characteristic anomalies in the nonsuperconducting state are also plotted in each panel: (i) All three compositions exhibit an anomaly in dρ/dT below 150 K, which is suppressed with increasing p until the first indication of superconductivity is observed. This suggests that its origin competes with the superconductivity. We refer to this type of anomaly as T1

for all three compositions, owing to the similar T range in which it appears and its common negative p dependence. For La 3Ni 2O7, this anomaly has been associated in the literature with a density-wave (DW) instability, sometimes more specifically with a CDW transition [3, 11, 20, 21], suggesting that this is also the case in La 2−xNdSr xNi 2O7.(ii) Another common feature among the three compositions is observed above 150 K. It manifests itself in ρ(T ) as shoulders or pronounced maxima in the intermediate p range for La 3Ni 2O7 and La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7, but appears rather weak in La 2NdNi 2O7. In contrast to the T1 feature, this type of anomaly is enhanced upon increasing pressure until it starts to fade out. For La 3Ni 2O7, it is traceable up to the pressure range where zero resistance is observed, while it vanishes for the two other compositions before the first indication of superconductivity appears. This suggests a competition between its origin and robust superconductivity. Again, on the basis of the similar T range and p dependence, we denote this feature as T2 in all compositions. It remains unclear, however, how the T2 anomalies in Fig. 3 are connected to ambient p. According to Ref. [20], there is a SDW instability in La 3Ni 2O7 at ambient p around 150 K which is enhanced as a function of p at least up to 

∼ 2.3 GPa. This anomaly appears to extrapolate into the respective T2 feature shown in Fig 3(a). Hence, the T2 anomaly observed here may be associated with SDW formation. (iii) In the case of La 3Ni 2O7, there is a minimum in ρ as a function of T at 10 GPa. In La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7, a series of pronounced minima is observed in the same pressure range as T2. The difference is that this feature, labeled T3 in Fig. 3, shifts to lower T with p.However, there is no clear indication that it is related to T1, and the origin of the T3 feature remains unclear at present.  

> III. DISCUSSION

Finally, we discuss and compare the results obtained on La 2−xRSr xNi 2O7, on the basis of Fig. 4 which summarizes selected characteristic quantities as a function of the Sr concen-6tration x, including results on nonsuperconducting La 2.9Sr 0.1Ni 2O7 and La 1.8NdSr 0.2Ni 2O7.In Fig. 4(a), the absolute values of the resistivity ρ10K at T = 10 K and ambient pressure are shown to quantify the impact of the different compositions on the ground-state conduct-ing properties [A comparative plot of the ρ(T ) data for all five compositions at ambient p is shown and discussed in Section S6 in [39]]. La 3Ni 2O7 exhibits the smallest ρ10K in agreement with the metallic ρ(T ) dependence at ambient p. The introduction of smaller isovalent Nd 3+ 

is expected to keep the carrier count unchanged, while increasing the lattice distortion and reducing the band width in La 2NdNi 2O7. Hence, ρ(T ) should increase, as it is observed: 

ρ10K is enhanced by approximately one order of magnitude, and ρ(T ) increases with T only up to ∼ 230 K, cf. Figs. 2(a) and (b). Introducing divalent Sr 2+ aims for doping holes and possibly increase the conductivity. In as-grown La 2.9Sr 0.1Ni 2O7, however, we observe a strongly enhanced ρ(T ) upon cooling (not shown). This could imply that Sr is not properly incorporated into La 3Ni 2O7, but this is unlikely, given the observed changes in the lattice constants (Fig. S2 in [39]). Another possibility to explain the large ρ(T ) is oxygen deficiency. This reduces the Ni valence state toward Ni 2+ , making the system more insulating as experimentally observed in oxygen-deficient La 3Ni 2O7 [21, 40]. To address this issue, we anneal a sample from the as-grown batch of La 2.9Sr 0.1Ni 2O7 in the presence of KClO 4, yielding smaller resistivity, but retaining a pronounced semiconducting T dependence, as shown in Fig. S7 in [39]. The plotted value in Fig. 4(a) is for this annealed sample. Nevertheless, the resistivity is still very high and superconductivity is not observed up to 20 GPa. This may indicate that either the growth parameters are not yet fully optimized or that the decreased band filling might favor the formation of an insulating state, e.g., due to charge ordering. By contrast, substituting Sr 2+ in La 2−xNdSr xNi 2O7 has the desired effect and restores conductivity: For x = 0 .1, ρ10K decreases. Further increasing the Sr content to x = 0 .2yields a slightly larger ρ10K than for x = 0 .1. This might be attributed to a stronger disorder effect, leading to additional localization at low T as shown in Fig. S11 in [39]. Note that for 

x = 0 .2, our growth method does not diminish impurity phases as successfully as for x = 0 .1(Section S2 in [39]), possibly indicating that x = 0 .2 is close to the solubility limit of Sr in La 3Ni 2O7, even when employing high-pressure synthesis. Figure 4(b) summarizes the composition dependence of the T1 anomaly at ambient p.For La 3Ni 2O7, we find T1 ∼ 110 K. A similar anomaly is observed for La 2NdNi 2O7 at a 7higher T1 ∼ 130 K, implying that the introduction of smaller Nd 3+ ions stabilizes the T1

anomaly. If the origin of T1 is related to DW formation, this might be understood as a consequence of enhanced correlation effects, although we cannot rule out that Fermi-surface nesting plays a role, based on the data at hand. By contrast, Sr doping strongly suppresses this feature: The T1 anomaly is absent in La 2.9Sr 0.1Ni 2O7 and is shifted to below 100 K in La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7. While one could argue that the large resistivity of La 2.9Sr 0.1Ni 2O7 may hide this feature, the comparison with much more conductive La 2−xNdSr xNi 2O7 suggests that Sr effectively counteracts the origin of the T1 anomaly. It is successively suppressed as a function of x and only barely visible for x = 0 .2 (Section S6 in [39]) which mimics the physical pressure effect. Figure 4(c) presents the composition dependence of the critical pressure pc corresponding to the pressure at which zero resistance is observed. The result for La 3Ni 2O7 agrees with published data [1–4]. Whether adding Sr in La 3Ni 2O7 truly destroys the superconductivity remains an open question, since the large resistivity observed for this batch does not follow a systematic trend, as implied by Figs. 4(a) and S11 in [39]. This is further supported by the beneficial effect of adding Sr in La 2NdNi 2O7: While introducing smaller Nd 3+ ions requires higher pressure to induce zero resistance, hole doping by Sr 2+ shifts the superconducting phase back to lower pressure. This is in good agreement with the expected opposite effects of Nd 3+ and Sr 2+ with modifying the band width and filling, respectively. As can also be seen in Fig. 4(c), the Sr concentration range in which a robust superconducting phase is established is rather narrow. One possible explanation is that the Sr concentration of 0.2, albeit in favor of reducing the tilting of the NiO 6 octahedra, might remove too many electrons from the d3z2−r2 bonding band. According to Ref. [10], too much hole doping of this bonding band suppresses the superconductivity. As for the anomalies observed at finite pressure, the origin of T3 remains elusive, whereas 

T2 may be related to a SDW transition, as suggested in the literature [14, 20]. Interestingly, 

T1 and T2 exhibit opposite pressure dependences, in contrast to the cuprate case, where DW features are usually coupled. This points to an important difference in the electronic ground states of these families of high-Tc superconductors, i.e., nickelates versus cuprates, and provides an appealing starting point for future experimental and theoretical studies. 8IV. MATERIALS AND METHODS A. Sample growth and characterization 

All batches La 2−xRSr xNi 2O7 were grown by a high-pressure synthesis method, which requires precursor material. For La 3Ni 2O7, a wet-chemical method was employed, see e.g., [21, 40], while for the other four compositions, a standard solid-state reaction was used. In both cases, the resulting material was thoroughly ground, pressed into pellets, and fired several times under O 2 gas flow in a tube furnace to improve the phase purity. Ruddlesden-Popper series such as La n+1 Ni nO3n+1 are known to suffer from impurity phase formation of other compositions with different n and oxygen vacancies, e.g., [21, 38]. The final high-pressure synthesis step was implemented here because it has potential to minimize or even remove such impurity phases and, by adding an oxidizing material such as KClO 4,to adjust the oxygen stoichiometry. Hence, high-pressure synthesis offers a unique and fast way to approach these issues. The growth parameters were optimized for each composition in subsequent high-pressure synthesis attempts and are summarized in Table S1 in [39]. In the case of La 2.9Sr 0.1Ni 2O7, the optimized as-grown batch is rather insulating and a post-growth annealing step, also under high pressure and utilizing KClO 4, was added to further reduce possible oxygen vacancies. All batches were checked with an in-house powder x-ray diffractometer (Rigaku). Re-spective data are summarized and discussed in Section S2 in [39]. The chemical composition and homogeneity of the optimized batches were probed by utilizing a scanning-electron mi-croscope equipped with an energy-dispersive x-ray analyzer (SEM-EDX; JEOL Ltd. and Bruker). Selected results are presented in Section S3 in [39].  

> B. Atomic resolution chemical analysis

A La 2NdNi 2O7 sample was gently crushed in ethanol and dispersed on a holey amor-phous carbon grid for scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) observations. The selected-area electron diffraction was performed using a 2100HC (JEOL Ltd.) operated at 200 kV. Annular dark-field (ADF)- and annular bright-field (ABF) STEM images were ac-quired using a ARM200F (JEOL Ltd.), equipped with an ASCOR corrector and a cold field-emission gun, operated at 200 kV. The convergence semi-angle was 24 mrad, and the 9collection semi-angles for ABF and ADF were 12 − 24 and 53 − 170 mrad, respectively. Electron energy-loss spectra (EELS) were acquired by a continuum spectrometer (Gatan Inc.), equipped with an ARM 200F microscope. The convergence and collection semi-angles were 24 and 62 mrad, respectively. To improve the signal-to-noise ratio, a sequential av-eraging method was used for the ADF-STEM images [41], and total third-degree variation (TTDV) regularization was used for EELS elemental maps [42]. In this work, we used the ARIM-mdx data system [43] to process the data.  

> C. Transport measurements

Rectangular-shaped samples were cut from each batch and polished down to a size of typically 750 × 550 × 450 μm3 for resistivity measurements under pressure. Four electrical contacts were made with EPO-TEK H20E epoxy. Curing was performed in a two-step process: (i) A sample with fresh contacts was placed on a hot plate (15 − 20 min at nominally 150 ◦C − 200 ◦C in air) to initially cure the EPO-TEK. (ii) It was transferred into a furnace (mini-lamp annealer, Mila 5050Z, Advance Riko) and heated for 1 h at nominally 500 ◦C in O 2 gas flow, cf., e.g., [44]. This resulted in two-point resistances of typically a few Ω. Then the sample was measured at ambient pressure in a commercially available system (physical property measurement system, PPMS, Quantum Design) and eventually in a custom-made cubic-anvil press with Daphne oil 7575 as pressure-transmitting medium. Resistivity data under pressure were taken upon warming between ∼ 4.2 K and ∼ 290 K. Here, we use a cubic-anvil press to generate a highly hydrostatic pressure distribution.

S3 La 3Ni 2O7 is the bilayer ( n = 2) composition of the Ruddlesden-Popper (RP) series La n+1 Ni nO3n+1 . RP series are known to suffer from impurity phase formation of related compositions with different n. In the present case, phase fractions of the monolayer La 2NiO 4

(n = 1) and the trilayer La 4Ni 3O10 (n = 3) are often observed, e.g., [4–7]. After optimizing the high-pressure synthesis parameters, La 2NiO 4 is usually not de-tectable within the resolution limit of the in-house powder x-ray diffractometer used [Figs. S1(a) – (d)]. Only in the data for La 1.8NdSr 0.2Ni 2O7 shown in Fig. S1(e), there is a tiny feature discernible at the left-hand side of the n = 2 main peak, possibly reflecting a small n = 1 phase fraction, cf. Figs. S1(e) – (g). This may indicate that x = 0 .2 is close to the solubility limit of Sr in La 2−xNdSr xNi 2O7.In the XRD data on La 3Ni 2O7 shown in Fig. S1(a), a small extrinsic peak is seen on the higher-2 θ side of the n = 2 main peak. It is likely to stem from an La 4Ni 3O10 impurity phase. For the four other compositions, n = 3 impurity reflections are not clearly discernible due to the shift of the intrinsic peaks induced by chemical substitution. Broadening of the, e.g., n = 2 main peak in La 2−xNdSr xNi 2O7 may hint toward the formation of a tiny n = 3 phase as a function of x [Figs. S1(c) – (e)]. In addition, there is usually a small NiO phase fraction remaining. Notably, for these batches, there are no indications of the “1313” polymorph of La 3Ni 2O7

consisting of alternating mono- and trilayers as discussed in the literature [5, 8, 9].

The chemical compositions of the batches La 2−xRSr xNi 2O7 discussed in the main text were determined by employing a scanning-electron microscope equipped with an energy-dispersive x-ray analyzer (SEM-EDX; JEOL Ltd. and Bruker). EDX analyses were performed on different samples from the same batches as used for the transport measurements. On each sample, up to 30 spots in different surface areas were analyzed, and the results averaged with hot spots, e.g., due to NiO impurities excluded. The quantitative results are summarized in Table S2. The errors therein are given by the respective standard deviations. Due to the limited sensitivity of EDX to light elements, oxygen is excluded from the quantitative analyses. In this work, the nominal compositions are used when referring to samples. In all batches, away from hot spots, the spatial distributions of La, Nd, Sr, and Ni are fairly homogeneous, and the quantitative EDX results are close to the nominal compositions with comparatively small error bars. By contrast, in small but recognizable Ni-rich areas, La and, if included, Nd are depleted, cf. Figs. S3 and S4. These present representative elemental mapping images for La 3Ni 2O7 and La 1.9NdSr 0.1Ni 2O7, respectively.
