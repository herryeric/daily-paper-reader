(Dated: March 26, 2026) We investigate the electronic structure at the surface of the correlated oxide Ca 3Ru 2O7, a low-symmetry ruthenate oxide which hosts an unconventional polar-metal phase. From a combination of angle-resolved pho-toemission spectroscopy and scanning tunneling spectroscopy measurements, we demonstrate that the surface hosts an insulating phase, a distinct departure from metallicity within the bulk. Utilizing quantitative low-energy electron diffraction in conjunction with electronic structure calculations, we show how this results from a combined surface structure relaxation and the impact of marked electronic correlations in this system. Our findings highlight the proximity of Ca 3Ru 2O7 to an insulating metallic state, and illustrate how subtle structural distortions can control its emergent electronic phases. 

In materials where the electron-electron interactions are strong, numerous nearly degenerate ground states can arise from the complex interplay of various degrees of freedom, including lattice, orbital, charge, and spin [1]. In these so-called strongly correlated electron materials, a subtle pertur-bation to any of these can result in a significant change in the material’s physical properties. Consequently, strongly corre-lated materials exhibit diverse phase diagrams that encompass exotic many-body quantum phases, including strange metal-licity, unconventional superconductivity, and Mott insulators [2, 3]. The Ruddlesden-Popper (RP) ruthenates are an ideal materials class in which to explore this physics. They host a wide range of ground states which can be accessed via only rather minor modification of the crystal structure and com-position, including unconventional superconductivity, mag-netism and correlated insulating states [4–7]. In particular, Ca 3Ru 2O7, the bilayer member of the RP se-ries of calcium ruthenates, displays competing electronic and magnetic orders, and appears to lie on the verge of multi-ple electronic and magnetic instabilities [8–10]. Upon cool-ing from a ‘bad-metal’-like metallic state at room tempera-ture, it undergoes a sequence of magnetic and structural phase transitions. A N´ eel ordering occurs to an anti-ferromagnetic (AFM) phase at TN ≈ 56 K, where the spins orient along the crystallographic a-axis, aligning ferromagnetically within a bilayer but antiferromagnetically between neighboring bi-layers (AFM a). At Ts ≈ 48 K, the spins reorient to point along the b-axis (AFM b), coupled with an isostructural phase transition which leads to a large jump in the measured resistiv-ity [11–14]. This change in the electronic ground state of Ca 3Ru 2O7 is accompanied by a change in the low-temperature magnetic structure. Increasing the Ti concentration, the material transi-tions from the AFM b phase to an AFM G phase, with G-type AFM order and spins canted along the c-axis [19]. Interest-ingly, the Ti-doping into the crystal lattice drives a change in the rotation and tilt of the RuO 6 octahedra [18], highlighting the sensitivity of the physical properties of Ca 3Ru 2O7 to subtle distortions of its constituent RuO 6 octahedra. This sensitivity offers rich opportunities to explore different correlated phases. Within prior studies, pressure and strain have been utilized to alter the structure of the RuO 6 octa-hedra, and hence, the magnetic and electronic properties of Ca 3Ru 2O7 [20, 21]. The local structure can also, however, be different at the surface of the crystal to the bulk [22, 23], providing an ultra-clean route to track the impact of subtle structural distortions on the electronic states and phases that form. In this letter we report on the impact of such surface structural distortions in Ca 3Ru 2O7, demonstrating how they not only tune the electronic structure but, in fact, also allow for a distinct, correlated insulating ground state to be stabi-lized. Single crystal samples of Ca 3Ru 2O7 were grown by the floating zone method [24]. Both antiphase and polar do-mains are typically present in growths of Ca 3Ru 2O7. The spatial extent of antiphase domains is such that we could se-lect mono-domain samples for angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) using polarized light microscopy [25] or measure diffracted intensity from a single antiphase domain in low energy electron diffraction (LEED) (see supplemen-tary figure 1). In comparison, polar domains are significantly smaller [26]. Consequently, our LEED measurements average over an approximately equal population of polar domains (see supplementary figure 2). All samples were cleaved in situ before the measurements. For Ca 3Ru 2O7, the intra-bilayer coupling is much stronger than the inter-bilayer coupling [27]. This means the crystal cleaves between bilayers, exposing a CaO 2 terminated surface [28]. ARPES measurements were performed on a freshly-cleaved surface using the BLOCH beamline of the Max-IV synchrotron. The samples were measured at a temperature of T ≈ 18 K using 33 eV synchrotron light. Scanning tun-neling spectroscopy (STS) measurements were performed us-ing a home-built low temperature scanning tunneling micro-scope (STM) operating in a vector magnet [29]. The measure-ments were performed at 4.2 K unless stated otherwise. The bias voltage was applied to the sample with the tip on virtual ground. The differential conductance was recorded using a standard lock-in technique, adding an AC modulation to the sample bias. Samples for STM are cleaved using a cleaving stage mounted to the 20 K-plate of the insert. LEED mea-surements were performed using an OCI LEED BDL800IR optics mounted onto a ultra-high vacuum system. Measure-ments were performed at a temperature of T ≈ 10 K. Images of the LEED pattern were acquired using a CCD camera, and taken in steps of 1 eV between 150 and 500 eV. Experimental 

I(V, Q) spectra were extracted from the LEED images using Python code before being analyzed using the CLEED pack-age [30, 31]. To estimate the uncertainties associated with our LEED analysis, we measured and fit two different samples of Ca 3Ru 2O7. The displacements presented below are the mean best-fit displacement, and uncertainties are one standard devi-ation from the mean. To model the surface electronic structure of Ca 3Ru 2O7,density functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed using the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP) code [33–35] and local density approximation exchange-correlation functional. Both spin-orbit coupling (SOC) and magnetic order along the b-axis were included within the cal-culations and correlations were included by adjusting the U

value of the Ru atoms within the Dudarev approximation [36]. To model the effect of structural distortions at the surface while maintaining acceptable computational cost, calculations were performed for a free-standing bilayer of Ca 3Ru 2O7, with a 15 ˚A gap to the next bilayer. Consistent with prior studies, we observe a significant spectral weight in the deep-lying valence band, originating from Ru t2g states [25, 37, 38], but with broad linewidths that point to rather in-coherent states. Close to the Fermi level, this spectral weight becomes strongly suppressed. Over a narrow energy range be-low the Fermi level, we observe a vanishing spectral weight. Nonetheless, with an enhanced contrast, we can observe sharp but extremely weak states close to and crossing the Fermi level (figure 1d), before their spectral intensity becomes dominated by the incoherent states beyond ∼ 100 meV. The finite albeit small DOS at the Fermi level, figure 1e, is consistent with the ground state of Ca 3Ru 2O7 as being a low carrier-density metal, as known from bulk studies [15]. However, within this picture, the almost vanishingly-small coherent spectral weight at EF

as compared to the incoherent weight at higher binding ener-gies would suggest an extremely small quasiparticle residue. To better understand the electronic structure, we have there-fore performed STS measurements, displayed also in figure 1e. These data, taken on the same batch of crystals as the ARPES measurements, display a full gap of ∼ 150 meV asym-metrically distributed around the Fermi level. This indicates an insulating state, seemingly at odds with our photoemission measurements. We will show below that this is due to a dis-tinct surface and bulk electronic structure, and different depths of the two techniques. First, we consider the geometry of the Ca 3Ru 2O7 (001) sur-face, as shown in figure 2a. In figure 2b, we present a to-pographic image of this CaO-terminated surface. Prominent within the topography are stripes. These stripes represent channels with a decreased (darker) or increased (lighter) den-sity of oxygen atoms [39], resulting from the tilting of the RuO 6 octahedra, as illustrated in figure 2a. As the tilting of the RuO 6 octahedra is unidirectional in Ca 3Ru 2O7, we ob-serve stripes only along the [010] crystallographic direction, and thus within the Fourier transform of the topography (in-set in figure 2b) the (1,0) and (-1,0) Bragg peaks are present whilst the (0,1) and (0,-1) Bragg peaks are extinct. The uni-directional tilt of the RuO 6 octahedra is also observed in our LEED analysis of a pristine (001) surface of Ca 3Ru 2O7, figure 2c. A tilting of the RuO 6 octahedra in RP crystals destroys one glide line in the two-dimensional unit cell. If the octahedral tilt is along a single axis, only a single glide line symmetry is broken whilst the orthogonal glide line is preserved. This is illustrated by the dot-dashed lines in the top-down view of the two-dimensional unit cell in figure 2a. Therefore, in our LEED measurements, we observe spots along a single direc-tion, the [100] direction, whilst those along the orthogonal [010] direction are extinct. Together, these measurements suggest a surface symmetry consistent with the one expected from the bulk crystal struc-ture. In fact, at all incident electron beam energies mea-sured, we observe only LEED spots corresponding to the bulk-terminated unit cell, indicating that Ca 3Ru 2O7 does not undergo a structural reconstruction at its surface. Surface relaxation can still occur, however, resulting in changes in RuO 6 octahedral rotation, tilt angle or vertical displacements of atoms from their bulk positions. Indeed, the symmetry-preserving structural phase transition in the bulk indicates a strong coupling of electronic, magnetic and intra-unit-cell structural degrees of freedom in this system, necessitating searching for structural changes beyond simple reconstruc-tion effects. This motivates quantitative LEED measurements, recording and modeling the intensity of the LEED spots as a function of energy in order to extract quantitative information about atomic displacements of the surface atoms. Despite an increased rotation angle of the surface RuO 6 octahedra, there is no substantial narrowing of the bandwidth. However, when including electronic correla-tions through a local Coulomb repulsion U , we find a marked difference for the electronic structures. As shown in figure 4c, a band gap opens at the Fermi level with increasing U , point-ing to a correlation-driven metal-to-insulator transition (MIT). While susceptibility to such a correlated-insulating state is not surprising in and of itself, intriguingly our calculations find that the surface relaxation causes this transition to occur at a different (slightly lower) U value than in the bulk. For a nar-5row range of U values ( ∼ 2.5 to 2.75 eV), we calculate that the bulk crystal is metallic, while for the surface structure we find a correlated insulator, as shown for U = 2 .625 eV in figure 4d and e. These DFT +U calculations provide a natural explanation for the seemingly contradictory results introduced in figure 1d. While both ARPES and STS are surface-sensitive probes, they probe the electronic structure with different effective informa-tion depth. Within ARPES measurements, where the surface sensitivity is determined by the mean free path of the escaping photoelectrons, there is a finite contribution to the signal from the bulk-like layers immediately below the surface. In con-trast, STS probes the tails of the wavefunctions of the surface electrons, and is thus practically only sensitive to the surface-most layer. Consequently, within our ARPES measurements in figure 1c, we observe both the surface and (with lower intensity) bulk-like electronic states. STM, however, probes only an insulating surface layer. We thus assign the metal-lic bands with weak spectral weight visible in ARPES to be those of the bulk crystal, consistent with prior transport stud-ies which reveal metallicity in the bulk crystal [15, 27]. Con-trastingly, we attribute the incoherent states located at energies 

> 100 meV away from the Fermi level, which carry the dom-inant spectral weight in ARPES and which will dominate the gap edges in STS, as the lower and upper Hubbard bands of a Mott-like correlated insulating phase. We thus conclude that, while the subtle structural distortions of the surface layer are insufficient to significantly modify the electronic structure on their own, they facilitate the formation of a correlation driven insulating state. In summary, via a combined approach of precise surface structural and spectroscopic measurements, accompanied by electronic structure calculations, we have found that a struc-tural relaxation at the surface of Ca 3Ru 2O7 facilitates the for-mation of a correlation-driven insulating phase. This allows us to reconcile seemingly conflicting spectroscopic measure-ments, and reveals the striking role that surface relaxations can have, even when they do not lead to a symmetry-lowering reconstruction. Our calculations highlight that this insulating phase is rather fragile, being on the verge of metallicity. This raises an opportunity to drive a phase transition into a metal-lic state with moderate external stimuli, for example, via the application of strain or electric field. Furthermore, the sub-tle structural relaxations at the surface of Ca 3Ru 2O7 and the narrow U window within which we calculate the surface to be insulating while the bulk remains metallic indicate that the bulk material is also on the verge of a MIT, raising the possi-bility of driving the bulk through a phase transition.
