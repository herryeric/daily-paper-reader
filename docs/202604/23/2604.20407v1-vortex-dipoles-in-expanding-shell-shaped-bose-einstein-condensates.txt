Releasing shell-shaped Bose-Einstein condensates from their confinement produces a spherically symmetric density distribution characterized by concentric ripples surrounding a central peak. Here we investigate how a vortex-antivortex dipole affects this dynamics, finding that increasing dipole separation progressively breaks the spherical symmetry and, correspondingly, the interplay of vortex physics and curvature produces a non-monotonic behavior of the cloud aspect ratio. These features can be used for preparing and detecting vortex dipoles in shell-shaped superfluids, as well as for analyzing their signatures in other thin superfluids with more general curved geometries. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Shell-shaped Bose-Einstein condensates are nowadays routinely realized by confining bosonic gases in hollow shells with magnetic and optical potentials [1–5]. Inter-estingly, these thin shells must have zero net vorticity in the incompressible regime due to topological constraints on the phase distribution of the superfluid order param-eter [6]. This fact implies that the simplest vortex con-figuration allowed is a vortex-antivortex dipole [7]. Ac-tually, the zero-temperature superfluid ground state is vortex free, because the annihilation of eventually exist-ing dipoles always lowers the system energy. Therefore, vortices appear as the main (topological) excitation of shell-shaped gases, and several studies focused on vor-tex dynamics in spherical superfluids [6–16], as well as in classical fluids [17–21]. Experimentally, vorticity is typically imaged by allow-ing the gas to expand, and by comparing its phase profile with that of a reference condensate [22–24]. In shell-shaped gases, this requirement is partially alleviated by the possibility of observing self-interference patterns dur-ing the expansion [25]. While this process has been ex-tensively studied in both experiments [2, 3] and theory [25–28], and despite numerous investigations of vortex dynamics in spherical superfluids, the signature of vor-tices in expanding shell-shaped condensates is still unex-plored. Here we analyze the free expansion of thin spherical shells with a phase profile describing two point vortices with opposite circulation. Once shell geometry and other basic gas parameters are fixed, the only relevant scale for describing the system is the vortex-antivortex angular separation 2 ℓ, i.e. twice the latitude ℓ of each vortex (see Fig. 1). We find that, during the gas expansion, vortex cores produces density holes around the latitudes ±ℓ, lo-cally broadening the cloud in the direction perpendicular to the surface. Even though this phenomenology is com-patible with previous studies on flat low-dimensional su-perfluids [29–33], the observed interference patterns sig-nificantly differ because varying 2 ℓ displaces vortices on a curved surface. In particular, while vortices located near the poles enhance the density spread in the equa-torial plane, vortices near the equator broaden more the cloud along the vertical direction. This interplay of vor-tex physics and geometry leads to a non-monotonic de-pendence of the cloud aspect ratio on ℓ, a feature that 

flat low-dimensional systems cannot possess. This asym-metry provides a measurable quantity which, since ab-sorption imaging is a standard detection technique, can favor the experimental detection of vortices reproducibly prepared under controlled initial conditions.

Our analysis contributes to a better understanding of vortex dynamics in curved geometries, which is one of the main motivations for studying shell-shaped gases [2]. In particular, the current method can be adapted to study multi-vortex configurations, and to model the free expan-sion of superfluids with known vortical phase distribution in different geometries, including the cylinder [34], the cone [35], the torus and surfaces of revolution [36], and the ellipsoid [16]. In all these cases, one expects quali-tatively similar behaviors: expanding vortex cores gener-ate density holes [29–33], leading to different aspect ra-tios from the vortex-free case, with geometry-dependent anisotropies determined by the surface curvature.

For sufficiently weak interactions and small atom numbers, so that nonlinear terms are weak, the change in the behavior of A reasonably occur at the value 2 ℓ ∼ π/ 2, at which vortices are located half-way between the poles and the equator. However, when reducing the shell thickness and there-fore increasing the initial kinetic energy of the shell, the magnitude of this non-monotonic behavior is dimin-ished, as shown by the trend of A versus R/l r plot-ted in Fig. 4(b). Indeed, while for 2 ℓ = 0 .6, a non-monotonic region persists for a short time for thicker shells, a more complex dynamics driven by the stronger role of nonlinearity and by the larger kinetic energy re-duces this region. A and similar observables can thus help experiments to intuitively reconstruct vortex location in thicker shells, while quantitative side-by-side comparison with the sim-ulated shell sizes is needed to image vortices in the deep two-dimensional regime. Finally, we also analyzed the maximal central density achieved inside the angular region r < 0.3 during the ex-pansion. Consistently with our previous analyses [28], we find that this quantity shows a clear decreasing behavior with 2 ℓ for two-dimensional shells in the two-dimensional interaction regime having R/l r ∼ 6. This result can be interpreted as the increasing difficulty of the gas to reach the center when a larger fraction occupies highly-excited angular momentum states l, which face the filtering by a repulsive centrifugal potential Vl(r) ∝ l(l + 1) /r 2. At the same time, the dependence of the maximal central den-sity with 2 ℓ becomes non-monotonic, and therefore less informative on the underlying physical processes when considering thinner shells having R/l r ≫ 1. > IV. CONCLUSIONS

In this paper, we have considered the expansion dy-namics of a shell-shaped Bose-Einstein condensate whose initial phase profile contains a vortex-antivortex dipole located at distance 2 ℓ. The expanding dipole in such curved geometry reduces the cloud sphericity with in-creasing 2 ℓ, and correspondingly yields a non-monotonic behavior of the shell aspect ratio, a fact explained by the interplay between vortex physics and geometry. These results can be observed experimentally by studying the expansion of vortex-antivortex dipoles pinned at a con-trollable distance in shell-shaped Bose-Einstein conden-sates. Possible experiments include binary Bose mix-tures in the phase-separation regime [3, 4, 37, 38] (see also Refs. [5, 39–42]), for which we tailor our numerical parameters. The method developed here can be easily extended to evaluate the signature of vortex dynamics in superfluid films with any curved geometry. For this scope, it is sufficient to know the superfluid phase profile 5analytically, to imprint the desired configuration of point vortices as the initial condition of the Gross-Pitaevskii equation. This information is available for all standard curved surfaces, including the cylinder [34], the cone [35], the torus and surfaces of revolution [36], and the ellipsoid [16].
