## Josephson phase shift and diode effect due to the inverse spin Hall effect 

Gen Tatara 

RIKEN Center for Emergent Matter Science (CEMS), 2-1 Hirosawa, Wako, Saitama, 351-0198 Japan 

Yositake Takane 

Graduate School of Advanced Science and Engineering, Hiroshima University, Higashihiroshima, Hiroshima 739-8530, Japan 

Aurelien Manchon 

CINaM, Aix-Marseille University, France 

(Dated: April 17, 2026) We theoretically study the direct and inverse spin Hall effects in a superconductor–normal metal– superconductor junction induced by a spin–orbit interaction that is invariant under spatial inversion. We show that a supercurrent induces a spin Hall effect, leading to a static spin accumulation with opposite polarizations at the two edges, analogous to that in normal conductors. For the inverse effect, we consider a spatially inhomogeneous static magnetic field and show that it induces an anomalous phase shift, which, in the presence of higher harmonics, results in a diode effect. Unlike Rashba systems, the present mechanism does not require broken structural inversion symmetry. 

Introduction– Spintronic effects in superconductors have attracted considerable interest over the past few decades, driven by the prospect of combining non-dissipative su-percurrents (SCs) with the nonvolatility of spin-based devices. After pioneering experiments dating back to the late 1970s [1], superconducting spintronics has un-dergone rapid development over the past decade. Key advances include the realization of long-range spin-triplet SCs in ferromagnet–superconductor hybrids [2–7], elec-tric and magnetic control of superconducting spin trans-port [8–10], and the integration of materials with strong spin–orbit (SO) coupling (e.g., topological insulators and Rashba interfaces) to manipulate spin textures [11–13]. These developments open pathways toward dissipation-less memory and logic elements and deepen the interplay between superconductivity and topology [14, 15]. Of particular recent interest are systems with broken symmetries. In superconductors lacking inversion sym-metry, nonreciprocal SC transport becomes possible in the presence of an applied magnetic field or magneti-zation. This manifests as a direction-dependent criti-cal current, known as the superconducting diode effect, first observed in inversion-asymmetric superlattices [16]. The superconducting diode effect has since been demon-strated in Josephson junctions comprising superconduc-tors coupled to normal metals or semiconductors with Rashba spin–orbit coupling [17–19]. Rashba SO cou-pling, which arises from the interplay of SO interac-tion and inversion symmetry breaking, generates a uni-form spin polarization under an applied current via the Rashba–Edelstein effect, for both dissipative currents and SCs [20, 21]. In superconductor–normal metal–superconductor (SNS) Josephson junctions, Ref. [22] predicted that a SC induces a uniform spin polarization and, conversely, that a magnetic field generates a SC — an effect known

as the inverse Rashba–Edelstein effect. This inverse effect was shown to produce an anomalous phase shift between the superconductors. However, such a phase shift alone does not give rise to a diode effect, as it does not break the symmetry of the phase potential and therefore yields identical barrier heights for both current polarities. More recently, it has been shown that this anomalous phase shift can lead to a Josephson diode effect when higher harmonics—arising from multiple interface scattering—are taken into account [19]. In this paper, we study theoretically the spin Hall ef-fect (SHE) and its inverse effect (ISHE) in an SNS junc-tion induced by a SO interaction that is invariant under spatial inversion (Fig. 1). We show that a SC induces a SHE, leading to a static spin accumulation of opposite polarizations at the two edges, analogous to the behavior in normal conductors. We also investigate the inverse effect by considering a spatially inhomogeneous static magnetic field. Such a magnetic field, acting on the elec-tron spin, generates a steady spin current [23], which in turn couples to the superconducting channel and induces an anomalous phase shift. We show that the anomalous phase shift results in the diode effect by accounting for higher harmonics. In contrast to the diode effect due

Premises on spin Hall effect– Conventionally, the SHE in normal metal is described in terms of the spin current generated by the applied electric field [24]. Since the spin current is not directly measurable, it is converted to an experimentally measurable spin accumulation using clas-sical spin diffusion equation. Inverse spin Hall-induced diode effect– The anomalous phase shift, which indicates a directional current, might naively be expected to lead to a Josephson diode effect, where the critical current depends on the current direc-tion. This is not the case, however, since a static anoma-lous phase shift is a ground-state property and does not affect the response to an injected current. A diode effect emerges when higher harmonics due to multiple interface scattering is taken into account [19]. The inverse Rashba-Edelstein effect reacts to the uniform component of the applied field, while the ISHE reacts to the spatial asymmetry of the field. We have demonstrated that the ISHE in SNS Joseph-son junctions provides a powerful mechanism for phase control [28] via equilibrium spin currents generated by proximate magnetic textures. Such equilibrium spin cur-rents may arise, for instance, from a proximate magnetic dot, a ferromagnet hosting a magnetic domain wall (Fig. 5(a)) or from an applied magnetic field gradient.
