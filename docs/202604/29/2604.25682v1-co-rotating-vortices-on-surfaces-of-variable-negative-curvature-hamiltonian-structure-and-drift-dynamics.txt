# Co-rotating Vortices on Surfaces of Variable Negative Curvature: Hamiltonian Structure and Drift Dynamics 

Gaurang Mangesh Joshi 1 and Rickmoy Samanta 1, 2 1Birla Institute of Technology and Science, Pilani, Hyderabad Campus, Telangana 500078, India 

> 2

Indian Institute of Technology Kharagpur, West Bengal 721302, India 

Vortices in fluids and superfluids underpin phenomena ranging from Bose–Einstein condensates and superfluid films to neutron stars and hydrodynamic micro-rotors, where geometry can strongly influence their motion. Curvature can induce vortex motion with no planar analogue. We study Hamiltonian vortex motion on a catenoid, a minimal surface of variable negative curvature, and derive explicit equations of motion, conserved quantities, and reductions for co-rotating vortex pairs. For two identical vortices we find an exact antipodal solution in which the pair rotates rigidly at fixed latitude, with angular velocity Ω = (Γ /16 π) K′(V )/p−K(V ), where K(V )is the Gaussian curvature. Thus the motion is governed by the curvature gradient rather than the curvature itself. The symmetric state is linearly unstable, with growth rate λ = √3|Ω|, in agreement with numerical simulations. For generic equal-strength pairs, conservation of the Hamiltonian and rotational momentum reduces the nonlinear dynamics to a single quadrature, yielding bounded relative oscillations together with a secular azimuthal drift. Simulations of the full equations confirm the reduced theory and reveal the same curvature-induced transport mechanism in a localized many-vortex cluster, motivating a broader theory of collective vortex drift on curved surfaces. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Vortices are fundamental carriers of vorticity in a wide range of physical systems, from Bose–Einstein condensates and superfluid films to astrophysical fluids such as neutron star interiors, as well as engineered hydrodynamic rotors and active flows. A minimal theoretical framework for their motion is provided by point-vortex dynamics, which offers one of the 

> arXiv:2604.25682v1 [math-ph] 28 Apr 2026

2simplest Hamiltonian descriptions of two-dimensional rotating incompressible flow. In the plane, the motion is governed by the Kirchhoff–Routh function and inherits the Euclidean symmetries of the underlying domain, leading to conserved linear and angular momenta and to logarithmic pair interactions [1–4]. Classical vortex pairs, dipoles, and clusters have therefore served as paradigmatic models, see, for example, [5, 6], and their extensions to curved and confined geometries [7–11]. On curved surfaces, geometry fundamentally alters vortex motion. Both the hydrody-namic Green function and the self-interaction (also known as the Robin function) acquire explicit geometric dependence. As a result, curvature acts as an effective field: it breaks translational invariance, modifies the phase-space structure, and generates drift and confine-ment mechanisms with no direct planar analogue. The study of point vortices on curved manifolds has developed along several directions. Vortex dynamics on spheres and other constant-curvature surfaces has revealed how global topology and curvature alter relative equilibria, streamline topology, and integrability prop-erties [12–16]. More general surfaces, including surfaces of revolution and closed curved manifolds, have been used to clarify the role of the Kirchhoff–Routh function, momentum maps, and the curvature-dependent Robin term in vortex motion on curved surfaces has been extensively studied [17–23], with recent developments in [24, 25]. Recent work has also shown that curved membranes and surfaces of revolution can support vortex transport, nontrivial streamline topology, and collective cluster dynamics [7–9]. These developments suggest that variable curvature is not merely a geometric background, but an active ingre-dient in the organization of vortex motion. Curved-surface vortex dynamics is also relevant for quantum fluids. In thin superfluid films and quasi-two-dimensional Bose–Einstein condensates, point vortices provide an effec-tive description of quantized vortex motion, and curved or anisotropic trapping geometries can strongly affect vortex trajectories, vortex-pair dynamics, and turbulent clustering [26– 34]. These systems motivate the search for analytically tractable geometries in which the consequences of curvature can be isolated and understood in closed form. In this paper we focus on co-rotating point vortices on a catenoid. The catenoid is a min-imal surface with tunable throat radius a and nonuniform negative Gaussian curvature. Its axial symmetry yields a conserved momentum, while its conformal metric allows the Green function, Hamiltonian, symplectic form, and equations of motion to be written explicitly. 3This makes the catenoid a useful example for studying how curvature gradients influence vortex motion beyond constant-curvature geometries. We first derive the global Hamiltonian formulation for N vortices on the catenoid, revis-iting and extending the framework of Ref. [10], where dipolar vortex motion was studied. In contrast, the present work focuses on co-rotating vortex pairs. The Hamiltonian consists of the pairwise Green-function interaction together with a curvature-induced self-interaction term. The corresponding symplectic form is weighted by the catenoid area element, and the rotational symmetry gives a momentum map. These two conserved quantities, the Hamil-tonian H and the momentum J, provide the main organizing structure for the two-vortex dynamics. Our main analysis concerns two equal-strength co-rotating vortices. We show that the system is Liouville integrable: fixing J eliminates the collective meridional coordinate, while fixing H eliminates the relative azimuthal angle, leaving a single quadrature for the relative meridional separation. This reduction gives a complete analytic description of the nonlinear two-vortex motion and allows direct reconstruction of the mean azimuthal drift. We then identify an exact symmetric solution in which two identical vortices remain antipodal at a fixed latitude and rotate rigidly around the catenoid. The angular velocity is controlled by the curvature gradient rather than by the curvature itself. In particular, if 

K(V ) is the Gaussian curvature along the meridional coordinate, the angular velocity can be written as a local geometric combination involving K′(V )/p−K(V ). Consequently, the rotation vanishes at the throat, where the curvature is extremal but its gradient is zero, and is maximal away from the throat. We also show that this symmetric co-rotating state is linearly unstable, with growth rate proportional to the rigid-rotation frequency. Direct numerical simulations of the full two-vortex equations confirm both the instability rate and the reduced quadrature dynamics. Finally, we include a brief outlook on many-vortex dynamics. A representative simulation of a localized same-sign vortex cluster shows coherent azimuthal drift while the cluster remains compact. This indicates that the curvature-induced transport mechanism found for vortex pairs persists in the many-vortex problem, although with additional shape-dependent collective corrections. A detailed theory of such cluster drift will be developed in future work [35]. The paper is organized as follows. In Sec. II we introduce the catenoid geometry and 4derive the Hamiltonian formulation for N point vortices, including the Green function, symplectic form, equations of motion, and the conserved angular momentum. In Sec. III we specialize to two vortices and rewrite the dynamics in collective and relative variables; the conserved Hamiltonian and momentum are then used to reduce the two-vortex problem to a single quadrature. In Sec. III A we identify an exact symmetric co-rotating solution in which two identical vortices remain antipodal at fixed latitude and rotate rigidly around the catenoid. We then analyze the linear stability of this solution and verify the predicted instability rate numerically in Sec. III C. In Sec. IV we study generic equal-strength co-rotating pairs, showing that the reduced dynamics produces bounded meridional oscillations together with a secular azimuthal drift. The quadrature reduction and drift reconstruction are tested against full numerical simulations in Sec. IV A. Finally, in Sec. V, we give a brief preview of many-vortex dynamics by showing that a localized same-sign cluster remains compact while drifting coherently around the catenoid.

> VI. CONCLUSION

We have developed a Hamiltonian formulation of point-vortex dynamics on a catenoid and used it to obtain an explicit reduction of the co-rotating two-vortex problem. Starting from the global catenoid parametrization, we derived the Green’s function, the interaction Hamiltonian, the symplectic form, and the associated equations of motion in the coordi-nates ( v, u ). The geometry enters the dynamics in two distinct ways: through the pairwise interaction kernel and through the curvature-induced Robin term. The latter produces a self-induced azimuthal velocity proportional to tanh( v/a ) sech 2(v/a ) and is responsible for several effects absent in the planar problem. The rotational symmetry of the catenoid yields a conserved momentum J, in addition to the Hamiltonian H. For two vortices, these two invariants allow a Liouville reduction of the dynamics. The momentum constraint determines the collective meridional coordinate as a function of the relative separation, while the energy constraint determines the relative azimuthal angle. The remaining relative motion is governed by a single quadrature. This 23 reduction shows explicitly that the two-vortex dynamics on the catenoid is integrable, al-though the collective and relative degrees of freedom do not decouple in the same way as in the planar problem because of the nonuniform conformal factor of the surface. For two identical co-rotating vortices, we identified an exact antipodal solution in which both vortices remain at a fixed latitude and rotate rigidly around the symmetry axis. The angular velocity is Ω( V ) = Γ4πa 2 tanh( V /a ) sech 2(V /a ).

This motion is entirely curvature induced. It vanishes at the throat and in the asymptotically flat regions, and its extrema occur at V = ±(a/ 2) ln(2 + √3). Equivalently, the angular velocity can be written in terms of the Gaussian curvature as Ω( V ) = Γ16 πK′(V )

p−K(V ).

Thus the rigid rotation is controlled by the curvature gradient rather than by the curvature itself. We also analyzed the linear stability of this symmetric rotating state. The collective meridional perturbation is neutral at linear order, while the relative perturbations form a hyperbolic pair. The instability rate is 

λ = √3 |Ω( V0)|,

so the same curvature-induced rotation that drives the rigid motion also fixes the exponen-tial growth rate of the unstable mode. Direct numerical integration of the full nonlinear equations confirms this prediction and reproduces the growth rate with high accuracy for sufficiently small perturbations. Beyond the symmetric solution, we studied generic equal-strength co-rotating pairs. In this case the relative separation undergoes bounded oscillations, while the mean azimuthal coordinate exhibits a secular drift. Using the Hamiltonian and momentum constraints, we reconstructed both the relative meridional motion and the mean azimuthal drift from the reduced one-dimensional dynamics. The numerical simulations agree with the reduced theory, including the branch changes at turning points. This confirms that the observed drift is not an additional independent degree of freedom, but is completely determined by the conserved quantities and the reduced relative motion. 24 Finally, we presented a preliminary many-vortex calculation showing that a localized same-sign cluster remains compact while its center drifts azimuthally around the catenoid. This provides evidence that the curvature-induced transport mechanism identified for vortex pairs persists in the collective dynamics of vortex clusters, with additional corrections arising from the internal shape of the packet. A systematic reduced theory of such many-vortex cluster motion, together with detailed numerical tests, will be developed in future work [35]. Taken together, these results show that the catenoid provides a minimal geometry in which curvature-induced vortex transport, exact integrability of the two-body problem, and instability of symmetric co-rotating states can be studied analytically. The explicit depen-dence of the dynamics on the curvature gradient suggests a broader organizing principle for vortex motion on nonuniform curved surfaces.
