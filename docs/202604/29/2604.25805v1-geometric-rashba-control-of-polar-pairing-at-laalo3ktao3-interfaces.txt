# Geometric Rashba Control of Polar Pairing at LaAlO 3/KTaO 3 Interfaces 

Yi Zhou 1, ∗

> 1

Institute of Physics, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing 100190, China 

(Dated: April 29, 2026) At LaAlO 3/KTaO 3 interfaces, the superconducting Tc exhibits a striking quasi-linear dependence on crystallographic orientation, coexisting with switchable polar nanoregions (PNRs). We propose an effective minimal Eliashberg framework in which overdamped PNR fluctuations provide the pairing glue, while geometric Rashba coupling controls its angular dependence. Within a reduced isotropic helicity-band description, the dynamic Rashba vertex scales as sin( θ), yielding a pairing strength λ(θ) = λ0 + C sin 2(θ). Exact Matsubara-Eliashberg numerical solutions show that this non-linear mapping naturally yields the same qualitative quasi-linear Tc(θ) dependence within the reduced model. Because the Rashba-activated polar channel is amplified by the large atomic spin-orbit coupling of Ta 5 d orbitals, the same framework also rationalizes why KTaO 3 interfaces exhibit both a much stronger orientational dependence and a substantially higher Tc scale than their SrTiO 3

counterparts. 

Introduction.— The discovery of two-dimensional superconductivity at KTaO 3 (KTO)-based oxide in-terfaces, including LaAlO 3/KTaO 3 (LAO/KTO), has opened a new frontier in the study of emergent interfa-cial quantum matter. A striking feature of LAO/KTO is the pronounced role of crystallographic geometry: the su-perconducting transition temperature Tc varies strongly with interface orientation, reaching its largest values on high-index facets while collapsing at the pristine (100) surface [1–4]. At the same time, KTO interfaces exhibit a substantially higher Tc scale than their SrTiO 3 (STO) counterparts [5–7]. Since polar modes are already known to provide an important pairing channel in STO [8], the central question is why their superconducting impact ap-pears so strongly amplified—and so strongly geometry-dependent—in KTO. Recently, two physically intertwined experimental ob-servations have sharpened this puzzle. First, the pro-nounced orientation dependence of Tc—which collapses at the pristine (001) surface and is maximized on the (111) facet—has been established as a universal feature of KTO interfaces, governing both oxide-capped and ionic-liquid-gated systems [9]. Across many high-index facets, this Tc exhibits a striking quasi-linear dependence on the geometric angle θ relative to the (100) plane [4]. Sec-ond, direct evidence of coexisting, switchable ferroelec-tricity has recently been reported at the interface [10, 11]. Low-temperature transport hysteresis and Raman scat-tering confirm the collective ordering of polar nanore-gions (PNRs) below ∼ 50 K [10], while piezoresponse force microscopy (PFM) reveals that this robust out-of-plane polarization strongly couples to the superconduct-ing state [11]. Taken together, these observations suggest that the relevant pairing channel should be simultane-ously (i) tied to a soft interfacial polar mode associated with PNR dynamics and (ii) strongly modulated by the broken inversion symmetry of the interface. Previous theories based on purely electronic monopole-dipole interactions [12] identified the importance of parity mixing and spin-orbit coupling (SOC). However, purely electronic mechanisms lack a soft low-energy boson and therefore remain deep in the weak-coupling Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer regime, making it difficult to account naturally for either the enhanced Tc scale of KTO or the observed quasi-linear Tc(θ) relation. In this Letter, we propose an effective phenomenological Eliashberg frame-work in which overdamped interfacial PNR fluctuations provide the pairing glue, while a geometrically controlled Rashba vertex governs its angular dependence. Our cen-tral thesis is that the same SOC-amplified polar pairing channel can account simultaneously for the strong ori-entational dependence of superconductivity in KTO and for its much larger Tc scale relative to STO. The slow, overdamped lattice dynamics of PNRs pro-vide substantial low-energy spectral weight, allowing the system to access the intermediate-to-strong coupling regime. At the same time, the geometric angle controls the leading-order dynamic Rashba coupling as sin( θ), generating an orientation-dependent electron-boson ver-tex. Within a projected helicity-band description, this interaction reduces to an effective scalar intra-band pair-ing kernel. Evaluated via both exact numerical Eliash-berg solutions and the Allen-Dynes interpolation for-mula, the resulting non-linear transformation of λ(θ) ∝

sin 2(θ) yields the same qualitative quasi-linear depen-dence within this reduced isotropic model. 

Nature of the Soft Bosonic Mode.— Bulk KTO is a quantum paraelectric where the transverse opti-cal (TO) phonon saturates at ∼ 3 meV [13]. While pre-existing polar nanoregions (PNRs) can form deep within the insulating KTO substrate, their polarizations are randomly oriented without long-range correlation. At the amorphous-LaAlO 3/KTaO 3 (a-LAO/KTO) inter-face, however, the confining potential creates a strong built-in electric field. This field explicitly breaks inver-sion symmetry and aligns the interfacial PNRs, forcing them to form static electric dipoles normal to the sur-face. Geometric Tuning and Helicity Projection.— In bulk KTO, inversion symmetry forbids the linear cou-pling of even-parity conduction electrons to odd-parity polar modes. At the LAO/KTO interface, the asym-metric confining potential breaks this inversion symme-try. While the exact microscopic description of arbi-trary ( hkl ) interfaces is notoriously complex due to highly anisotropic orbital hopping and confinement reordering, the functional form of the leading-order electron-boson coupling is strictly dictated by dynamic point-group se-lection rules. At the pristine (100) interface, parity strictly forbids linear dipole coupling between the primary in-plane dyz 

ground state (even parity) and the out-of-plane polar fluctuation (odd parity). The dynamic pairing vertex must therefore vanish. As derived analytically in Sup-plementary Note 1, cutting the crystal at an angle θ ge-ometrically tilts the bulk t2g orbitals out of the plane, breaking this orthogonal symmetry. Threshold Bounds and Quasi-Linearity.— As il-lustrated schematically in Fig. 2(b), the emergence of superconductivity requires the geometrically enhanced pairing strength to overcome the repulsive Coulomb pseu-dopotential ( λ > λ c ≈ μ∗). A fundamental constraint provided by the experimental data is that the pristine (100) interface ( θ = 0 ◦) remains non-superconducting down to a base temperature of 25 mK. Evaluating the threshold condition Tc ≲ 25 mK places a practical up-per bound on the allowable baseline coupling: λ0 ≲

0.43. Our representative baseline λ0 ≈ 0.25 lies com-fortably below this limit and near the effective Coulomb threshold λc ≈ 0.14. At θ = 0◦, the representa-tive near-threshold parameters yield transition temper-atures of order 10 −6–10 −5 K in Allen-Dynes and exact isotropic Eliashberg theory, respectively, both far be-low the experimental base temperature and therefore consistent with the observed collapse of superconduc-tivity. In the intermediate-to-strong coupling regime (λ > λ c), the non-linear strong-coupling dependence of the Eliashberg/Allen-Dynes mapping partially compen-sates for the concavity of the sin 2(θ) geometric projec-tion. The combination of these two exponential forms maps the quadratic input into an extended quasi-linear 

Tc(θ) output over the physical domain.

Discussion and Conclusion.— We emphasize that our exact numerical calculations validate the physics of this reduced scalar isotropic model; a complete micro-5scopic description would require incorporating the full multiband t2g orbital structure, facet-dependent confine-ment, and Fermi surface anisotropy. Within this effec-tive framework, however, the central physical message is clear: a geometrically controlled dynamic Rashba ver-tex can strongly amplify pairing by soft interfacial polar modes. An important consequence is that the same micro-scopic ingredient — the large atomic spin-orbit coupling of Ta 5 d orbitals — helps explain both why KTaO 3 inter-faces exhibit a much stronger orientational dependence than SrTiO 3 interfaces and why their superconducting transition temperatures are substantially higher. Be-cause the dynamic Rashba vertex g(θ) is parametrically amplified by the heavier Ta atoms ( ξ5d ∼ 400 meV vs 

ξ3d ∼ 20 meV [22, 23]), the corresponding polar pairing enhancement δλ (θ) ∝ sin 2(θ) is expected to be substan-tially larger in KTO. Even allowing for strong material-dependent renormalizations due to band hybridization, this strong parametric amplification naturally renders the orientation-dependent channel weak in STO while making it dominant in KTO. Our mechanism yields experimentally testable quan-titative fingerprints that can distinguish it from purely electronic pairing theories [12] as well as isotropic bulk paraelectric phonon models [24]. First, because pairing is mediated by a parity-breaking Rashba vertex, the re-sulting superconducting state naturally requires a mixed-parity gap structure [16]. As a heuristic estimate, the gap admixture ratio scales as ∆ odd /∆even ∼ αRkF /E F .Using typical ARPES parameters for KTO ( αRkF ∼ 10 meV, EF ∼ 40 meV [17, 22]), we predict a parity mixing amplitude of ∼ 20–25% at the (111) facet, which is con-tinuously tunable with crystallographic orientation and accessible to scanning tunneling spectroscopy. Second, because the bosonic glue originates from Ta-O bond dis-tortions within the PNRs, the framework predicts a sub-stantial oxygen isotope exponent α = −d ln Tc/d ln MO ≈

0.5 (subject to intermediate-coupling corrections), dis-tinguishing a lattice-driven boson from purely electronic plasmons. Third, the threshold and intercept of the Tc(θ)curve should be tunable through electrostatic boundary conditions that modify the interfacial electric field and PNR correlation length. While a fully self-consistent treatment beyond the Migdal-Eliashberg approximation would be necessary to capture exact quasiparticle residues in the strong-coupling regime ( λ ≳ 1), the geometric mechanism un-derlying the Tc scaling remains robust. In conclusion, we have presented an effective minimal mechanism for su-perconductivity at LAO/KTO interfaces in which a geo-metrically controlled Rashba vertex activates pairing by overdamped interfacial polar modes. Exact Matsubara-Eliashberg calculations show that, within the reduced isotropic model, this mechanism naturally maps geomet-ric orbital tilt into the observed quasi-linear Tc(θ) de-pendence, while also providing a unified rationale for why KTaO 3 interfaces are both more orientation-sensitive and more strongly superconducting than their SrTiO 3 coun-terparts. > SUPPLEMENTARY NOTE 1: BASIS ROTATION, THE FUNDAMENTAL DOMAIN, AND DIPOLE MATRIX ELEMENTS

In standard crystallographic convention, any arbitrary facet ( hkl ) can be mapped to a physically equivalent facet within the fundamental stereographic domain defined by h ≥ k ≥ l ≥ 0. The crystallographic orientation is uniquely defined by the polar angle θ between the interface normal ˆ n and the [100] axis: θ = arccos( h/ √h2 + k2 + l2). Because h is the largest index in this domain, the maximum possible polar angle occurs for the (111) facet, where h = k = l, yielding θmax = arccos(1 /√3) ≈ 54 .7◦. > SUPPLEMENTARY NOTE 2: MICROSCOPIC DERIVATION OF THE DYNAMIC RASHBA VERTEX AND HELICITY BASIS PROJECTION

In this section, we derive the electron-boson interaction vertex starting from the fundamental relativistic spin-orbit coupling (SOC) Hamiltonian. We show that amplitude fluctuations of the out-of-plane polar nanoregions generate a dynamic Rashba interaction. By projecting this matrix-valued vertex onto the Rashba-split helicity bands, we motivate the reduction of the intra-band interaction to a scalar-dominant kernel.

4. Caveat Regarding the Spinor Overlap Factor 

We note that the exact projection of the Rashba vertex between different momentum states k and k′ introduces an angular spinor overlap factor, proportional to cos[( ϕk − ϕk′ )/2]. This angular dependence is physically profound, as it dictates the exact symmetry admixture of s-wave and p-wave pairing components inherent to non-centrosymmetric superconductors. However, explicitly retaining this momentum-selective overlap converts the Eliashberg gap equations into a com-putationally intensive multi-channel anisotropic problem. By replacing this factor with an isotropic Fermi-surface average, our scalar reduction operates as an effective model. This reduction is deliberately designed to efficiently iso-late the overall integrated pairing energy scale ( λ) responsible for the strong-coupling Tc enhancement, while explicitly sacrificing the ability to resolve the exact mixed-parity gap structure. Fully momentum-dependent Nambu-Gorkov studies will be required in future work to map the precise momentum-space gap symmetry.

> SUPPLEMENTARY NOTE 6: DETAILS OF THE ALLEN-DYNES CURVE FITTING

To quantitatively connect our theoretical framework to the experimental data, we performed non-linear least-squares fits of the Allen-Dynes equation. Rather than treating Ω log and f2 as independent free constants, we constrained the fit by explicitly integrating the overdamped Brownian spectrum α2F (ω) at each optimization step to compute the corresponding spectral moments. The fitting dataset comprises the standard amorphous-LAO/KTO interfaces shown by the blue symbols in Fig. 4 of the main text; the ionic-liquid-gated points (orange stars) are displayed for comparison only and are not included in the fit because they represent a distinct electrostatic environment. The full dataset used in the main-text fit includes the non-superconducting (100) point. The resulting fitting landscape is strongly covariant. In particular, both a near-threshold family of solutions with relatively small λ0 and large C, and a higher-baseline family with somewhat smaller geometric enhancement, can provide reasonable descriptions of the data. In the main text, we focus on the physically motivated near-threshold family because it is most naturally connected to the physical collapse of superconductivity at the pristine (100) interface and to the interpretation of the geometric Rashba channel as the dominant orientation-dependent enhancement. Within this near-threshold regime and for a representative value μ∗ = 0 .13, we use the representative parameters Ω0 ≈ 9.88 K (0 .85 meV) ,λ0 ≈ 0.25 ,C ≈ 2.88 .

These values should be understood as representative parameters within the physically relevant near-threshold regime rather than as uniquely extracted quantities. The physically relevant feature is that this regime preserves a weak baseline channel together with a strong geometrically activated polar enhancement.

> SUPPLEMENTARY NOTE 8: CROSSOVER PROBLEM AND METHODOLOGICAL CAVEATS

The Migdal parameter λΩ0/E F ∼ 0.05 ≪ 1 ensures that vertex corrections to the electron-boson coupling are small at the diagrammatic level, motivating the use of the Migdal-Eliashberg framework. However, we note an important methodological caveat: in the intermediate-to-strong coupling regime ( λ ∼ 1–2), the quasiparticle residue 

Z−1 = 1 /(1 + λ) is significantly reduced, making the concept of well-defined Fermi liquid quasiparticles increasingly approximate. Furthermore, for overdamped soft modes, a unique propagating phonon frequency is ill-defined, meaning vertex corrections could depend on more than a simple single-frequency Migdal ratio. A fully self-consistent treatment beyond Migdal-Eliashberg theory would be desirable to confirm the pairing. Nevertheless, the qualitative prediction of a quasi-linear Tc(θ) dependence remains robust, as it relies fundamentally on the geometric projection of the vertex and the general mathematical character of the intermediate-coupling transformation.

1. Constrained sensitivity to the Coulomb pseudopotential μ∗

In the main text, we fix μ∗ = 0 .13 as a representative value for a strongly screened oxide interface. Because μ∗ and the attractive pairing strength enter the Allen-Dynes exponent in a correlated way, a full nonlinear refit causes the effective parameters to covary. Our central physical conclusion, however, does not rely on a unique parameter set: it is the existence of an extended quasi-linear Tc(θ) regime generated when the intermediate-coupling non-linearity acts on the geometric input λ(θ) = λ0 + C sin 2 θ.Experimentally, the pristine (100) interface remains non-superconducting down to the base temperature, so in the main text we focus on the near-threshold regime of the reduced model as the physically relevant one. 2. Comparison with empirical alternatives 

One may ask whether simpler empirical forms can describe the same dataset without invoking the reduced Eliashberg framework. To address this, we compare the present fit against two representative alternatives: (i) a purely linear fit, 

Tc = aθ + b, and (ii) a thresholded quadratic fit, Tc = A max[sin 2(θ) − θ0, 0], where θ0 is an effective onset threshold. A purely empirical linear fit captures the approximate trend at intermediate and large angles, but it fails to represent the collapse of superconductivity near the pristine (100) plane unless one allows an ad hoc intercept or piecewise cutoff. Indeed, removing the (100) point substantially improves the linear-fit residual, indicating that the non-superconducting (100) interface is precisely where a naive linear phenomenology breaks down. By contrast, the thresholded quadratic form captures the near-threshold suppression more effectively and yields a slightly smaller raw summed squared residual than the reduced Eliashberg fit. However, it remains purely descriptive: it does not provide a bosonic pairing scale, a geometric Rashba pairing vertex, or a natural threshold mechanism tied to Coulomb competition. The value of the reduced Eliashberg framework is instead that it unifies the threshold and quasi-linear regimes within a physically motivated pairing scenario.
