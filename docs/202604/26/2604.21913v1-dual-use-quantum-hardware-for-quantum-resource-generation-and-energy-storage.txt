(Dated: April 24, 2026) Quantum resources such as entanglement form the backbone of quantum technologies and their ef-ficient generation is a central objective of modern quantum platforms. Independently, quantum bat-teries have emerged as nanoscale devices that utilize collective quantum effects to store energy with a charging advantage over classical strategies. Here, we show that these two pursuits can co-exist: protocols for fast generation of resourceful quantum states can simultaneously charge a quantum battery with a collective advantage, and conversely, a quantum battery protocol with a charging ad-vantage can produce resource-rich states. Using this connection, we propose an integrated hardware protocol on superconducting circuits in which each experimental run can interchangeably accom-plish either quantum battery charging, or quantum sensing through generation of metrologically useful states. Our results establish that quantum resources and stored energy are distinct yet co-producable quantities, opening the door to modular quantum architectures that dynamically switch between sensing and energy-storage functions, thereby producing additional functionalities without extra hardware cost. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Quantum technologies draw their power from uniquely non-classical features of quantum mechanics to achieve advantages in computation, communication, and sens-ing [1, 2]. Entanglement, coherence, and non-Gaussianity have all been formalized as resources within the frame-work of quantum resource theories [3], and their efficient generation on physical hardware underpins the perfor-mance of every branch of quantum technology. For in-stance, quantum-enhanced metrology, that informs quan-tum sensing protocols, utilizes entanglement as a re-source to surpass the standard quantum limit, achieving Heisenberg-limited precision in parameter estimation [4– 8]. Concurrently, the energy required to operate quan-tum hardware at scale has become a pressing concern, and motivates a unified approach that connects quan-tum thermodynamics, information science, and engineer-ing in order to ensure energy-efficient, sustainable quan-tum technologies [9]. Quantum batteries—nanoscale de-vices that store and release energy by harnessing collec-tive quantum effects—have emerged as a natural arena where resource-theoretic and energetic perspectives con-verge [10–12]. Theoretical work has demonstrated that many-body or collective effects can yield super-extensive scaling of charging power which implies that the time re-quired to charge a many-body quantum battery can be reduced with increasing number of battery units [13–20]. This inverse relationship between battery-size and charg-ing time constitutes a charging advantage. Experimental realizations of quantum batteries have now spanned mul-tiple platforms, from organic semiconductor microcavi-ties to superconducting transmon qubits, and together

In particular, in quantum metrology, one seeks to evolve an unentangled initial state into a metrologically use-ful target state in the shortest possible time through fast state-preparation protocols, thereby minimizing ex-posure to decoherence [25–28]. Our paper is organized as follows: In Sec. II, we first show how fast state preparation and quantum battery charging are related with a coexistence of quantum re-source generation and energy injection. In Sec. III, we use a system of two coupled superconducting LC resonators to concretely illustrate this connection and demonstrate that fast-charging of a quantum battery results in fast preparation of states with high quantum Fisher infor-mation and squeezing. In Sec. IV, we propose a dual-use hardware set-up that combines quantum sensing and quantum battery charging. Finally, we conclude with a discussion on how our dual-use scheme, combined with the recent theoretical and experimental advances, points towards a broader paradigm of multi-use quantum hard-ware, where a single device can serve multiple functions without additional overhead.  

> II. RELATING STATE PREPARATION PROTOCOLS AND QUANTUM BATTERIES

In quantum state preparation, one starts with a sim-ple initial state, |ψi⟩, with the goal to evolve the system into a final state, |ψf ⟩, that exhibits strictly non-classical features such as topological order [29–32], quantum com-putational utility [33, 34] and enhanced metrological ca-pability [35–39]. The preparation protocol can be written as 

|ψf ⟩ = ˆV (T )|ψi⟩. (1) Here ˆV (T ) is a time-dependent operator that transforms the initial state into the final state after evolution time 

T . It can be chosen to be either unitary [29, 36, 40] or non-unitary [41–44]. Generally, the goal of any state preparation protocol is to minimize the preparation time 

T in order to avoid the inevitable effects of decoherence and noise before the target state is utilized. This goal of fast state preparation connects it to the quest for charg-ing advantage in quantum batteries. To map this to a quantum battery, we can interpret the initial state, |ψi⟩ as the low energy uncharged start-ing state of a quantum battery. As |ψi⟩ is typically chosen to be an easy-to-prepare state such as a product state of spins, we can find a Hamiltonian, ˆHB, such that |ψi⟩ is a low energy eigenstate of ˆHB. This makes ˆHB our quan-tum battery Hamiltonian. The state preparation opera-tor, ˆV (T ) can be simply mapped to the charging protocol of a quantum battery. Finally, |ψf ⟩ can be interpreted as a charged quantum state as long as the following con-dition is satisfied: ∆E = ⟨ψf | ˆHB|ψf ⟩ − ⟨ ψi| ˆHB|ψi⟩ > 0. (2) This implies that the charging protocol ˆV (T ) has success-fully charged the quantum battery by supplying energy ∆E in time T . Therefore, in this process of preparing the final state |ψf ⟩, we have produced both quantum re-sources and energy storage. For a system of N spins or particles, when ∆ E/T scales as N α with α > 1, the corresponding quantum battery is said to have a charg-ing advantage [45]. > III. METROLOGICALLY USEFUL STATES FROM A QUANTUM BATTERY

Just as fast state preparation protocols can be re-interpreted as fast charging of a quantum battery, so too can well-known quantum battery models with charg-ing advantage provide templates for fast preparation of resourceful quantum states. Paradigmatic examples of resourceful states include states with high amount of quantum Fisher information (QFI) and quantum squeez-ing [49]. These states enable precise measurements that can surpass the standard quantum limit imposed by the Heisenberg uncertainty principle [38, 39]. Their metro-logical advantage stems from multipartite entanglement, and hence these features also serve as entanglement wit-nesses [50–52]. As exemplified below, the same collec-tive quantum processes that produce charging advan-tage in quantum batteries [19] also build multipartite entanglement, making them well-suited to produce use-ful quantum states during the charging (or discharging) processes. > V. CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK

Our work lays the foundation for building multi-use quantum technological platforms by adding an indepen-dent direction in the pursuit of on-chip energy source for quantum hardware [59]. In particular, we have discussed the general ingredients required to achieve quantum bat-tery charging and quantum resource generation from the same underlying dynamics. When applied to a model of two coupled superconducting LC resonators with com-mensurate mode frequencies, we showed that the non-linear charger-battery interaction generates states at in-termediate times with super-extensive scaling of QFI and two-mode squeezing, depending on the choice of the ini-tial state. We proposed a hardware protocol in which each experimental run on this device can interchangeably accomplish either quantum sensing or quantum battery charging. Broadly, the notion of dual-use developed here is not restricted to LC resonator architectures: any platform supporting non-linear bosonic interactions or collective spin dynamics, eg. trapped ions, photonic systems, and solid-state spin ensembles, could host analogous proto-cols, enabling a comparative benchmarking. Our results on dual-use quantum hardware for sensing and energy storage, combined with recent work on other dual-use formalisms, enables an extension into a multi-use quan-tum hardware paradigm. In particular, recent work has shown how quantum batteries can be connected to quan-tum computers to power gate operations for quantum processing [59]. In parallel, Ref. [60] has demonstrated how quantum algorithms running on a quantum com-puter can be used to perform enhanced sensing of oscillat-ing fields with a new lower bound on sensing time. Thus, quantum hardware can be adapted to multiple uses, each connected to the other as shown by Fig. 4. For instance, we can envision a set-up involving multiple devices such as flux tunable transmons [61] or kinemons [62] that can be tuned to operate either as qubits or harmonic res-onators. By implementing the model we introduced in Sec. III, we can use some of them to perform QFI based quantum sensing as described earlier in this paper. Be-cause the sensing procedure still leaves us with a partially charged battery, we can use that energy to power another set of devices acting as qubits for a quantum computer. The quantum computer can then be used to sense weak oscillating signals using the formalism of Ref [60]. As il-lustrated in Fig. 4, this completes a trifecta of multi-use capabilities of current quantum hardware.
