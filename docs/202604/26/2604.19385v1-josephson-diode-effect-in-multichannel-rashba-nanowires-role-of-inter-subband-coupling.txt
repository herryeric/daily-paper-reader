# Josephson diode effect in multichannel Rashba nanowires: role of inter-subband coupling 

Ardamon Sten 1, ∗ and Sudeep Kumar Ghosh 1, †

Department of Physics, Indian Institute of Technology, Kanpur 208016, India 

(Dated: April 22, 2026) The Josephson diode effect (JDE) has attracted considerable attention for its ability to en-able directional, dissipationless supercurrents in quantum devices. While hybrid semiconductor-superconductor nanowire Josephson junctions provide a canonical platform, most theoretical treat-ments assume the single-channel limit of the nanowire; however, realistic devices are inherently multichannel due to transverse confinement. Here, we investigate the JDE in multichannel Rashba nanowire Josephson junctions, focusing on the role of inter-subband coupling. We show that sub-band hybridization qualitatively modifies both the topological phase diagram and the JDE response of the device. In contrast to the single-channel case, the topological phase is restricted to a finite window of Zeeman fields, within which Majorana bound states lead to a strong enhancement of the diode efficiency. Crucially, inter-subband coupling enables a finite JDE even when the Zeeman field is aligned purely along the spin-orbit direction– a mechanism absent in independent-channel and strictly one-dimensional systems. Furthermore, multichannel coupling enhances spectral asymmetry and significantly increases the diode efficiency compared to single-channel junctions. These results identify multichannel hybridization as a key ingredient for realizing and optimizing nonreciprocal superconducting transport in experimentally relevant hybrid semiconductor nanowire Josephson junctions. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Nonreciprocal superconducting transport has emerged as a central theme in modern quantum materials, offer-ing a dissipationless route toward directional electronic functionalities. A primary manifestation of this behavior is the superconducting diode effect (SDE) [1–3], where the critical current differs for opposite bias directions. Microscopically, such nonreciprocity originates from the interplay of broken time-reversal and inversion symme-tries, which can give rise to magnetochiral anisotropy and finite-momentum pairing states [4–9]. An alterna-tive route to intrinsic nonreciprocal transport can also be achieved in noncentrosymmetric superconductors via spontaneous time-reversal symmetry breaking [10–15]. In this respect, Josephson junctions (JJs) [16–18] pro-vide a versatile and controllable platform for realizing the SDE, as demonstrated in recent experiments [19– 21]. In these systems, the Josephson diode effect (JDE) manifests as an asymmetry of the current-phase relation (CPR), i.e., I(ϕ)̸ = −I(−ϕ), leading to unequal posi-tive and negative critical currents. Such asymmetry typ-ically arises in junctions where spin-orbit coupling (SOC) and an external magnetic field jointly break the relevant symmetries [22, 23], thereby inducing a phase-dependent spectral asymmetry in the Andreev bound states. The JDE has been investigated across a diverse range of platforms, including finite-momentum superconduc-tors [4, 22, 24], quantum-dot junctions [25–27], topologi-cal insulators [28–32], altermagnets [33–36] and systems

Among these, semiconductor-superconductor hybrid nanowires are a promising platform as they natu-rally combine strong SOC, proximity-induced supercon-ductivity, and magnetic tunability, while also hosting Majorana bound states (MBSs) [48–51] that can strongly influence the diode response. Existing theoretical works [38, 52, 53] have shown that MBSs can enhance the JDE, but they typically rely on an effective single-channel one-dimensional (1D) description. In realistic nanowires, however, transverse confinement leads to multiple occu-pied subbands and finite inter-subband coupling [54, 55]. This multichannel structure is not merely a quantitative correction but can qualitatively modify both the topo-logical properties and the transport response [55]. This raises a central question: how does the multichannel na-ture of a quasi-one-dimensional Josephson junction re-shape the topological phase diagram and the resulting JDE, and can it generate mechanisms for nonreciprocal transport that are absent in single-channel systems? In this work, we address this question by investigat-ing the Josephson diode effect in multichannel Rashba nanowire Josephson junctions, explicitly incorporating the role of inter-subband coupling. We show that sub-band hybridization qualitatively modifies the topological phase diagram and the nonreciprocal supercurrent, lead-ing to a finite topological regime as a function of Zee-man field and a pronounced enhancement of the diode response. Most importantly, we find that inter-subband coupling enables a finite diode effect even when the Zee-man field is oriented purely along the spin-orbit direc-tion, a feature absent in independent-channel and strictly one-dimensional limits. At lower values of the Zee-man field, the spectrum is fully gapped and as the Zee-man field increases the spectrum gap reduces and reaches the first critical point where a gap closing occurs which signifies a topological phase transition from a trivial to non-trivial topological phase. Beyond the first critical point, a pair of zero-energy states (ZES) emerge which correspond to a pair of MBSs located at the ends of the nanowire. Furthermore, as the Zeeman field is further increased, a second critical point is reached where the gap reopens and the system undergoes a second phase transition from the topological to the trivial phase where the two MBSs emerge as finite energy modes. This is a direct consequence of the spectral even-odd effect [58– 60] where the MBSs are observed only at odd number of occupied subbands, beyond the second critical point the number of occupied subbands changes from one to two, therefore the two MBSs hybridize to form two non-zero trivial states. This signifies that a multichannel super-conducting nanowire supports only a finite topological regime due to hybridization of the spectrum of the two subbands, this characteristic is unique to the multichan-nel case as compared to the single-channel where there is only one phase transition from trivial to topological at the critical field Bc = pμ2 + ∆ 2. We also note that the critical field for topological phase transition is no longer given by pμ2 + ∆ 2 in the case of multichannels with finite interband coupling. To further demonstrate the fi-nite topological regime of the multichannel nanowire, we present the topological phase diagram in Fig. 2(d) as a function of the Zeeman energy and chemical potential where the blue and red regions represent the trivial and topological regimes respectively. The topological regime strongly depends on the chemical potential and Zeeman field, the two branches of the topological regime at low and high chemical potential correspond to the non-trivial phase arising from the occupancy of one and three sub-bands, respectively. In both the topological branches, further increasing the Zeeman field results in a transition back to the trivial regime due to change of occupancy from odd to even number of subbands, thus featuring a finite topological regime. > IX. CONCLUSIONS

In this work, we have theoretically characterized the Josephson diode effect in multichannel semiconductor-superconductor hybrid nanowire Josephson junctions, fo-cusing on the interplay between a tilted Zeeman field and transverse confinement. Our results show that inter-subband coupling, mediated by spin-orbit interactions, modifies the topological phase diagram since the topo-logical phase is realized only when an odd number of subbands are occupied, thereby confining the Majorana-supporting regime to a finite range of Zeeman energy. We demonstrate that subband hybridization leads to a pro-nounced asymmetry in the Andreev bound state spec-trum with respect to the superconducting phase differ-ence, which underlies the emergence of nonreciprocal su-percurrents. A notable consequence of this multichan-nel physics is the appearance of a finite diode effect for a Zeeman field oriented purely along the spin-orbit direction, a feature absent in strictly one-dimensional or independent-channel limits. By comparing single-channel, independent-channel, and interacting-channel regimes, we find that the diode efficiency is enhanced in the multichannel junction for both the interacting and independent channels, particularly in the topolog-ical regime where Majorana bound states contribute to the nonreciprocal response. In the former, the enhance-ment comes from the increased asymmetry in the energy spectrum due to the inter-subband coupling while in the latter the enhancement is due to the presence of addi-tional MBSs in the topological regime. The qualitative consistency of these features across different confinement potentials further supports the robustness of the under-lying mechanism. More broadly, our results highlight that moving beyond idealized single-channel descriptions is essential for understanding realistic hybrid nanowire devices. The multichannel nature of these systems not only affects quantitative aspects of transport but also enables qualitatively distinct behavior arising from subband hybridization. In this context, controlling the subband structure and inter-subband coupling provides a natural route for tuning nonreciprocal superconducting re-sponses. Our findings offer a framework for interpreting experiments in multichannel nanowires and point toward the potential of leveraging multiband effects in designing nonreciprocal superconducting devices. We expect that the interplay between multichannel topology and non-reciprocity explored here will motivate further studies in this direction and contribute to the development of superconducting circuits with enhanced directional functionalities.

Appendix C: Josephson diode effect in the tunneling regime 

The diode effect is investigated in the main text in the limit of a fully transparent ( τ = 1 ) junction. Recent studies in the pure 1D junction reveal that the critical currents crucially depend on the transmission coefficient; in the tunneling regime the critical current exhibits a re-entrant behavior where it vanishes below the critical field and suddenly becomes non-zero in the topological regime [63]. This gives rise to a JDE which is dominated by the MBSs in the tunneling regime. In the interacting multichannel junction, the symmetric low-energy spectrum for θ = 0

> c

Indeed, the efficiency exhibits a strong dependence on the transmission coefficient as seen in Fig. 16(d) where the efficiency is plotted as a function of the Zeeman energy.
