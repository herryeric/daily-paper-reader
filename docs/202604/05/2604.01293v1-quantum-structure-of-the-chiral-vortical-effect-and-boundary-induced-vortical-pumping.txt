## Quantum structure of the chiral vortical effect and boundary-induced vortical pumping. 

Boqun Song 1, 2 and Pavan Hosur 1, 2

(Dated: April 3, 2026) The chiral vortical effect (CVE) ‚Äď an axial current driven by rotation in chiral matter ‚Äď appears in systems ranging from relativistic fluids to Weyl semimetals, yet its quantum origin remains unclear because existing derivations are semiclassical. We present an exact quantum solution of a rotating Weyl fermion in a finite cylinder. We show that the bulk vortical response is entirely a magnetization current while the current density on the rotation axis remains finite and matches semiclassical predictions. For spin-polarized boundary conditions, we uncover an additional effect beyond the known CVE: a robust family of chiral modes that transport axial charge, ‚ąÜ Q = ŌáN 2 ‚ąÜőł/ 4ŌÄ, under rotation by angle ‚ąÜ őł, where Ōá is the Weyl node chirality and N is the number of chiral modes. The pump is independent of temperature, Fermi level and Weyl velocities, but depends on the UV-sensitive number N . These results establish a fully quantum picture of the CVE and reveal a boundary-enforced chiral spectral structure underlying vortical response in Weyl systems. 

The discovery of the integer quantum Hall effect 1,2 ush-ered in the modern era of topological condensed mat-ter physics 3‚Äď14 . It revealed that charge transport, once thought to depend on microscopic details, can become precisely quantized 2,15‚Äď17 . The phenomenon admits an exact quantum-mechanical description in terms of elec-trons confined to discrete Landau levels in a magnetic field and whose robustness is explained by topological invariants 18‚Äď21 , marking the first clear union of topology and quantum matter. Thus, it stands as the prototype of a universal quantum transport phenomenon whose quan-tization stems directly from spectral structure. One of its three-dimensional extensions ‚Äď the chiral magnetic effect (CME) 22‚Äď25 ‚Äď arises from a single chiral Landau level in a magnetic field, producing a current density parallel to the field and linking topology, anomalies, and transport in chiral quantum matter 26‚Äď28 .The chiral vortical effect (CVE) 29‚Äď39 occupies a par-allel position to the classical Hall effect in the physics of rotating chiral matter. Defined as a current gener-ated by vorticity or rotation, it links phenomena from parity-violating heavy-ion collisions and neutrino fluxes from rotating black holes 29 to electron transport in Weyl semimetals 33,34,39 . The CVE is invariably described semiclassically, often as the rotational analogue of the CME, where the Coriolis force in the rotating frame mim-ics the Lorentz force of a magnetic field 40,41 . While this picture captures the correct scaling with angular veloc-ity, it leaves several crucial gaps. First, the CME ad-mits a clear microscopic origin in a chiral Landau level, whereas no comparably transparent quantum mechanism is known for rotation. Secondly, a static magnetic field preserves equilibrium, while a rotating system is neces-sarily out of equilibrium in the laboratory frame, making the thermodynamic status of the CVE murky. Lastly, existing treatments of the CVE ‚Äď whether in high-energy physics or condensed matter ‚Äď are for infinite bulk. How-ever, Weyl fermions in finite systems produce Fermi arc surface states, which can contribute to transport too. What, then, is the fundamental quantum mechanism that produces chiral flow under rotation and how does it manifest in a finite system with boundaries? In this work we answer this question by developing a fully quantum theory of the CVE in a finite system. Starting from the Weyl equation in a rotating frame, we assume a non-equilibrium steady-state distribution that appears thermal to a co-rotating observer. We recover the established semiclassical current density locally on the rotation axis. In addition, we find that the trans-port current density carried by the bulk states vanishes in the thermodynamic limit, implying that the bulk CVE current density is of purely magnetization origin. The presence of a boundary places the system in a qualitatively distinct regime, where boundary-supported modes can contribute to vortical response. While gen-eral boundary conditions render the surface state wave-functions intractable, we observe that a specific type of boundary condition ‚Äď one where the boundary spin is fully polarized ‚Äď yields a set of critical states that have remarkable properties of their own: (i) they are 1D chiral modes; (ii) their wavefunctions have simple, holomorphic closed forms for arbitrary boundary shapes and sizes; and (iii) under rotation, they pump axial charge: ‚ąÜQ = Ōá ‚ąÜőł

Conclusions: We have provided a quantum formula-tion of the CVE, which reveals its precise structure for a Weyl fermion in a finite system and the presence of boundary-induced chiral modes that govern transport. In the bulk, the response is magnetization-like and survives semiclassically as a local current density near the rota-tion axis. A spin-polarized boundary, however, exposes an exactly solvable chiral sector that supports quantized spectral flow and particle pumping under rotation. These results separate the universal and boundary-sensitive as-pects of vortical response: the magnetization character of the bulk current is robust, whereas quantized pumping relies on the existence and occupation of the boundary-induced chiral sector. More broadly, our results show that the quantum structure of vortical response is gov-erned not only by bulk anomaly physics but also by boundary-enforced spectral topology. It would be inter-esting to explore how this structure is modified in lat-tice Weyl semimetals with multiple nodes and Fermi-arc boundary conditions.

Appendix C: Topology of the chiral modes 

In this Appendix, we show that the chiral modes are remarkably general and robust ‚Äď existing for arbitrary spin, Weyl monopole charge, and shape of the cross-section. Under rotation, all these chiral modes pump charge by an amount that ties together quantum geometry in momentum, real and spin space.
