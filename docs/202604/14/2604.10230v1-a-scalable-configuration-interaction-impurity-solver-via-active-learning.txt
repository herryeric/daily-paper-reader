## A Scalable Configuration-Interaction Impurity Solver via Active Learning 

Jeongmoo Lee 1 and Ara Go 1, ∗

> 1

Department of Physics, Chonnam National University, Gwangju, Korea 

Finite-Hamiltonian impurity solvers provide direct real-frequency spectra and a natural route to enlarged impurity Hamiltonians, but their applicability is limited by the rapid Hilbert-space growth with the number of bath or other added one-particle orbitals. We introduce an active-learning extension of adaptive-truncation configuration interaction (AL-ATCI) that identifies the determi-nant manifold relevant to the correlated state. The approximation is systematically controlled by the query size Nquery , which also provides an internal convergence parameter when no external benchmark is available. Over the benchmark range studied here, the computational cost grows only weakly with bath size, because enlarging the bath mainly expands the combinatorial determinant space rather than the physically relevant manifold. In dynamical mean-field-theory benchmarks for the one-dimensional Hubbard model, AL-ATCI reproduces exact-diagonalization accuracy and ex-tends cellular calculations to clusters as large as Nc = 10. For a three-orbital rotationally invariant Sr 2RuO 4 impurity problem, we demonstrate systematic convergence of dynamical quantities and a highly compressed configuration space as Nb is increased from 9 to 18. These results substantially alleviate the bath-discretization bottleneck of exact-diagonalization- and configuration-interaction-based impurity solvers and make large-bath and enlarged-orbital calculations more practical. 

Introduction. —Dynamical mean-field theory (DMFT) is a central framework for treating strong local elec-tronic correlations in quantum materials, with applica-tions ranging from minimal lattice models to realistic electronic-structure calculations. DMFT maps a corre-lated lattice problem onto a quantum impurity model coupled to a self-consistent bath [1, 2]. The quality of the resulting solution hinges on the impurity solver, which must capture many-body correlations faithfully. Continuous-time quantum Monte Carlo solvers [3, 4] remain a powerful and actively developing class of meth-ods, with recent progress on multiorbital problems with general interactions [5] and on steady-state transport [6]. The numerical renormalization group [7, 8] provides ex-ceptional low-energy resolution and continues to yield insights into multiband Hund metals [9, 10] and heavy-fermion quantum criticality [11]; its cost, however, still grows rapidly with the number of active orbitals and screening channels. In this work, we instead exploit the complementary advantages of finite-Hamiltonian solvers based on exact diagonalization (ED) [12–14] and con-figuration interaction (CI) [15–22]. These methods are sign-problem free, work at zero temperature on the real-frequency axis without analytic continuation, and con-struct an explicit finite Anderson impurity Hamiltonian. This explicit Hamiltonian formulation carries a distinc-tive practical advantage alongside its well-known limi-tation. The limitation is that replacing the continuous hybridization function by a fitted finite bath introduces dependence on the bath parametrization, making large 

Nb desirable. The advantage is that once the impurity model is converged, it can be enlarged with additional ligand or core orbitals to compute observables such as x-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) or resonant inelas-tic x-ray scattering (RIXS) within the same Anderson-impurity framework [23–25]. In conventional ED, how-ever, the rapid growth of the Hilbert space makes both directions—larger baths and enlarged impurity models— quickly prohibitive. This motivates solvers that retain the explicit Hamiltonian structure while making added noninteracting orbitals substantially cheaper. Several CI-based truncation and machine-learning se-lection strategies have been developed in quantum chem-istry [26, 27] and have begun to be explored for impu-rity and cluster problems. Recent examples include a CI impurity solver applied to elemental Ce [28] and a neural-network-supported basis optimizer for Anderson impurity CI [29]. Building on these advances, we com-bine active learning with the ATCI framework and show, in self-consistent DMFT, that the selected determinant space is governed by correlation physics rather than by the nominal one-particle dimension. Metallic problems require a broader selected manifold than insulating ones, reflecting their larger entanglement structure, but in both cases the approximation remains systematically control-lable through Nquery . This weak dependence on the total number of bath or other added noninteracting orbitals is important not only for larger bath discretizations but also for enlarged finite-Hamiltonian impurity models. We ver-ify these points on the one-dimensional Hubbard model, reaching cluster sizes up to Nc = 10 in cellular DMFT, and on Sr 2RuO 4, where dynamical quantities converge systematically as Nb is increased from 9 to 18 in a three-orbital rotationally invariant problem. 

Method. —Active-learning adaptive-truncation config-uration interaction (AL-ATCI) builds on the adaptive-truncation configuration interaction (ATCI) frame-work [20], which we use as our baseline. Starting from a restricted active space CI (RASCI) calculation [30], one selects reference configurations—Slater determinants with the largest wavefunction coefficients—and expands the space by particle-hole substitutions (PHS) within the

2active space. The effective Hamiltonian is diagonalized (Lanczos [31] or Davidson [32]), after which the one-body reduced density matrix is diagonalized to obtain natural orbitals, providing an optimal single-particle basis that accelerates convergence [17]. The procedure is iterated until convergence; Green’s functions are computed within the same truncated space. Full algorithmic details are given in Ref. [20]. The main bottleneck of ATCI is the Hamiltonian con-struction and Lanczos diagonalization in the expanded Hilbert space: the candidate space grows rapidly with active-space size, making the subsequent diagonalization increasingly expensive [20]. AL-ATCI addresses this by introducing a targeted selection step before Hamiltonian construction and diagonalization (see Fig. S1 in Supple-mental Material [33]). A random-forest classifier [34, 35], used here as a representative low-overfitting model, is trained on data accumulated from previous iterations to predict the importance of each candidate configuration and rank them by probability [21, 26, 27, 29]. Only the top Nquery configurations are then retained for Hamil-tonian construction and diagonalization; the resulting speedup comes from reducing the dimension of the ex-pensive matrix problem. In the benchmark regime studied here, the ground-state diagonalization dominates the computational cost, so the speedup comes primarily from reducing the dimen-sion of the expensive matrix problem rather than from avoiding PHS enumeration. By screening out less im-portant determinants before the expensive oracle step, AL-ATCI achieves substantially better performance than the earlier ATCI scheme. Relative to previous machine-learning-based determinant-selection studies, our empha-sis here is on integration with the ATCI framework and the resulting bath-size scalability within DMFT. Because 

Nquery is a user-controlled parameter, the accuracy can be systematically improved toward the ED limit. Imple-mentation details—including class-imbalance treatment, binary occupation encoding, symmetry-partner book-keeping, and dataset management across DMFT cycles— are provided in the Supplemental Material [33]. The qualitative difference from ATCI is not merely that fewer configurations are kept, but that the number of essential determinants grows only weakly with bath size over the benchmark range studied. The classifier does not filter orbitals, but ranks candidate Slater deter-minants by relevance. Although enlarging the bath in-creases the number of possible configurations, only a lim-ited subset contributes significantly to the ground-state wavefunction, so adding bath sites incurs comparatively little additional cost.

Application to a multiorbital system. —We now test AL-ATCI on Sr 2RuO 4, a prototypical three-orbital t2g

Hund metal [43, 44]. This serves as a demanding mul-tiorbital impurity benchmark: the solver must handle the full rotationally invariant Slater–Kanamori interac-tion [45] (see Supplemental Material [33] for the explicit Hamiltonian), and the three-orbital structure produces a substantially larger Hilbert space than the single-band case. We use a three-band tight-binding model with hopping parameters and interaction values ( U = 2 .5 eV, 

J = 0 .4 eV) from Ref.[46], fixing the total occupancy at four electrons per unit cell ( d4 filling). Spin–orbit cou-pling is neglected; since Sr 2RuO 4 is known to be sensitive to spin–orbit coupling [47], the present calculation should be viewed as a solver benchmark rather than a complete materials description. Bath parameters are determined by weighted least-squares fitting on the Matsubara axis (1 /ω n weighting). Figure 4 demonstrates systematic convergence of the self-energy with increasing query size, both on the Mat-subara and real-frequency axes (a, b), confirming that the accuracy of AL-ATCI remains controllable in the multi-orbital setting. In addition, the distribution of CI coeffi-cients exhibits a rapid decay across all bath sizes consid-ered (c), with the cumulative weight reaching near sat-uration with only a small fraction of configurations (d). This behavior provides direct evidence that the many-body wavefunction remains highly compressible even in the enlarged Hilbert space of the three-orbital problem, thereby justifying the efficiency of the truncated config-uration space. At the level of the Hilbert-space bottle-neck addressed here, increasing Nb and appending addi-tional noninteracting orbitals pose the same determinant-selection problem. The large-Nb multiorbital bench-mark below therefore directly probes the mechanism that makes enlarged-orbital finite-Hamiltonian calcula-tions more practical. The key point is that this convergence is achieved in a fully rotationally invariant three-orbital problem, where conventional ED is unavailable at the target bath sizes. Convergence is instead controlled internally through Nquery , while the compressed CI-weight distribu-tions across Nb = 9–18 show that the determinant-space compression underlying the weak bath-size dependence of AL-ATCI persists in a realistic multiorbital impurity problem. 

Conclusions. —We have introduced AL-ATCI, a finite-Hamiltonian impurity solver whose central organizing principle is that the relevant computational complex-ity is set by the physically important determinant man-ifold, not by the nominal bath discretization or total one-particle dimension. AL-ATCI identifies this mani-fold on the fly and controls the approximation through 

Nquery , which also provides an internal convergence knob in regimes where no external benchmark is available. The size of the selected space naturally reflects the underlying entanglement structure—broader in metallic problems and more compact in insulating ones—but in both cases the convergence is systematic and directly testable. Benchmarks on the one-dimensional Hubbard model show ED-level accuracy at greatly reduced cost and enable cellular DMFT calculations up to Nc = 10. For Sr 2RuO 4, AL-ATCI achieves systematic convergence of dynamical quantities in a rotationally invariant three-orbital problem up to Nb = 18 while maintaining a highly compressed configuration space. Over the bench-mark range studied here, AL-ATCI preserves the dis-tinctive advantages of finite-Hamiltonian solvers—direct real-frequency access and straightforward enlargement of the orbital space—while making large-bath and enlarged-orbital calculations substantially more practical. Implementation Details of AL-ATCI 

Each configuration is represented as a binary vector encoding the spin-orbital occupations of all single-particle states. The training labels are assigned based on the magnitude of the wavefunction coefficient: configurations whose absolute coefficient exceeds a threshold are labeled as important, and all remaining configurations as unimportant; in the present work, we use a threshold of 10 −6. Because important configurations constitute a small fraction of the total candidate space, the training set is severely class-imbalanced; we address this by assigning class weights inversely proportional to the number of samples in each class. When the top Nquery configurations are selected, their time-reversal partners are appended to preserve time-reversal symmetry. As a result, the total number of Slater determinants N G.S. SD in the ground-state wavefunction can exceed 

Nquery ; the ratio N G.S. SD /N query does not substantially exceed unity in practice, as shown in Table S1. The training dataset accumulates across AL-ATCI iterations within a single DMFT cycle and is reset at the beginning of each new DMFT cycle. Self-Energy Convergence with Query Size Nquery 

In the main text, Fig. 1 demonstrates that various physical quantities computed with AL-ATCI converge to exact diagonalization (ED) results as the number of active learning queries Nquery increases. Here we extend this analysis to the impurity self-energy, shown on both the real- and Matsubara-frequency axes as Σ( ω + iϵ ) and Σ( iω n). Figure S2 shows the imaginary part of the self-energy on both the real and Matsubara frequency axes for Nc = 4, Nb = 8, at U/t = 2, with columns corresponding to Nquery = 256 , 1024 , 4096 , 16384, and 65536. Details of Scalability Measurement 

Each data point in Fig. 2 of the main text corresponds to the minimum number of configurations N G.S. SD for which the resulting self-energy satisfies the causality condition Im Σ( ω) ≤ 0 for all frequencies, together with the corresponding wall-clock time. All timing measurements were performed on a single node equipped with two Intel Xeon Platinum 8360Y processors (36 cores each, 72 cores in total, 2.40 GHz base clock, 48 KiB L1d and 32 KiB L1i per core, 1.25 MiB L2 per core, 54 MiB L3 per socket), using OpenMP parallelization across all available cores. Timing measurements are restricted to the first five CI iterations to ensure a consistent comparison across system sizes. This restriction is necessary because the selected configuration space can evolve differently in later iterations as the natural-orbital basis and the training dataset both evolve, making a direct comparison between AL-ATCI and ATCI ambiguous beyond the early iterations. tG0 , as a function of Nb and Nc, respectively, confirming that diagonalization dominates the total cost in both ATCI and AL-ATCI.
