(Dated: April 21, 2026) Understanding how coherent quantum dynamics give way to correlation-dominated behavior in low-dimensional systems remains a central challenge in quantum many-body physics. Here, we ad-dress this problem by investigating the interplay of interactions and initial population imbalance in a one-dimensional Bose–Josephson junction confined in a box trap. Using the multiconfigurational time-dependent Hartree method for bosons (MCTDHB), we identify distinct dynamical regimes governed by the interplay between coherence and correlation-induced fragmentation. In the weakly interacting regime, the system exhibits coherent Josephson oscillations, while strong initial imbal-ance leads to damping. At intermediate interaction strength, fixing the interaction and varying only the initial imbalance, we uncover a crossover in the dynamics: very small imbalances yield nearly pure, non-fragmented oscillations; moderate imbalances induce many-body dephasing with collapse-and-revival behavior; and large imbalances drive equilibration accompanied by strong frag-mentation and saturation of many-body observables, including orbital entropy and participation ratio. In the strongly interacting regime, the system enters a dynamical freezing regime charac-terized by pronounced fragmentation, where the density develops well-separated, particle-resolved peaks and tunneling is strongly suppressed. These results establish a unified picture of how co-herence, dephasing, equilibration, and dynamical freezing emerge and compete in one-dimensional Josephson dynamics. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Understanding nonequilibrium dynamics in low-dimensional quantum systems remains a central chal-lenge in modern many-body physics, as reduced di-mensionality enhances quantum fluctuations and corre-lations [1, 2]. Ultracold atomic gases provide an excep-tional platform to explore these effects, offering precise control over interactions [3, 4], trapping geometries [5–7], and population imbalances. These capabilities have en-abled the experimental realization of paradigmatic sys-tems where the interplay of coherence, tunneling, and correlations can be studied in real time [8–15]. Among such systems, two weakly coupled Bose–Einstein condensates (BECs)—realizing an atomic Bose–Josephson junction (BJJ)—have emerged as a versatile platform for investigating tunneling dynamics [6, 9, 16–26] as well as many-body correla-tions [8, 27–32]. Although the theory of the Josephson junction was originally developed in the context of superconductivity [33–35], it can be directly extended to describe two weakly coupled BECs [16, 22, 24] and as well as their counterparts with fermionic superfluid

In the weakly interacting regime, Josephson oscilla-tions between the two condensates are well captured by mean-field approaches [16, 42–46], which typically pre-dict nondissipative dynamics. However, recent studies have reported the emergence of dissipation in BJJs [43, 47–49], particularly when the system is coupled to exter-nal bosonic baths [29, 30]. For further developments in both theoretical and experimental investigations of dis-sipative BJJs, see Refs [9, 21, 43, 47–52]. Moreover, in one-dimensional (1D) systems, interactions and quantum correlations give rise to phenomena such as dephasing, fragmentation, and equilibration, which lie beyond the scope of simple two-mode models [16] or conventional mean-field descriptions [43–46]. When investigating Bose–Josephson junctions, the sys-tem geometry plays a central role in determining the un-derlying dynamics. Most studies have focused on double-well potentials, where two elongated condensates are ar-ranged side by side [47, 53, 54]. Alternatively, ring ge-ometries [46, 55, 56], in which the condensates are cou-pled in a head-to-tail configuration, have also been con-sidered. The former is particularly suited for matter-wave interferometry [54, 57], whereas the latter enables the realization of atomtronic circuits [58–60]. In this context, box-shaped potentials [44, 61, 62] in-troduce qualitatively distinct features. The flat-bottom

These characteristics sig-nificantly modify collective excitations and the dynamics of coherence. Consequently, box traps [61–64] provide a natural platform to investigate the crossover from coher-ent tunneling to strongly correlated many-body behavior, including phenomena such as fragmentation and fermion-ization, which are hallmarks of low-dimensional strongly interacting systems [1, 65–68]. Motivated by these considerations, we present a sys-tematic theoretical study of a 1D BJJ in a box-shaped potential using the multiconfigurational time-dependent Hartree method for bosons (MCTDHB) [67, 69–71] im-plemented in the MCTDH-X software [72, 73], which en-ables an accurate treatment of correlations and fragmen-tation beyond mean-field approaches. By varying both the interaction strength and the initial population imbal-ance, we investigate how interactions, initial conditions, and confinement jointly influence the nonequilibrium dy-namics of strongly correlated 1D BJJ. Specifically, we focus on: (i) Characterizing the dif-ferent dynamical regimes, including coherent Josephson oscillations, many-body dephasing, equilibration, and strong dynamical fragmentation-induced freezing; (ii) Analyzing the role of initial population imbalance in driving these behaviors; and (iii) Quantifying coherence and correlations through experimentally accessible ob-servables such as population imbalance and oscillation frequency, while characterizing fragmentation via more sophisticated measures including natural orbital occupa-tions, orbital entropy, and participation ratio. To achieve this, we consider a system of N bosons in a 1D box with hard-wall boundaries, weakly coupled into two regions, and initiate dynamics via a quench of the external po-tential, while keeping the interaction strength fixed. Our study reveals distinct dynamical regimes in the 1D BJJ, controlled by interactions and initial imbalance. Weak interactions lead to coherent/damped Joseph-son oscillations, while intermediate interactions show a crossover from oscillations to many-body dephasing and equilibration as the initial imbalance increases. For very strong interactions, the system enters a dynamical freez-ing regime associated with strong fragmentation, and tunneling is strongly suppressed. The paper is organized as follows. In Sec. II, we present the theoretical framework, including the many-body Hamiltonian, the quench protocol, the MCTDHB methodology, and the observables used to characterize the system. In Sec. III, we discuss the results, divided into three subsections corresponding to the different dy-namical regimes. Finally, Sec. IV summarizes our main findings and conclusions.

> B. Method

To investigate the nonequilibrium dynamics, we em-ploy MCTDHB [67–70], as implemented in the MCTDH-X software [67, 71–75]. MCTDHB uses a time-dependent variational approach in which the many-body wavefunc-tion is expanded in a basis of M time-dependent single-particle functions (orbitals), allowing for an accurate de-scription of correlations and fragmentation beyond mean-field theory. This approach has been widely applied to both ground-state and dynamical properties of strongly correlated systems [76–82]. Both the coefficients C⃗n (t)and the orbitals ψk(x, t ) are variationally optimized at each time step, allowing the method to dynamically track correlations arising from interactions. The time-adaptive basis in MCTDHB allows the wave-function to dynamically adjust to the evolving many-body correlations, ensuring that the most relevant or-bitals are always included. The method becomes exact in the limit M → ∞ ; in practice, a finite number of orbitals is sufficient when additional orbitals remain negligibly populated and key observables converge. Convergence is checked by systematically increasing M until quanti-ties such as natural orbital occupations, and many-body entropy become insensitive to further increases. Importantly, by allowing multiple orbitals to be sig-nificantly occupied, MCTDHB naturally captures frag-mentation, in which the condensate is distributed over more than one orbital. This is essential for describing dynamics beyond mean-field theory, including dephas-ing, collapse-and-revival phenomena, equilibration, and fragmentation-induced freezing effects in strongly inter-acting or imbalanced systems. The variational optimiza-tion ensures that both the orbitals and their occupation numbers evolve self-consistently [83–86], providing an ac-curate representation of the full correlated many-body state throughout the time evolution.

> III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS

We begin by preparing the system in the ground state of a 1D box trap with an imposed potential offset that creates a finite population imbalance between the two sides of the junction. The presence of the central barrier partitions the system into two weakly coupled regions, while the offset controls the initial occupation asymme-try. Throughout this work, we consider a fixed particle number of N = 10. The interplay of interaction strength and barrier height determines the degree of coherence and the emergence of many-body correlations already in this initial state, with signatures of partial fragmentation becoming more pronounced in the strongly interacting regime. This imbalanced configuration provides a well-defined starting point for the subsequent nonequilibrium dynamics. At time t = 0, the offset potential is suddenly removed, thereby quenching the system out of equilib-rium and initiating the dynamical evolution. To systematically explore these dynamics, we monitor key observables including the population imbalance z(t), oscillation frequency, natural orbital occupations, orbital entropy, coefficient entropy, participation ratio and cor-relation function. As we vary the interaction strength and initial con-ditions, three distinct dynamical regimes emerge: (i) weakly interacting Josephson oscillations, characterized by coherent or weakly damped tunneling between the reservoirs; (ii) intermediate interactions with imbalance-driven dephasing and equilibration, where many-body correlations lead to collapse-and-revival phenomena and eventual equilibration for sufficiently large initial imbal-ances; and (iii) strongly interacting, frozen dynamics, in which the system becomes strongly fragmented and the density profile remains essentially static. In the following subsections, we analyze each of these regimes in detail, highlighting how fragmentation, and correlation effects evolve across different interaction strengths and initial population imbalances.  

> A. Weak-Interaction Regime: Coherent and Damped Josephson Oscillations

In the weakly interacting regime (Λ = 0 .01), the dy-namics are primarily governed by tunneling across the central barrier, with behavior strongly dependent on the initial population imbalance. B. Intermediate interaction regime: Josephson oscillation, many-body dephasing and relaxation 

We now turn to the intermediate interaction regime (Λ = 0 .5), where the interplay between interactions and initial population imbalance gives rise to qualitatively richer many-body dynamics. In contrast to the weakly interacting case, correlation effects play a central role in shaping the time evolution. A key observation in this regime is that, for a fixed interaction strength, the dynamical behavior is predom-inantly controlled by the initial population imbalance, while the dependence on the barrier height V1 becomes comparatively weak. Accordingly, throughout this sec-tion, the simulations are performed with V1 = 3 .0 in order to highlight the distinct dynamical features. We further analyze these three regimes in detail, high-lighting how increasing initial imbalance drives the tran-sition from coherent oscillations to dephasing and ulti-7mately to relaxation in the presence of moderate inter-actions. For weak initial imbalances , the system exhibits nearly coherent Josephson oscillations with a well-defined single frequency. In this regime, the dynamics remain largely mean-field-like despite the presence of finite interactions. The population imbalance z(t) shows regular oscillations without noticeable damping or dephasing, and the many-body state remains essentially nonfragmented through-out the evolution. This is confirmed by the natural or-bital occupations, where the first orbital remains dom-inantly occupied, while higher orbitals carry negligible population. Consistently, entropy measures such as the coefficient entropy and orbital entropy do not show any significant growth, indicating the absence of correlation buildup. Due to the lack of qualitative many-body fea-tures beyond coherent oscillations, we do not present ex-plicit figures for this regime. For intermediate initial imbalances , the system enters a regime dominated by many-body dephasing, leading to a pronounced departure from coherent Josephson dy-namics. The population imbalance z(t) (with V1 = 3 .0and imbalance offset parameter Vimb = 0 .8), shown in Fig. 5(a), exhibits oscillations whose amplitude under-goes repeated collapse-and-revival cycles over the entire time evolution. This behavior is further elucidated by the Fourier spectrum in Fig. 5(b), which displays multi-ple contributing frequency components, in contrast to the single-frequency response of the weak-imbalance regime. The coexistence of several frequencies leads to contin-uous dephasing and rephasing among the contributing many-body modes, giving rise to the observed periodic collapse–revival structure rather than a single irreversible decay. Additional insight is provided by Figs. 5(c)-(f), where we present the natural orbital occupations, coefficient entropy SC (t), orbital entropy Sn(t), and participation ratio (PR), respectively. The entropy measures and PR are plotted on a logarithmic time scale, allowing both the transient dynamics and long-time behavior to be clearly resolved. The natural occupations indicate the onset of fragmentation, with higher orbitals acquiring finite pop-ulation during the dynamics. For numerical convergence, we employ M = 6 orbitals. In contrast to a clear sep-aration of time scales between growth and saturation, both SC (t) and PR exhibit persistent fluctuations around a nearly constant mean value after an initial transient, while Sn(t) shows a comparatively smoother behavior. This indicates that the system does not relax to a sta-tionary many-body state but instead evolves within a dynamically active subspace of Hilbert space. These results demonstrate that the apparent modu-lation of oscillation amplitude in z(t) is a consequence of ongoing dephasing and rephasing processes, rather than irreversible relaxation. The system thus remains far from ergodic, with coherence continuously redistributed among multiple many-body modes. > C. Strong interaction regime: interaction-induced dynamical freezing

We now turn to the regime of very strong interac-tions (Λ = 5 .0), where the dynamics undergo a qual-itative change and enter a state of interaction-induced dynamical freezing. In this limit, the interplay between strong repulsive interactions and confinement strongly suppresses tunneling between the two reservoirs, lead-ing to a breakdown of Josephson dynamics. In contrast to the intermediate regime—where increasing imbalance promotes ergodic relaxation—the dominant effect of in-teractions here is to localize particles and inhibit collec-tive motion. In the following, we demonstrate that this frozen dy-namics is closely connected to the near-fermionization limit of the bosonic system, in which particles effectively avoid one another and arrange into well-separated den-sity peaks. This behavior results in a pronounced sup-pression of transport and a near absence of temporal fluc-tuations, signaling a fundamentally distinct nonequilib-rium regime governed by strong correlations rather than dephasing or chaotic dynamics. > IV. CONCLUSION

This work studies how the nonequilibrium dynamics of a 1D BJJ are governed by a subtle interplay of tunnel-ing, interactions, and initial population imbalance. De-pending on the interaction strength, the system transi-tions from coherent Josephson oscillations to correlation-driven dephasing and ultimately to interaction-induced dynamical freezing, revealing how many-body correla-tions reshape quantum transport and relaxation in low-dimensional bosonic systems. These regimes are directly accessible in ultracold atom experiments, providing a platform to probe fundamental aspects of nonequilibrium quantum dynamics. In detail, for weak interactions, the population imbal-ance exhibits coherent Josephson oscillations, dominated by tunneling and well captured by a mean-field descrip-tion. At intermediate interactions, many-body corre-lations become significant, resulting in interference be-tween multiple modes and many-body dephasing. This dephasing gradually suppresses oscillations, driving the imbalance toward its long-time average, accompanied by partial fragmentation and saturation of many-body ob-servables such as entropies and participation ratio. In the strongly interacting limit, large interaction energy suppresses particle transfer between reservoirs, leading to effective dynamical freezing. The density develops particle-resolved peaks with left–right symmetry, while entropies and participation ratio remain essentially con-stant, signaling a nearly nonergodic fermionized state. 11 These distinct regimes illustrate how interactions and initial conditions jointly control the transition from co-herent dynamics to dephasing and ultimately to frozen, nonergodic behavior. Importantly, these dynamical regimes can be directly explored in ultracold atomic gases confined in optical box potentials with tunable interactions. Techniques such as Feshbach resonances and optical box traps enable precise preparation of initial imbalances and real-time observa-tion of fragmentation and imbalance dynamics, making the predicted phenomena experimentally accessible. Several open questions remain. The precise boundary between the dephasing and frozen regimes, and its scaling with particle number, system size, and dimensionality, is not fully understood. It is also of interest to investigate whether similar transitions persist in larger systems, in different trap geometries, or in the presence of long-range interactions, which may qualitatively modify many-body interference and relaxation processes. Addressing these questions could provide deeper insight into nonequilib-rium equilibration and transport in strongly interacting bosonic systems.
