(Dated: April 21, 2026) Quantum gases confined in reduced dimensions have enabled the observation of many exotic quantum phenomena. While existing experiments primarily focus on homonuclear systems, we report here on the efficient preparation of a quasi-two-dimensional (2D) heteronuclear quantum degenerate mixture of ultracold 23 Na and 87 Rb. We describe the design of the vacuum system and detail the experimental procedures for preparing the 2D quantum mixture. The designed apparatus has several unique features, including compact and modular 2D-MOT sources, a science chamber that accommodates various lattice geometries, a precision in-vacuum electrode assembly, and high-resolution imaging for both atomic species. After loading the dual-species condensate into a single layer of a vertical optical lattice, we prepare a 2D gas mixture and observe quantum immiscibility in the in-situ equilibrium density profiles. The observed density profiles agree well with mean-field theories. The apparatus provides a versatile platform for investigating several interesting problems, including quantum impurities, quantum droplets, or polar molecules in low dimensions. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Ultracold atomic mixtures offer independent control and measurement over constituent species and tunable interspecies interactions, emerging as a powerful plat-form for exploring complex quantum few- and many-body physics [1–3]. Research in this field spans abroad range of fundamental questions, including tun-able quantum miscibility [4–6], the formation of quan-tum droplets [7–11], and the properties of Fermi or Bose polarons [12–15], etc. These mixtures further enable the association and production of ultracold quantum degen-erate polar molecules in recent years [16–19]. A wide array of dual-species mixture experiments - utilizing com-binations such as 85 Rb– 87 Rb [4], 6Li– 40 K [20], 6Li– 133 Cs [21], 23 Na– 40 ,39 K [22–24], 23 Na– 87 Rb [6], 23 Na– 133 Cs [25], 39 ,40 ,41 K– 87 Rb [10, 26, 27] - have demonstrated the versatility of heteronuclear systems. While these studies mainly focus on tunable interactions in three dimensions (3D), tight geometrical confinement of quantum gases into lower dimensions introduces an additional indispens-able degree of freedom that enables the observation of unique critical behaviors, most notably the Berezinskii-Kosterlitz–Thouless (BKT) phase transition [28, 29]. In this work, we extend these capabilities by realiz-ing a 23 Na – 87 Rb mixture in the quasi-2D regime. For ultracold quantum mixtures confined to low dimensions [30, 31], a variety of novel quantum phases are predicted to emerge from the interplay between enhanced quan-tum fluctuations and tunable short- or long-range inter-actions. For instance, balanced mixtures may lead to the formation of a stable quantum droplet in 2D as long as the interspecies interaction is attractive [32]. Interest-ingly, a mixture in the gas phase in 3D may transition to a liquid state upon confinement to a quasi-2D geometry [33]. In highly imbalanced mixtures - where a minority species is immersed in a low-dimensional quantum fluid of the majority species [34] - a range of exotic quantum phenomena can arise, including tunable quantum friction [35], quantum flutter in impurity motion [36], and medi-ated interactions between impurities via the background fluid[37–39]. Furthermore, if an atomic mixture is as-sembled into polar molecules and confined to a 2D ge-ometry, collisional shielding can be engineered via a DC electric or microwave field [40, 41]. Given sufficient colli-sional stability, the long-range dipolar interactions may lead to the formation of quantum crystals in 2D pancake traps [42] or supersolids in 2D optical lattices [43]. To enable microscopic investigation of these phenomena, an advanced experimental apparatus offering versatile op-tical, magnetic, and electric-field controls [16] - partic-ularly capable of single-site detection and addressing in various lattice geometries [44–46] - is highly desired. This paper details the construction of an experimental apparatus and the procedures for preparing dual-species

II. VACUUM AND LASER COOLING SYSTEM 

The vacuum system is divided into four primary sec-tions, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Two compact 2D-MOT source chambers are utilized for transverse cooling and for generating high-flux slowed atomic beams. Centered between these sources is an octagon 3D-MOT chamber, featuring CF100 re-entrant windows for the installation of high-gradient magnetic coils. To maximize effective pumping speed and maintain ultra-high vacuum (UHV), a CF100 pumping tube is directly welded onto the oc-tagon, with a getter pump mounted. This 3D-MOT chamber serves as the primary stage for capturing laser-cooled atoms, performing molasses cooling, and carrying out forced evaporation within a magnetic trap. To ensure UHV in the 3D-MOT region, the 2D source chambers are connected via differential tubes, bellows, and CF16 all-metal, non-magnetic gate valves. To overcome the optical and spatial constraints of the 33D-MOT chamber, we have integrated a dedicated sci-ence chamber with enhanced optical access. The science chamber is equipped with eight CF25 and eight tilted CF16 vacuum windows, enabling a variety of horizon-tal lattice geometries and a vertical long-lattice formed by beams intersecting at ±10 ◦ relative to the horizon-tal plane. Additionally, two CF125 re-entrant windows enable high-resolution imaging and the formation of a vertical short-lattice via retro-reflection from the bottom window. Together, these vertical lattice configurations provide different confinement strengths for studying 2D atomic or molecular physics. For future control of dipo-lar interactions between 23 Na 87 Rb polar molecules, the science chamber houses a precision in-vacuum electrode assembly, as shown in the inset of Fig. 1. This assem-bly consists of a pair of ITO-coated thin glass plates and four precision tungsten rods [16, 49], mounted on the endplate of the bottom CF125 re-entrant window. Because the lower ITO-coated glass plate is positioned within the high-resolution imaging path, its installation and alignment are critical. The direct-mounting strategy ensures the stable alignment of the electrodes relative to the vacuum window throughout the vacuum pumping and high-temperature baking. The specific procedures used to ensure diffraction-limited imaging performance will be detailed in a separate publication. A single-beam optical dipole trap (ODT) is employed for both hybrid-trap evaporation in the 3D-MOT cham-ber and the subsequent transport of the ultracold mixture into the science chamber. During normal operations, the background pressures in both the 3D-MOT and science chambers are maintained below 1 × 10 −11 mbar. In the following, we explicitly explain the design of the 2D-MOT regions.

III. 3D-MOT LOADING AND HYBRID TRAP EVAPORATION 

With the pre-cooled atomic fluxes from the 2D-MOT chambers, we describe here the procedures and param-eters for capturing atoms in a Dark-SPOT and subse-quently evaporating them in a hybrid magnetic and op-tical trap. 

A. Dark-SPOT and Sub-Doppler Cooling 

Atoms of both species, after being pushed into the 3D-MOT chamber, are captured in a Dark-SPOT [50]. The quadrupole magnetic field is generated by a pair of coils mounted inside the CF100 re-entrant flanges, with an optimal axial ( z-direction) gradient of 15 G/cm. Three pairs of counter-propagating, dual-color MOT beams were sent along three orthogonal directions. All cool-ing beams are locked to the red-detuned side of the 

|F = 2 ⟩ → | F ′ = 3 ⟩ cycling transition of the D2 line, where F denotes the hyperfine energy level and mF its magnetic sublevel. The beam diameters are 37 mm for the 589 nm cooling beam and 43 mm for the 780 nm cool-ing beam. Repumping light is injected through one of the horizontal viewports, with a beam diameter of about 28 mm for both species. To increase the atomic density, a black aluminum spot is placed to block the central area of the repumping beam, creating an 8 mm-diameter dark zone at the chamber center. This allows atoms to accu-mulate in the |F = 1 ⟩ state. Other parameters such as laser intensities and detunings are summarized in Table I. The peak optical depth (OD) of Na atoms in the dark-SPOT reaches 80, as determined by fitting the trans-mission curve of a frequency-scanned repumping probe beam. After a 3-second MOT loading phase, we can load 6.4×10 8 23 Na atoms with a temperature of 190 μK and 2.8×10 9 87 Rb atoms with 97 μK simultaneously. We empirically found that directly loading the 3D-MOT atoms into the magnetic trap was inefficient. To solve this issue, a sub-Doppler cooling stage is essential to increase the atomic phase-space density (PSD). For Rb atoms, we applied standard red-detuned molasses cool-ing by increasing the cooling detuning to 80 MHz and reducing its power to 90% in a fully compensated ambi-ent magnetic field. After 5 ms of sub-Doppler cooling, the temperature of the Rb atomic cloud dropped to 54 μK. More importantly, the number of Rb atoms captured in the magnetic trap increased by a factor of 29 compared to the case without molasses cooling. The cooling light for the gray molasses is the same one as the 3D-MOT cooling light, while the corresponding repumping light is generated via a resonant EOM. B. Magnetic Trap Loading and Evaporation 

Forced evaporative cooling was then carried out in the magnetic trap to further increase the PSD. Following the molasses stage, the magnetic field was rapidly ramped up to above 30 G/cm to levitate both species against gravity, and then slowly ramped to 160 G/cm over 60 ms. This further compresses the atomic ensemble, resulting in tem-peratures of ∼ 200 μK and PSDs on the order of 10 −6 for both species. For RF-forced evaporation, we installed a square macro-ceramic (26×40 mm) inside the 3D-MOT chamber and wound six turns of Kapton-insulated cop-per wire. The assembly was positioned 47 mm from the magnetic trap center. An external RF signal, at an input power of 33 dBm, drove the atomic spin-flip transitions. Both Na and Rb atoms are held in the magnetic trap in the |F = 1 , m F = −1⟩ state, and their Zeeman split-tings are nearly identical under low field. Consequently, a single frequency-swept RF signal simultaneously evap-orates atoms of both species, which is another feature of this work compared to earlier experiments with Na-Rb mixture [46, 52]. > IV. PREPARATION OF QUASI-2D QUANTUM DEGENERATE MIXTURE

With the near-degenerate dual-species mixture pre-pared as described in the previous section, the atoms were transferred from the 3D-MOT chamber to the sci-ence chamber via a transport ODT. Subsequently, the mixture was evaporatively cooled to quantum degener-acy in a pure crossed optical trap and then loaded into a single plane of a long vertical optical lattice, producing a quasi-2D quantum degenerate mixture. This section de-tails the experimental design and procedures employed.  

> A. Optical Transport

To transport the atoms in the near-degenerate gas, we simplified the hybrid-trap evaporation procedures de-scribed earlier. First, the single-beam optical trap was converted into a small-angle crossed optical trap [54] to enhance its axial confinement. Specifically, a polarizing beam splitter was used to divide the collimated transport beam into two parallel beams with perpendicular polar-izations, as shown in Fig. 6, separated by 14 mm. These beams passed through an f = 500 mm lens in parallel, converging at an angle of 1.6 ◦ at the focal point, with each focused beam having a waist of 55 μm. Two mirrors mounted on an air-bearing translation stage fold the op-tical path; displacing the stage by a given distance shifts the trap focus by twice that amount. A 1:1 telescope composed of two f = 400 mm lenses then relayed the focused crossed beams into the vacuum chamber. This arrangement placed the translation stage far away from the atoms, avoiding magnetic interference from the stage motors. By moving the stage, the intersection point of the optical trap could be shifted 375 mm from the cen-ter of the 3D-MOT chamber to the center of the science chamber. Second, we simplified the RF evaporation stage and directly loaded the mixture into the hybrid trap at full magnetic gradient. Within 100 ms, the total trap power was ramped up to 13.2 W while the magnetic trap was simultaneously switched off. Each transport beam has a depth of 56 μK for Na and 184 μK for Rb. In this trans-port trap, the measured trap frequencies were ωAxial(x ′)

= 2 π× 18.6 (18.1) Hz and ωRadial = 2 π× 1169 (1121) Hz for Na (Rb). A small bias magnetic field was applied dur-ing transport to maintain the quantization axis, because interference between the two transport beam paths at the focus effectively produces a random elliptical polarization that induces vector light shifts for both species. We ob-served a vector field for Rb corresponding to a magnetic field of 75 mG, pointing along the trap beam’s propaga-tion direction, using microwave spectroscopy. The trans-fer trajectory must be adiabatically smooth to avoid heat-ing. We therefore employed an S-curve motion profile – a segmented constant-jerk motion comprising acceler-ation followed by deceleration [54]. After optimization, the maximum jerk was set to 5000 mm/s 3, and the entire transfer process took about 1.1 s. With optimized RF evaporation and transport param-eters, we routinely transfer up to 1.1×10 7 Na atoms and 1.0×10 6 Rb atoms to the science chamber. The over-all transport efficiency exceeded 85% for Na (whose trap depth is about 0.31 of that for Rb) and nearly 100% for Rb, and the temperature of both atomic species remains 12.8 μK throughout the transfer. In addition, the trap lifetime for Na atoms held in the stationary transport trap exceeds 36 s.

> V. CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK

In conclusion, we have developed a new experimental apparatus capable of efficiently producing dual-species quantum degenerate mixtures confined in a quasi-2D ge-ometry. We have detailed the design considerations for the vacuum system, along with the laser cooling and trapping procedures that collectively reduce atomic tem-peratures from room temperature down to the hundred-nanokelvin regime. The Rb 2D-MOT cooling stage fea-tures a compact, modular, and stable design. Further-more, the science chamber is engineered to accommodate various optical dipole traps and lattice geometries, mag-netic fields for tuning interspecies Feshbach resonance be-tween atoms, and the precise installation of an in-vacuum electrode assembly to control ultracold NaRb molecules. Finally, high-resolution optical imaging enables the reso-lution of the in-situ density distributions of the 2D quan-tum mixture with sub-micron spatial resolution. The 2D quantum mixture provides an ideal start-ing point for exploring a variety of intriguing ultracold low-dimensional phenomena. For instance, in a repul-sively interacting and balanced mixture, one could tune the interspecies interaction strength or further reduce the mixture temperature to map the miscible-immiscible phase boundary [60, 67] or investigate mixed bubbles [5], potentially providing a route toward probing Andreev-Bashkin effects. Conversely, attractively interacting and balanced mixtures allow for the study of unique Bose droplet properties in low dimensions [32, 68, 69] and their finite temperature behavior [33]. For imbalanced 2D mixtures, our high-resolution imaging setup is well-suited to study Bose polarons in reduced dimensions and probe the induced quantum correlations between impu-rities and the baths [70–72]. Furthermore, the ultra-cold atomic mixture can be associated into 23 Na 87 Rb po-lar molecules [73]. With the in-vacuum electrode, these trapped 2D bosonic polar molecules can be polarized to study collision properties under shielding potentials engi-neered via uniform DC electric field [41, 42, 74], a regime where only the fermionic counterpart has recently been observed [16].
