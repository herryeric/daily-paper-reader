# Abstract 

We present an exact solution in the large-N limit of the L¬ī evy Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev (LSYK) model introduced in Ref. [1], wherein the couplings are drawn from a L¬ī evy Stable distribution parameterized by a tail exponent őľ ‚ąą [0 , 2] .Starting from the Hamiltonian and its associated partition function, we high-light the key differences from the standard Gaussian SYK model and derive the large-N Schwinger-Dyson equations via a bosonic oscillator representation of the action. These equations are solved both numerically and analytically in the large-q and infrared limits. We subsequently analyze the chaotic proper-ties of the model by computing the Krylov exponent from the large-q Green‚Äôs function and extracting the Lyapunov exponent from the 4-point function. The parameter őľ continuously interpolates between a free theory at őľ = 0 

and the conventional, maximally chaotic Gaussian SYK model at őľ = 2 , with non-maximal chaos persisting throughout the intermediate regime 0 < őľ < 2.Thermodynamic quantities, including the entropy, free energy, average energy, and specific heat capacity, are computed and compared with their Gaussian SYK counterparts. The interpretations of the thermodynamics are discussed with respect to the holographic dual and non-Fermi liquid theory. Finally, we discuss an alternative representation of the LSYK model based on a distinct decomposition of the L¬ī evy Stable distribution, which establishes a non-trivial connection to Gaussian SYK, and provide supporting analytical and numerical results in the appendices.

# 1 Introduction 

The study of quantum chaos forms a central research direction in quantum mechanics, quantum field theory and quantum gravity. In quantum mechanics, the central dogma of quantum chaos is the Bohigas-Giannoi-Schmidt (BGS) conjecture [2], connecting quan-tum chaos to random matrix theory [3, 4]. The basic idea of this conjecture is that the spectral correlations in quantum systems that are ‚Äúchaotic‚ÄĚ are faithfully represented by the spectral correlations of random matrices. This has been successfully tested in a large number of systems, most notably in billiard systems demonstrating semi-classical chaos [5]. Treating the BGS conjecture as a de-facto definition of quantum chaos, it has been explored extensively in quantum many-body systems . Several distinct approaches (apart from random matrix universality) are used to study many-body chaos, such as eigenstate thermalization [6, 7], free probability [8], entanglement [9], out-of-time-ordered correlators [10] and Krylov complexity [11], among many others. A quantum mechanical many-body model that has proven to be particularly useful 2SciPost Physics Submission 

in the study of many-body chaos is the Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev (SYK) model [12, 13]. This is a model described by a quenched, disordered Hamiltonian with all-to-all interacting fermions. The most extensively studied instance of the model consists of Gaussian disorder and Majorana fermions [14‚Äď17]. The unique feature of this model is that it is solvable in the 

N ‚Üí ‚ąě limit, where N is the number of Majorana fermions. This is part of the reason why the model is of significant interest also from the perspective of holography [18‚Äď20]. This model has been discussed in several comprehensive reviews [21‚Äď24]. From the perspective of many-body quantum chaos, the model and its‚Äô modifications have been extensively studied [25‚Äď28]. In practice, every random variable Ji1i2¬∑¬∑¬∑ iq is kept with probability p and set to zero with probability 1 ‚ąí p, thus reducing the connectivity of the model. The introduction of the sparsity parameter effectively eliminates a finite fraction of interactions between the Majorana fermions, and this is found to induce non-chaotic behaviour. This comes at the cost of loosing the inherent solvability of the model in the large-N limit. An open problem was to find a model that had all the salient solvability features of the SYK model, and still featured a crossover from chaotic to integrable quantum many-body behaviour in a controlled manner by effectively emulating sparse SYK. Such model was proposed in Ref. [1]. To briefly recap, the main modification is the replacement of the Gaussian disorder Ji1i2¬∑¬∑¬∑ iq by a L¬ī evy-Stable distribution. L¬ī evy distri-butions are known to posses fat tails (usually controlled by a parameter őľ ‚ąą (0 , 2], where 

őľ = 2 is Gaussian) and therefore display large deviations in their sample sets. Treating the random realisations of the interaction in a hierarchical manner [29], it is possible to construct a scaling theory for eigenvalue correlations [1,30] which demonstrates that there is an N ‚ąí dependence on values of őľ that cause deviation from chaotic behaviour. This is exactly the scenario also obtained in sparse SYK with respect to the sparsity param-eter. Normalising the Hamiltonian by the largest of Ji1i2¬∑¬∑¬∑ iq in a given realisation, an approximate sparse network is obtained since the largest values are parameterically larger 

than the typical values. However, the main difference from the sparse SYK is that all-to-all models with L¬ī evy Stable disorder are solvable , as evidences (for example) in L¬ī evy Spin glass problems [31‚Äď33] suggest. In this article, we solve the L¬ī evy Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev model [1] in the large ‚ąíN limit, for any value of the parameter őľ. A brief summary of our findings is given below.

Comments on the choice of saddles 

So far in the analysis, we have not discussed the features of the saddle manifold of the bosonic oscillator action. A detailed analysis is beyond the scope of this work. However, we can make some comments here. The motivation of choosing saddles that are invariant in the index I is two-fold. First, invariance in the index I respects the statistical independence of the (L)SYK Hamiltonian (on average) with respect the indexing rule the Majorana fermions that we choose. # 7 Conclusions 

We solve for the equilibrium dynamics of the Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev model with L¬ī evy Stable disorder in limit of large number of fermions (large ‚ąíN limit), providing a framework for analytically understanding effectively sparse SYK models. The model is controlled by a parameter őľ ‚ąą [0 , 2] where őľ = 0 corresponds to a free theory (no interactions) and őľ = 2 corresponds to fully connected Gaussian SYK model. We develop a new method to extract effective dynamical correlators from formally divergent functions that are characteristic of L¬ī evy Stable distributions. We derive the Schwinger-Dyson equations for this model and solve them (a) numerically and (b) in the limit of large ‚ąíq and (c) in the limit of strong coupling. We elucidate the phase diagram of the model. The model is found to be maximally chaotic in the őľ = 2 point, free in the őľ = 0 point and non-maximally chaotic for 0 < őľ < 2. The chaoticity of the model in the intermediate parameter regime is quantified, by computing the chaos exponents for 2 ‚ąí and 4 ‚ąí point functions. Using the solution of the SD equations, we compute the equilibrium thermodynamic observables (entropy, average energy, free energy) for the model both analytically and numerically. We highlight the similarities and difference with Gaussian SYK, and comment on possible holographc dual. The model studied in this manuscript represents a non-trivial modification of the Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev model that gives rise to a chaotic ‚Üíintegrable crossover in the dy-namics of the model as well as temperature-modified thermodynamics. It serves, in an appropriate sense [1], as a solvable version of the sparse Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev model [27]. We emphasise that the model is purely integrable only at the point őľ = 0, and therefore does not have a transition . A natural future direction is to develop such a model which 30 SciPost Physics Submission 

remains solvable and demonstrates a chaotic transition. It also remains to be established whether the crossover in this model holds to higher orders in 1 /N . The thermodynamics observed in this model warrants further careful analysis, along the lines of holographic duality or non-Fermi liquid behaviour. These can be explored further by computing other holographic quantities such as entanglement, and comparing with bulk RT-surface cal-culation. Finally, several modifications of this model (coupled, dissipative, double-scaled etc.) promise interesting physics and warrant consideration.
