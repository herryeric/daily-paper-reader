Symmetry and entanglement are two fundamental concepts in quantum many-body physics. Their inter-play is captured by symmetry-resolved entanglement, which decomposes the total entanglement into contri-butions from different symmetry sectors. Computing symmetry-resolved entanglement in strongly interacting higher-dimensional quantum systems remains challenging. Here, we introduce a quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) approach for computing symmetry-resolved R´ enyi entropies (SRRE) in large-scale interacting systems by mea-suring disorder (symmetry-twisted) operators on replica manifolds and reconstructing SRRE from the corre-sponding charged moments. We apply this method to the transverse-field Ising model (TFIM) in one and two dimensions. In one dimension, we recover the conformal-field-theory prediction for the logarithmic scaling of the disorder operator and observe the expected approach to entanglement equipartition. In two dimensions, our data provide numerical evidence consistent with entanglement equipartition at the (2+1)D Ising critical point. We further apply the framework to the 1D Heisenberg chain and obtain results consistent with the expected asymptotic scaling and finite-size corrections in the U (1) -resolved sectors. Our work establishes a practical nu-merical route to symmetry-resolved entanglement in interacting lattice models and provides a useful framework for future studies beyond one dimension. 

Introduction. — Entanglement entropy (EE) is a powerful probe of quantum many-body states, offering insights that transcend the paradigm of conventional order parameters [1– 3]. Its scaling with subsystem size offers an effective or-ganizing principle, uncovering universal features of distinct quantum phases. While EE typically obeys an area law in ground states of local gapped Hamiltonians [4], deviations from this behavior often signal more exotic forms of quan-tum matter. For example, topological entanglement entropy characterizes topologically ordered phases [5, 6], while log-arithmic corrections carrying universal information can arise in (1+1)D conformal field theories (CFTs), in systems with Nambu-Goldstone modes, and for entangling boundaries with corners [7–11]. As a result, entanglement scaling has be-come a valuable framework not only for classifying equilib-rium phases, but also for diagnosing nonequilibrium phenom-ena such as measurement-induced phase transitions, which emerge from the interplay between unitary evolution and local measurements [12–14]. In recent years, this framework has been extended by in-corporating the role of global symmetries, yielding the notion of symmetry-resolved entanglement [15–23], which resolves the total entanglement entropy into contributions from dif-ferent symmetry sectors, providing a more refined probe into the entanglement structure of a quantum state. Current re-search frontiers are rapidly expanding its scope to encompass systems with non-Abelian symmetries [24], entanglement dy-namics in non-equilibrium settings [25], and the resolution of multipartite entanglement [26]. Furthermore, experimen-tal proposals for measuring symmetry-resolved entanglement have begun to bridge the gap between theory and experiment, establishing it as a promising and experimentally accessible probe for quantum simulators [27]. However, while the understanding of symmetry-resolved entanglement in one-dimensional systems is now well-established, extending these insights to higher-dimensional in-teracting systems still presents a formidable challenge. Com-puting symmetry-resolved entanglement in interacting two-dimensional quantum systems remains particularly challeng-ing. Analytical approaches such as CFTs are most power-ful in (1+1)D, while numerical workhorses like exact diag-onalization are severely limited by system size. Tensor net-work methods for two-dimensional systems are often trun-cated at small bond dimensions, which limits the precision with which entanglement structures can be resolved [28–30]. Consequently, fundamental questions—such as the explicit scaling forms of symmetry-resolved entanglement with sub-system sizes and whether the entanglement equipartition per-sists at (2+1)D quantum critical points—remain largely un-explored and lack direct numerical verifications. In this article, we introduce an unbiased quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) algorithm for computing symmetry-resolved R´ enyi entropies in interacting systems beyond one dimen-sion. Building on the charged-moments framework [16], we show that, in an n-replica geometry, the charged moment is related to the expectation value of a subsystem disorder op-erator, which is therefore naturally accessible to QMC sam-pling. The symmetry-resolved contribution hidden in the total R´ enyi entropy can then be obtained by Fourier transforming the charged moments, which are conjugate to the moments projected onto the corresponding symmetry sectors. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. We first introduce the concept of symmetry-resolved entangle-ment and the equipartition of entanglement entropy. We then present our general QMC framework for computing symmetry-resolved R´ enyi entropies. Next, we briefly describe the models studied in this work, namely the 1D and 2D quan-tum Ising models and the 1D Heisenberg chain. Finally, we present the results for these models and discuss limitations and future directions. In Appendix A, we benchmark our QMC-based method against exact diagonalization.

Discussions. — In summary, we have developed a scalable and unbiased quantum Monte Carlo approach for comput-ing symmetry-resolved R´ enyi entropies in interacting quan-tum many-body systems. By relating charged moments to ex-pectation values of subsystem disorder operators on replica manifolds, the method provides a practical route for com-puting symmetry-resolved entanglement in settings that are difficult to access with existing analytical and numerical ap-proaches. More broadly, it establishes a direct connection be-tween field-theoretic formulations of symmetry-resolved en-tanglement and large-scale lattice simulations. Our results provide representative benchmarks of the method in several settings. For the 1D TFIM, we recover the CFT-predicted logarithmic scaling of the disorder oper-ator. For the 1D Heisenberg chain, our data are consistent with the characteristic double-logarithmic subleading contri-bution and the O(1 / ln L) finite-size corrections associated with U (1) symmetry. In the 2D TFIM at criticality, the data are well described by an area law supplemented by a sublead-ing correction consistent with a logarithmic form within the accessible system sizes. For all models studied here, the sub-tracted SRREs in different symmetry sectors converge toward a common asymptotic value, providing numerical evidence consistent with entanglement equipartition. The present framework can be extended in several con-crete directions. A natural next step is to generalize the method from Abelian symmetries to non-Abelian ones, where symmetry resolution is organized by irreducible representa-tions rather than a single conserved charge. Such an ex-tension would make it possible to study how entanglement is distributed among different representation sectors in in-teracting lattice models. Another important direction is to apply the method to additional (2 + 1) D critical systems in order to clarify the structure of subleading corrections to symmetry-resolved entanglement and to test the robustness of entanglement equipartition beyond the Ising universality class. It would also be interesting to use this approach in sign-problem-free models with more exotic ground states, such as topological phases or deconfined quantum critical points, where symmetry-resolved entanglement may provide infor-mation that is not accessible from conventional entropy mea-sures alone. More broadly, the method provides a practical numerical tool for studying the interplay between symmetry
