# Dissipative Floquet engineering of gapped many-body phases using thermal baths 

Lorenz Wanckel 1, ∗ and Andr´ e Eckardt 1, †

> 1

(Dated: April 3, 2026) Floquet engineering, the control of a quantum system by means of time-periodic driving, allows to modify the properties of the system so that it becomes described by an approximate effective time-independent Hamiltonian. However, in the presence of interactions the stabilization of inter-esting many-body ground states of such effective Hamiltonians is possible only on a certain time scale, beyond which Floquet heating sets in, as it results from unwanted driving induced resonant excitation. Moreover, already the preparation of such states is challenged by excitations due to im-perfect adiabatic dynamics, especially when a phase transition has to be passed. Here, we propose a general dissipative strategy for the preparation and stabilization of effective ground states that are protected by an energy gap. Our approach relies on coupling the driven system to a thermal bath, the properties of which are chosen so that it both suppresses Floquet heating and guides the system into a non-equilibrium steady state with a large occupation of the effective ground-state, but generally non-thermal occupations of excited states of the effective Hamiltonian. We use the Floquet-Born-Markov master equation to verify the proposed strategy for the example of a strongly driven Bose-Hubbard chain with an effective gapped Mott-insulator ground state. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

When applying a rapid time-periodic drive to a many-body quantum system [1–6], we can, roughly speaking, expect two effects. On the one hand, the drive modifies the system on time average. Apart from rapid oscilla-tory dynamics at the driving frequency (known as mi-cromotion), this effect can be captured by an effective time-independent Hamiltonian ˆHeff . For instance, aver-aging over the phases induced by applied modulated (and static) on-site potentials in a lattice system over one driv-ing period leads to an effective modification of tunneling matrix elements. Here an interesting application is to realize effective Hamiltonians with broken time-reversal symmetry for charge-neutral particles (such as atoms or photons in quantum simulators), as it can be achieved simply by driving the system in a fashion that differs when viewed forward or backward in time. Controlling quantum systems by means of this effect is known as Floquet engineering. Beyond simple time averaging, it can be described using systematic high-frequency expan-sions [1–3, 7]. The second effect is that the drive induces resonant transitions. For angular driving frequencies Ω that are large compared to the typical single and two-particle en-ergy scales of the Hamiltonian, such an absorption or emission of a driving quantum ℏΩ requires a substantial change of the system’s wave function (corresponding to a high-order process in perturbation theory), so that the corresponding time scale can become large. Nevertheless, in generic many-body systems these resonant processes make themselves felt eventually; this is known as Flo-quet heating [2–5, 8–13]. Even though the associated

heating time increases exponentially (with the order of perturbation theory) when the driving frequency is in-creased [14], it cannot become arbitrarily large. Namely, the frequency also needs to remain small compared to the large energy associated with leaving/destroying the system, such as the energy of high-lying orbital states ne-glected in a low-energy tight-binding description of a lat-tice system [15, 16]. This competition of heating effects for too low and too high frequencies, respectively, leads to an optimal window of driving frequencies [17]. Besides choosing favorable frequencies, Floquet heating can also be attenuated by exploiting the coherent suppression of dominant heating channels [18], destructive interference from two-tone driving [19], or the suppression of multi-photon transitions via spectral filtering [20]. However, in the thermodynamic limit generic isolated Floquet sys-tems are expected to approach an infinite-temperature state in the long-time limit, as a consequence of eigen-state thermalization without energy conservation [21, 22]. In recent years, such high-frequency Floquet engineer-ing has been employed successfully in different experi-mental platforms, such as ultracold atoms in optical lat-tices or photons in waveguides and superconducting cir-cuits. It was used, among others for, the control of the bosonic Mott transition [23, 24], studying frustrated tun-nel kinetics in a triangular lattice [25, 26], the realization of artificial magnetic fields and topological band struc-tures [27–32], the control of localization [20, 33], the realization of the anyon Hubbard model for two parti-cles [34, 35], as well as the preparation of “baby” frac-tional Chern insulators of up to three particles [36–38]. Recently, also the interactions between ultracold atoms were modified using the Floquet control of Feshbach res-onances [39]. However, the stabilization of correlated states of matter of more than just a few particles over a longer time remains elusive. Apart from Floquet heat-ing, here a second problem is the failure of adiabatic state preparation, not only when passing the transition to a

2new Floquet engineered phase, but also when passing resonances on the way [18]. A possible solution for the preparation and stabiliza-tion of interesting Floquet engineered states of matter is the controlled use of dissipation. For cooling a non-driven system close to its ground state, it can be coupled to a cold thermal bath or a suitable driven-dissipative environment (like in the case of laser cooling). In both cases, it is much easier for the environment to absorb energy from the system rather than to emit it to it (un-less the system has already reached a low-energy state). These strategies can, however, not simply be adapted in a straightforward fashion to Floquet engineered sys-tems, since here Floquet heating occurs also on the level of system and environment together. Namely, via the absorption of driving quanta ℏΩ, “easy” bath-induced transitions become possible that increase the energy not only in the bath, but at the same time also in the system. As a consequence of these processes, open driven systems genenerically approach steady states that are neither (in some approximate sense) thermal nor low-energy (see, e.g., Refs. [40–44]). For small Floquet systems, with an upper energy bound well below ℏΩ, these unwanted tran-sitions can, however, be suppressed by choosing also an environment with a narrow spectral width, so that even together system and bath cannot absorb a driving quan-tum ℏΩ. This strategy also works for non-interacting particles, as long as their single-particle energies, which are relevant in this case, are bounded, and it has been proposed for the stabilization of approximately thermal Floquet engineered topological insulators of free fermions [45–48]. For large, interacting systems, the above strategy for preparing approximate thermal states should fail. Yet, it has been shown in Ref. [49] for the example of a spin system that also in larger systems the resonant absorp-tion of energy can still be suppressed by a thermal bath, leading to an approximate thermal state of the effective Hamiltonian ˆHeff , as long as the system-bath coupling remains small compared to the energy level splitting of ˆHeff (see also Ref. [50]). In this approach the coupling to the environment suppresses resonant coupling within the system [51], while further conditions ensure that also bath-induced Floquet heating becomes negligible. How-ever, as we explain below, this strategy becomes difficult for the relevant case of gapped effective ground states in the regime where the bath temperature β−1 becomes small compared to the energy gap Egap .In this paper, we present a general and simple strat-egy for approximately preparing and stabilizing gapped ground states of effective Hamiltonians of interacting many-body Floquet systems via the coupling to a ther-mal bath. This includes the relevant case of topologically ordered states of matter, such as fractional Chern insu-lator phases in systems with both strong Floquet engi-neered artificial magnetic fields and strong interactions. For that purpose, we require excited states of Heff to be occupied weakly, but not to approximately follow a quasi-thermal state ∼ exp( −βH eff ). This turns out to be possible under realistic conditions in a steady state that is stabilized by a steady energy flow from the driven system into the bath. Notably, our approach does not require the system-bath coupling to be small compared to the energy level splitting of ˆHeff , making it scalable to large system sizes. We discuss and verify it for the simple example of a driven Bose-Hubbard chain at unit filling, the effective Hamiltonian of which possesses a gapped Mott-insulator ground state when interactions are strong or the tunneling is suppressed coherently by driving [23].

> V. DISSIPATIVE SUPPRESSION OF FLOQUET HEATING

When two states of different “photon” number m are resonantly coupled and hybridize at an avoided crossing of the quasienergy spectrum (see inset of Fig. 1 and dis-cussion above), we can distinguish two different regimes with respect to the coupling to the bath [51]: In the limit of ultra-weak system–bath coupling, defined as small compared to the level splitting of the avoided crossing, the density matrix will approach diagonal form with re-spect to the hybridized “adiabatic” basis given by the (actual) Floquet states (black dashed lines in the inset of Fig. 1). In this limit the bath-induced dynamics is slow compared to Floquet heating. In the opposite regime, where the system-bath coupling is strong compared to the level splitting of the avoided crossing, it approaches diagonal form with respect to the “diabatic” basis (col-ored lines), given by states of definite “photon” number 

m corresponding to the eigenstates of ˆHeff . This latter regime corresponds to a situation, where Floquet heating is suppressed by the coupling to the bath. In Ref. [49], it was shown for the example of a spin chain that this ef-fect can actually be used for stabilizing an effective Gibbs state with respect to ˆHeff at a temperature close to that of the environment (see also Ref. [50] for a more general 5discussion). Here, in turn we discuss a general strategy for achieving a large occupation of a gapped ground state of Heff . It works also in regimes, where an approximate thermal occupation is difficult to achieve, as will be de-tailed below.

> IX. CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK

We have proposed a general strategy for the dissipa-tive preparation of gapped ground states of Floquet en-gineered approximate effective Hamiltonians of interact-ing Floquet systems, as they result from low-order high-frequency expansions. This is achieved by the coupling to a low-temperature bath of narrow spectral width and intermediate system-bath coupling strength, which cor-responds to a realistic scenario for experimental realiza-tions. Such an environment suppresses coherent reso-nant excitations out of the effective ground state (Flo-quet heating) and, at the same time, induces incoherent transitions that strongly favor the population of the effec-tive ground state. We test this strategy for the example of a Bose-Hubbard chain. For that purpose, we compute the steady state of the full Floquet-Born-Markov master equation and compare it to simplified rate equations. Our strategy can also be used for the preparation of other, more interesting gapped ground states of ef-fective Floquet engineered Hamlitonians, such as corre-lated topologically ordered fractional Chern insulators. It should, moreover, not rely on small system sizes and, thus, work also for large systems. This provides an advantage compared to adiabiatic preparation schemes, which not only suffer from Floquet heating, but also from excitations that are created when passing a quan-tum phase transition in the ground state of the effective Hamiltonian (as well as when passing Floquet resonances [18]). However, the numerical simulation of such larger systems using the full Born-Markov master equation (6), as it is required to describe the suppression of Floquet heating, is extremely challenging. Already deriving this equation requires computing the full quasienergy spec-trum and, since it is not of Lindblad form, it cannot simply be solved by standard quantum trajectory ap-proaches. This challenges already the classical simulation of minimal realizations of few-particle fractional Chern insulators. However, our approach can readily be tested and used experimentally in quantum simulators. For ex-ample in a system of ultracold atoms, where a thermal bath of narrow spectral width can be realized by a sec-ond atomic species in a narrow band of an optical lattice potential. 8
